## Summary
In early 2026, escalating tensions between the United States and Iran broke into open military conflict, with the US launching strikes against Iranian military and nuclear infrastructure. The conflict has triggered a severe global energy crisis: oil flow through the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly one-fifth of global oil normally passes — fell to approximately 30% of its previous levels in Q1 2026, sending oil prices surging and disrupting supply chains worldwide. Diplomatic efforts including Pakistani mediation are ongoing as of May 2026.

## Key Points
- The US has launched military strikes on Iran; Iran has responded with regional proxy escalation and threats to close the Strait of Hormuz
- Oil flow through the Strait of Hormuz fell to ~30% of previous quarter levels in Q1 2026 (US EIA data)
- Global protests erupted on May Day 2026 calling for peace, against the war, and against the resulting energy and fuel price crisis
- Pakistan's deputy PM Ishaq Dar met with the US chargé d'affaires to facilitate ceasefire talks — indirect diplomacy channel active
- The conflict has exacerbated an already-stressed global energy market, with cascading effects on inflation and cost-of-living globally
- Russian strikes on Kyiv (using Oreshnik hypersonic missiles) continue simultaneously, compounding geopolitical instability

## Details

### Background: US-Iran Tensions

US-Iran hostility has deep roots:
- **1979 Islamic Revolution** ousted the US-backed Shah; the hostage crisis cemented enmity
- **Nuclear program**: Iran's uranium enrichment program has been the central flashpoint for decades. The 2015 JCPOA (nuclear deal) temporarily capped enrichment; the US withdrew in 2018 (Trump). Subsequent negotiations failed to produce a lasting agreement
- **Regional proxies**: Iran funds and arms Hezbollah (Lebanon), Hamas (Gaza), Houthi rebels (Yemen), and Shia militias in Iraq — the so-called "Axis of Resistance"
- **2020 escalation**: US killed IRGC General Qasem Soleimani; Iran retaliated with ballistic missile strikes on US bases in Iraq
- **2024**: Iran and Israel engaged in direct missile exchanges (April and October 2024), dramatically escalating regional tensions
- **2025**: With nuclear negotiations fully collapsed and Iran having enriched uranium to near-weapons grade, the US and Israel increased pressure for military action

### The 2026 Conflict

As of May 2026, the following is known:
- **US military strikes**: The US has conducted strikes against Iranian military and nuclear sites; targets have reportedly included enrichment facilities at Natanz and Fordow, IRGC command infrastructure, and naval assets
- **Iranian responses**: Iran has threatened and in some instances attempted to block the Strait of Hormuz; proxy forces (Houthis in Yemen, Shia militias in Iraq) have escalated attacks on US and allied interests
- **Ceasefire negotiations**: Talks are ongoing through indirect channels including Pakistan. The US chargé d'affaires (rather than the full ambassador, suggesting elevated but not normalised diplomatic contact) has been engaged by Pakistani mediation

### The Strait of Hormuz and Global Energy Impact

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow chokepoint between Iran and Oman — at its narrowest, only 21 miles wide. Through it passes:
- ~17–21 million barrels of oil per day (pre-crisis) — roughly 20% of global supply
- Significant volumes of LNG from Qatar and the UAE
- A large fraction of Gulf state hydrocarbon exports

The 30% fall in Hormuz flow in Q1 2026 represents an unprecedented disruption. Comparable historical events:
- **1973 Arab oil embargo**: ~7% of global supply removed; oil prices quadrupled
- **1979 Iranian Revolution**: ~5–6% reduction; oil prices doubled
- **1990 Gulf War**: temporary spike on invasion

A sustained 70% reduction in Hormuz flow would remove ~14 million barrels/day from global supply — roughly 14% of global consumption — a shock with no historical precedent in the modern era.

**Market effects:**
- Brent crude prices have spiked significantly (specific prices to be updated as situation evolves)
- European natural gas prices have risen sharply as LNG diversions and risk premiums mount
- Airlines and shipping companies face fuel surcharges
- Inflationary pressures from energy costs are rippling into goods prices globally
- Nations with large strategic petroleum reserves (US SPR, IEA members) have released reserves to buffer the shock

**Alternative routes:**
- Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline (Petroline) can carry ~5 million bpd — a partial bypass
- UAE has an Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline bypassing Hormuz — ~1.5 million bpd capacity
- Neither comes close to substituting for full Hormuz flow

### Global Protests — May Day 2026

On May 1, 2026, coordinated global protests took place in dozens of countries. Demonstrators called for:
- An immediate ceasefire in the US-Iran conflict
- Higher wages in response to the energy-driven cost-of-living crisis
- Better working conditions (traditional May Day demands)
- An end to what protesters described as US military overreach in the Middle East

The protests reflected widespread public anxiety about the intersection of war and economic hardship — energy prices have become the most visible consumer impact of the conflict.

### Regional Spillovers

The US-Iran conflict is interacting with ongoing regional tensions:
- **Yemen**: Houthi rebels (Iranian-backed) have intensified attacks on Red Sea shipping and Gulf infrastructure
- **Iraq**: Shia militia groups have attacked US bases; Iraqi government is caught between US military presence and Iranian influence
- **Lebanon**: Hezbollah (Iranian-backed) has been a wildcard; the risk of renewed Lebanon conflict is elevated (see separate geopolitics analysis)
- **Syria**: Iranian-backed forces remain present; Israeli strikes on Iranian-linked targets in Syria have continued

### Diplomatic Landscape

**Pakistan as mediator**: Pakistan has historically maintained communications with both Washington and Tehran, and Islamabad has strong incentives to stabilise the region — it shares a long border with Iran, depends on imported energy, and has its own complex relationship with both the US and Iran.

**Other actors:**
- **China**: has significant oil purchase agreements with Iran; is calling for restraint but benefits from weakened US standing in the Middle East; also serves as a potential back-channel
- **Russia**: strategically benefits from high energy prices and US military distraction; unlikely to play a constructive mediating role
- **European Union**: pushing for negotiations; EU companies face severe economic impacts from energy price spikes
- **Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, UAE)**: cautiously supportive of weakening Iranian power but acutely vulnerable to Hormuz disruption and regional escalation

### Why This Matters

1. **Energy security**: The event has demonstrated how a single chokepoint can destabilise global energy markets; accelerating energy transition and supply diversification debates
2. **Nuclear proliferation**: If Iran's nuclear program is successfully degraded, a temporary setback to proliferation; if not, potential acceleration of other states' nuclear ambitions
3. **US credibility**: US military engagement tests alliance commitments and global perceptions of US power projection
4. **Global inflation**: Energy prices feed into transport, manufacturing, and food production; the war is amplifying inflationary pressures already present from structural factors
5. **Regional order**: The conflict is reshaping Middle East alignments — which actors can hedge, which must choose sides

### Current Status (May 27, 2026)

- Military operations ongoing; no ceasefire in place as of this note's writing
- Pakistani-mediated talks described as "efforts to secure a ceasefire and broader agreement"
- Hormuz flow remains severely reduced
- International community calling for de-escalation
- Situation remains highly fluid — monitor for ceasefire developments, escalation, or broadening of conflict to involve other actors

### Update — May 28, 2026

**Deal "largely negotiated" — Trump announcement (May 23, 2026)**: President Trump posted on social media that an Iran deal is "largely negotiated, subject to finalization." This follows several weeks of shuttle diplomacy. The proposed framework involves:
- A **60-day ceasefire extension** (not a permanent settlement)
- Iran reopens the Strait of Hormuz to normal traffic during the ceasefire
- Iran allowed to freely sell oil (partial sanctions relief)
- Negotiations on Iran's nuclear program to begin during the ceasefire window

**Key sticking points** (as of May 24–25, 2026 reporting):
- **Iran's uranium stockpile**: Trump insists Iran must relinquish its 440+ kg of highly enriched uranium (HEU near weapons grade). Iranian officials say nuclear negotiations cannot begin until the war-ending memorandum is first signed
- **Frozen assets**: Iran demands immediate unfreezing of billions in overseas accounts; the US is conditioning this on nuclear concessions
- **Sequencing dispute**: Iran wants security guarantees first, nuclear talk second; the US wants nuclear framework agreed before lifting economic pressure

**Strikes continuing despite talks (May 25–26, 2026)**: CNN reported US military strikes against Iranian missile launch sites and naval assets on May 25 even as negotiations proceeded — classic "maximum pressure + talks simultaneously" approach. Iran threatened retaliation, raising risks of the negotiating environment collapsing.

**Hormuz flow implications**: The proposed deal's central economic prize is Hormuz reopening. At 30% of normal flow, every additional week of closure deepens the global energy crisis. If the 60-day ceasefire is agreed and Hormuz flow normalizes even partially, oil markets could see a significant price correction downward.

**Historical parallel — next 60–90 days decisive**: The situation mirrors the structure of past Iran nuclear negotiations: mutual maximum pressure, a framework announcement declared premature, followed by either collapse or a partial deal that holds imperfectly. The parties are in a race between diplomatic momentum and military escalation on the ground.
