## Summary
In early 2026, escalating tensions between the United States and Iran broke into open military conflict, with the US launching strikes against Iranian military and nuclear infrastructure. The conflict has triggered a severe global energy crisis: oil flow through the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly one-fifth of global oil normally passes — fell to approximately 30% of its previous levels in Q1 2026, sending oil prices surging and disrupting supply chains worldwide. Diplomatic efforts including Pakistani mediation are ongoing as of May 2026.

## Key Points
- The US has launched military strikes on Iran; Iran has responded with regional proxy escalation and threats to close the Strait of Hormuz
- Oil flow through the Strait of Hormuz fell to ~30% of previous quarter levels in Q1 2026 (US EIA data)
- Global protests erupted on May Day 2026 calling for peace, against the war, and against the resulting energy and fuel price crisis
- Pakistan's deputy PM Ishaq Dar met with the US chargé d'affaires to facilitate ceasefire talks — indirect diplomacy channel active
- The conflict has exacerbated an already-stressed global energy market, with cascading effects on inflation and cost-of-living globally
- Russian strikes on Kyiv (using Oreshnik hypersonic missiles) continue simultaneously, compounding geopolitical instability

### Background: US-Iran Tensions

US-Iran hostility has deep roots:
- **1979 Islamic Revolution** ousted the US-backed Shah; the hostage crisis cemented enmity
- **Nuclear program**: Iran's uranium enrichment program has been the central flashpoint for decades. The 2015 JCPOA (nuclear deal) temporarily capped enrichment; the US withdrew in 2018 (Trump). Subsequent negotiations failed to produce a lasting agreement
- **Regional proxies**: Iran funds and arms Hezbollah (Lebanon), Hamas (Gaza), Houthi rebels (Yemen), and Shia militias in Iraq — the so-called "Axis of Resistance"
- **2020 escalation**: US killed IRGC General Qasem Soleimani; Iran retaliated with ballistic missile strikes on US bases in Iraq
- **2024**: Iran and Israel engaged in direct missile exchanges (April and October 2024), dramatically escalating regional tensions
- **2025**: With nuclear negotiations fully collapsed and Iran having enriched uranium to near-weapons grade, the US and Israel increased pressure for military action

### The 2026 Conflict

As of May 2026, the following is known:
- **US military strikes**: The US has conducted strikes against Iranian military and nuclear sites; targets have reportedly included enrichment facilities at Natanz and Fordow, IRGC command infrastructure, and naval assets
- **Iranian responses**: Iran has threatened and in some instances attempted to block the Strait of Hormuz; proxy forces (Houthis in Yemen, Shia militias in Iraq) have escalated attacks on US and allied interests
- **Ceasefire negotiations**: Talks are ongoing through indirect channels including Pakistan. The US chargé d'affaires (rather than the full ambassador, suggesting elevated but not normalised diplomatic contact) has been engaged by Pakistani mediation

### The Strait of Hormuz and Global Energy Impact

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow chokepoint between Iran and Oman — at its narrowest, only 21 miles wide. Through it passes:
- ~17–21 million barrels of oil per day (pre-crisis) — roughly 20% of global supply
- Significant volumes of LNG from Qatar and the UAE
- A large fraction of Gulf state hydrocarbon exports

The 30% fall in Hormuz flow in Q1 2026 represents an unprecedented disruption. Comparable historical events:
- **1973 Arab oil embargo**: ~7% of global supply removed; oil prices quadrupled
- **1979 Iranian Revolution**: ~5–6% reduction; oil prices doubled
- **1990 Gulf War**: temporary spike on invasion

A sustained 70% reduction in Hormuz flow would remove ~14 million barrels/day from global supply — roughly 14% of global consumption — a shock with no historical precedent in the modern era.

**Market effects:**
- Brent crude prices have spiked significantly (specific prices to be updated as situation evolves)
- European natural gas prices have risen sharply as LNG diversions and risk premiums mount
- Airlines and shipping companies face fuel surcharges
- Inflationary pressures from energy costs are rippling into goods prices globally
- Nations with large strategic petroleum reserves (US SPR, IEA members) have released reserves to buffer the shock

**Alternative routes:**
- Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline (Petroline) can carry ~5 million bpd — a partial bypass
- UAE has an Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline bypassing Hormuz — ~1.5 million bpd capacity
- Neither comes close to substituting for full Hormuz flow

### Global Protests — May Day 2026

On May 1, 2026, coordinated global protests took place in dozens of countries. Demonstrators called for:
- An immediate ceasefire in the US-Iran conflict
- Higher wages in response to the energy-driven cost-of-living crisis
- Better working conditions (traditional May Day demands)
- An end to what protesters described as US military overreach in the Middle East

The protests reflected widespread public anxiety about the intersection of war and economic hardship — energy prices have become the most visible consumer impact of the conflict.

### Regional Spillovers

The US-Iran conflict is interacting with ongoing regional tensions:
- **Yemen**: Houthi rebels (Iranian-backed) have intensified attacks on Red Sea shipping and Gulf infrastructure
- **Iraq**: Shia militia groups have attacked US bases; Iraqi government is caught between US military presence and Iranian influence
- **Lebanon**: Hezbollah (Iranian-backed) has been a wildcard; the risk of renewed Lebanon conflict is elevated (see separate geopolitics analysis)
- **Syria**: Iranian-backed forces remain present; Israeli strikes on Iranian-linked targets in Syria have continued

### Diplomatic Landscape

**Pakistan as mediator**: Pakistan has historically maintained communications with both Washington and Tehran, and Islamabad has strong incentives to stabilise the region — it shares a long border with Iran, depends on imported energy, and has its own complex relationship with both the US and Iran.

**Other actors:**
- **China**: has significant oil purchase agreements with Iran; is calling for restraint but benefits from weakened US standing in the Middle East; also serves as a potential back-channel
- **Russia**: strategically benefits from high energy prices and US military distraction; unlikely to play a constructive mediating role
- **European Union**: pushing for negotiations; EU companies face severe economic impacts from energy price spikes
- **Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, UAE)**: cautiously supportive of weakening Iranian power but acutely vulnerable to Hormuz disruption and regional escalation

### Why This Matters

1. **Energy security**: The event has demonstrated how a single chokepoint can destabilise global energy markets; accelerating energy transition and supply diversification debates
2. **Nuclear proliferation**: If Iran's nuclear program is successfully degraded, a temporary setback to proliferation; if not, potential acceleration of other states' nuclear ambitions
3. **US credibility**: US military engagement tests alliance commitments and global perceptions of US power projection
4. **Global inflation**: Energy prices feed into transport, manufacturing, and food production; the war is amplifying inflationary pressures already present from structural factors
5. **Regional order**: The conflict is reshaping Middle East alignments — which actors can hedge, which must choose sides

- Pakistani-mediated talks described as "efforts to secure a ceasefire and broader agreement"
- Hormuz flow remains severely reduced
- International community calling for de-escalation
- Situation remains highly fluid — monitor for ceasefire developments, escalation, or broadening of conflict to involve other actors

### Update — May 28, 2026

**Deal "largely negotiated" — Trump announcement (May 23, 2026)**: President Trump posted on social media that an Iran deal is "largely negotiated, subject to finalization." This follows several weeks of shuttle diplomacy. The proposed framework involves:
- A **60-day ceasefire extension** (not a permanent settlement)
- Iran reopens the Strait of Hormuz to normal traffic during the ceasefire
- Iran allowed to freely sell oil (partial sanctions relief)
- Negotiations on Iran's nuclear program to begin during the ceasefire window

**Key sticking points** (as of May 24–25, 2026 reporting):
- **Iran's uranium stockpile**: Trump insists Iran must relinquish its 440+ kg of highly enriched uranium (HEU near weapons grade). Iranian officials say nuclear negotiations cannot begin until the war-ending memorandum is first signed
- **Frozen assets**: Iran demands immediate unfreezing of billions in overseas accounts; the US is conditioning this on nuclear concessions
- **Sequencing dispute**: Iran wants security guarantees first, nuclear talk second; the US wants nuclear framework agreed before lifting economic pressure

**Strikes continuing despite talks (May 25–26, 2026)**: CNN reported US military strikes against Iranian missile launch sites and naval assets on May 25 even as negotiations proceeded — classic "maximum pressure + talks simultaneously" approach. Iran threatened retaliation, raising risks of the negotiating environment collapsing.

**Hormuz flow implications**: The proposed deal's central economic prize is Hormuz reopening. At 30% of normal flow, every additional week of closure deepens the global energy crisis. If the 60-day ceasefire is agreed and Hormuz flow normalizes even partially, oil markets could see a significant price correction downward.

**Historical parallel — next 60–90 days decisive**: The situation mirrors the structure of past Iran nuclear negotiations: mutual maximum pressure, a framework announcement declared premature, followed by either collapse or a partial deal that holds imperfectly. The parties are in a race between diplomatic momentum and military escalation on the ground.

**Hormuz traffic near zero (late May 2026)**: CNN shipping data visualization (April 29, 2026) and UANI tracking confirm that since approximately May 6, 2026, open vessel transits through the Strait have fallen to near zero per day, compared to approximately 3,000 vessels per month before hostilities began in February. This near-total blockade — not merely reduced flow — represents an energy supply emergency of historical proportions. The 30% flow figure cited by the EIA for Q1 2026 reflects partial movement through alternative routes (Saudi East-West pipeline, UAE pipeline, Cape of Good Hope diversions); the actual Strait itself is essentially closed.

**IRGC redefines the Strait's geographic extent**: Iran's IRGC Navy announced that the Strait of Hormuz has been redefined as a "vast operational area" stretching from Jask (on Iran's southeastern coast) to Siri Island — far broader than the historically recognized 21-mile-wide corridor. This expansion of claimed operational space complicates deal terms: any agreement specifying "reopening of the Strait" requires both sides to agree on what that means geographically.

**Bloomberg: normalization within a month of deal**: Bloomberg reported (May 27, 2026), citing Iranian state television, that maritime traffic could return to normal levels within one month of a deal being finalized. This timeline, if accurate, gives global energy markets a specific window for price recovery after agreement — a significant economic incentive for all parties to finalize terms quickly.

## Cross-Disciplinary Connections

### Energy Shocks and Macroeconomic Transmission: The Stagflation Mechanism in 2026

The 2026 global energy crisis generated by the Hormuz closure is the most direct test of macroeconomic resilience since the 2008 financial crisis — but unlike 2008, which was a demand shock originating in financial markets, this is a supply shock with inflationary teeth. The fundamental economic problem is that energy is both a final consumption good and an intermediate input into virtually every other production process: oil and gas price increases flow through transportation costs, petrochemical inputs into plastics and fertilizers, heating costs, and electricity generation, creating a cascading second-round effect where the initial energy price spike amplifies into broader inflation across the goods-and-services basket. This is precisely the mechanism that produced the "Great Inflation" of 1973–1981 in the United States and Europe — and the parallel is uncomfortably close. The critical variable distinguishing 2026 from 1973 is the degree to which central banks retain credibility: if inflation expectations become unanchored (workers demand higher wages anticipating continued inflation, firms pre-emptively raise prices), the supply shock transforms into a sustained inflationary spiral that requires Volcker-style rate increases to break. If central banks can maintain credibility while providing limited stimulus to offset the recessionary component of the shock, the stagflationary trap can be avoided.

The yield-curve implications are profound. An energy supply shock forces central banks into an impossible trilemma: raise rates to fight inflation (worsening the recession), cut rates to fight recession (ignoring inflation), or hold steady (doing neither effectively). The yield curve's shape — specifically the spread between 2-year and 10-year Treasury yields — becomes a real-time indicator of market beliefs about which policy dominates. During the Q1 2026 disruption, a yield curve that steepened at the back end (long rates rising faster than short rates) would indicate market belief in sustained inflation; a curve that flattened or inverted (short rates rising, long rates flat or falling) would indicate recession fears dominating. The oil price's specific path — whether it peaked quickly and fell on deal news, or remained elevated — determines which trajectory markets price. The Bloomberg report noting potential Hormuz normalization within one month of a deal is therefore not just an energy market story but a yield-curve story: a rapid price correction would allow central banks to stand down from emergency hawkishness and allow long rates to fall, steepening the curve in the benign direction.

### Strategic Petroleum Reserves as a Macroeconomic Shock Absorber

The coordinated release of Strategic Petroleum Reserves (SPR) by the US and IEA member states during the Hormuz crisis illustrates a policy instrument whose economic logic is elegant but whose practical capacity is limited. The US SPR holds approximately 350–400 million barrels (having been substantially drawn down during the 2022 Russia-Ukraine energy shock and only partially replenished by 2026). At 1 million barrels per day of release, it buys roughly 12–16 months of supplemental supply — meaningful for a temporary disruption but wholly inadequate for a sustained Hormuz closure. The IEA's coordinated mechanism is designed for exactly this scenario — the 1974 oil crisis precipitated its creation — but its reserve size, like the Saudi and UAE bypass pipeline capacity, is calibrated to bridge temporary disruptions, not to substitute for 70% of Hormuz throughput indefinitely. This limitation is a direct manifestation of a macroeconomic principle: no strategic reserve system is designed to substitute for a fundamental disruption of the magnitude the Hormuz closure represents; it is designed to smooth transitions and reduce panic-driven price spikes while diplomatic solutions are found. The economic incentive for rapid deal finalization is thus structurally embedded in the architecture of SPR policy.

### Global Protests, Political Economy, and the Energy-Inflation Nexus

The May Day 2026 global protests — combining anti-war demands with cost-of-living grievances — represent the political economy transmission of the energy crisis into democratic governance. Historical evidence strongly supports this link: the 1973–1974 energy crisis directly contributed to the collapse of the Heath government in the UK (miners' strike amplified by energy scarcity), the defeat of incumbent governments across Europe, and eventually the Carter malaise and Reagan revolution in the US. Energy price inflation is politically toxic because it is universal (no household or firm escapes it), visible (fuel prices at the pump are among the most frequently observed prices in any economy), and difficult to attribute to local governance (it arrives from the Middle East via global markets). This combination — universality, visibility, and distal causation — makes energy inflation the most destabilizing variety for incumbent governments. The 2026 protests were transnational precisely because the supply shock was global; whether their political consequences (government changes, policy pivots, accelerated energy transition spending) materialize depends on how quickly the Hormuz situation resolves.

### Interconnected Crises and Portfolio Risk Management

From a portfolio theory perspective, the 2026 energy crisis illustrates the failure of standard correlation-based diversification during tail events. The assumption embedded in mean-variance optimization (Markowitz) is that asset returns are drawn from a relatively stable joint distribution. The Hormuz crisis violates this assumption: it creates simultaneous negative shocks to equities (recession risk), bonds (inflation risk), and currencies of oil-importing nations (current account deterioration), while positively shocking oil producers' assets and commodity indices. An investor "diversified" across global equities, global bonds, and commodities might find all three legs of the portfolio moving adversely simultaneously — the classic scenario that drives the "risk parity" and "all-weather" portfolio debates. The practical implication is that geopolitical tail risk requires instruments beyond correlation diversification: physical commodity exposure, energy equity positions, inflation-linked bonds (TIPS), or explicit options strategies (buying long-dated call options on crude futures as a tail hedge) that pay off specifically in the Hormuz closure scenario.

### Update — May 29, 2026

**Oil prices quantified**: The energy market impact of the deal negotiations is now measurable in specific price data. As of May 27, Brent crude fell 3.83% to $95.77/barrel and WTI dropped 4.75% to $88.57/barrel on cumulative deal optimism — representing approximately an 11% decline from the Hormuz-crisis peak prices. This price action illustrates the energy market's sensitivity to diplomatic signals: each credible step toward an MOU triggers a measurable downward correction, while hawkish signals (strikes continuing, IRGC disputes about deal terms) push prices back up. The net effect is a highly volatile energy market in which deal uncertainty functions as a price premium of roughly 15–20% over where oil would trade if Hormuz were fully open.

**Piper Sandler contrarian warning (May 26)**: Investment bank Piper Sandler issued a note warning that even if a deal is signed, the Strait of Hormuz could remain effectively closed for months due to the practical complexities of mine clearance (Iran must remove mines within 30 days under the proposed MOU, but commercial shippers will face weeks of risk aversion even after formal clearance), Iranian military repositioning, and insurer risk assessments. Piper Sandler predicted oil could reach new highs if deal momentum collapses or if the mine clearance process is delayed or contested. This is a critical asymmetric risk: the upside of a deal (oil drops to pre-crisis levels over weeks) is real, but so is the downside (oil resumes upward trajectory toward historical highs if talks fail).

**Russia's Oreshnik strikes compound global energy instability (May 23–24, 2026)**: Russia launched its largest single attack on Kyiv of the entire full-scale war on May 23–24, deploying an Oreshnik hypersonic ballistic missile alongside 600 drones and 90 other missiles. The attack killed at least 4 people, injured approximately 100, and damaged or destroyed around 30 residential buildings in Kyiv. It was the third use of the Oreshnik in the war. European leaders condemned it as "nuclear brinkmanship." The significance for energy markets is indirect but real: simultaneous major military escalation in Ukraine and the Persian Gulf reinforces the "geopolitical risk premium" across all energy assets, reduces institutional appetite for rolling back strategic reserve releases, and creates additional upward pressure on European natural gas prices already stressed by Hormuz LNG disruption.

**Trump's final determination pending (May 29)**: Trump entered the Situation Room on May 29 to make his "final determination" on the Iran MOU. The proposed deal framework includes Hormuz reopening, 30-day mine clearance, phased sanctions relief, and Iranian commitment to never build a nuclear weapon — but Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei has not yet formally confirmed acceptance. The energy market implication: a Trump "yes" today could trigger another significant downward oil price move within hours; a "no" or delay could reverse the recent decline.

### Update — June 1, 2026

**Tentative MOU reached (PBS NewsHour, May 28–29, 2026):** US and Iranian negotiators have now reached agreement on the MOU text — going beyond "largely negotiated" to the text being agreed. Trump has not yet given final approval. The MOU now also includes a provision ending the **Israel-Hezbollah war in Lebanon** — a significant regional addition that was not in earlier reporting. If signed, this would simultaneously resolve the Iran-US conflict, the Hormuz closure, and the Lebanon ceasefire within a single diplomatic instrument.

**Brent crude confirmed peaked at $138/barrel (April 7):** BlackRock's Investment Institute confirmed the April 7, 2026 Brent crude price of $138/barrel as the crisis peak. The subsequent decline to ~$96 (May 27) represents deal-anticipation pricing. The EIA's formal position: oil prices will carry a structural risk premium "throughout the forecast period" regardless of Hormuz normalization, permanently repricing the geopolitical option on Strait closure.

**UAE's OPEC exit adds 2M bpd supply when conditions normalize:** The UAE's May 1, 2026 OPEC departure means that once Hormuz reopens and UAE increases production toward its 5 million bpd target (from current constrained 3.2M), the global oil market could see approximately 2 million additional barrels per day of supply. This, combined with Saudi production increases, creates a scenario where post-MOU oil prices could fall sharply — potentially back toward the $70–75/barrel pre-crisis range within weeks. The energy market's structural response to the crisis is therefore likely to overshoot on the downside once geopolitical premiums unwind.

**May Day protests' political consequences emerging:** The May 1, 2026 global protests generated political pressure on incumbent governments across Europe and Asia. Several European governments have announced emergency energy subsidy packages. The German coalition government is under pressure to extend winter heating subsidies through summer given the heatwave-energy price double squeeze. France's government approved emergency energy relief payments to low-income households in May. The political transmission of energy price shocks to government instability is tracking closely to the 1973–1974 pattern.
