## Summary
Cuba is experiencing its most severe humanitarian crisis in decades, triggered by a comprehensive US fuel blockade that began in February 2026 targeting oil tankers destined for the island. The US blockade — primarily aimed at Mexican state oil company Pemex, which had been supplying Cuba with subsidized crude — has left Cuba completely without diesel and oil as of May 2026. The consequences are catastrophic: nationwide blackouts lasting 20–22 hours per day, ~1 million Cubans lacking reliable water access (delivery trucks have no fuel), 96,000+ surgeries delayed, and the childhood immunization program for newborns suspended. Cuba's power grid collapsed entirely on March 16, 2026, leaving the island without electricity. The crisis demonstrates the devastating efficacy of targeted secondary sanctions on small, import-dependent economies — and has generated significant international humanitarian concern while remaining largely absent from Western media coverage dominated by Middle East and Ukraine conflicts.

## Key Points
- **Diesel exhaustion confirmed:** Cuban Energy Minister Vicente De La O'levy announced on May 14, 2026 that Cuba has completely run out of oil and diesel
- **Blackouts:** 20–22 hours per day of electricity outages across Cuba; complete national grid collapse on March 16, 2026
- **US blockade mechanism:** US began blocking oil tankers to Cuba in February 2026, targeting Pemex (Mexico) and other suppliers via secondary sanctions
- **Water crisis:** ~1 million Cubans lack reliable water access because delivery trucks have no fuel; sanitation infrastructure failing
- **Medical collapse:** 96,000+ surgeries delayed; childhood immunization program for newborns paused; hospitals on emergency backup power or candles
- **Diplomatic stall:** Axios reported (May 28, 2026) that US-Cuba talks to resolve the crisis have stalled, with no relief in sight

## Details

### Background: Cuba's Energy Dependency

Cuba's energy system has been structurally precarious since the Soviet collapse (1991), when the loss of Soviet oil subsidies triggered the "Special Period in Time of Peace" — Cuba's worst modern economic crisis. The island recovered gradually through Venezuelan oil subsidies (Hugo Chávez's PetroCaribe program, 2005–2018), which provided Cuba with 100,000+ barrels/day on preferential payment terms (often in exchange for Cuban medical services in Venezuela).

**Dependency structure (pre-2026):**
- Venezuela: ~50,000–70,000 barrels/day (heavily reduced since Maduro's oil collapse)
- Mexico (Pemex): ~10,000–20,000 barrels/day under Lopez Obrador's solidarity arrangements
- Russia: Limited direct supply, complicated by Ukraine war sanctions
- Cuba's own production: <2,000 barrels/day (declining domestic fields)
- Daily consumption need: ~70,000–80,000 barrels/day (dramatically reduced from pre-crisis levels)

Cuba has no meaningful domestic energy transition capacity. Renewables (primarily wind, some solar) account for <5% of electricity generation. Thermal plants burning heavy fuel oil or diesel generate ~85% of electricity. When fuel stops, electricity stops.

### The 2026 US Blockade: Mechanism and Escalation

**February 2026:** The Trump administration (second term) dramatically escalated Cuba sanctions via secondary sanctions targeting shipping companies, tankers, and flag-of-convenience registries that service Cuban ports with oil cargo.

**Primary target — Pemex:** Mexico's state oil company had been supplying Cuba under arrangements inherited from the Lopez Obrador administration. The US threatened Pemex with exclusion from US financial markets (dollar settlement) and secondary sanctions on Pemex executives if Cuban oil shipments continued. Given Pemex's debt load (~$100B) and reliance on US markets, Mexico complied and suspended Cuba oil deliveries.

**Other suppliers cut off:**
- Greek shipping firms operating tankers under Panama/Belize flags were threatened with US secondary sanctions
- Venezuela, already under maximum pressure sanctions, could not increase supply without risking additional asset seizures
- Russian supply: Complicated by Russia's own sanctions regime and limited tanker fleet reaching Caribbean

**Effect on Cuban economy:**
The blockade compounded already severe pre-existing conditions. Cuba's hard currency earnings come primarily from: (1) tourism (~$2.5B/year, devastated by COVID and not fully recovered), (2) remittances (~$3B/year, partially restricted by US sanctions), (3) nickel exports (~$300M/year), (4) medical services exports to Venezuela (~$5–8B/year, declining). With no hard currency to buy fuel at market prices from alternative suppliers (e.g., Algeria, Angola), Cuba had no escape valve.

### The Blackout Crisis: Cascading Infrastructure Failure

**March 16, 2026 — National Grid Collapse:**
Cuba's Unión Eléctrica (national electricity company) confirmed total national grid collapse. All six major thermoelectric plants went offline simultaneously when fuel reserves were exhausted. The restoration was partial and slow, with fuel deliveries from a final emergency shipment from Algeria buying only days of operation.

**Current situation (May 2026):**
- Havana: 20–22 hour daily blackouts. Power available for ~2–4 hours, typically between 2–6 AM
- Eastern provinces (Holguín, Santiago, Guantánamo): Often 24-hour outages for days at a time
- Essential services (hospitals, water pumping stations): Dependent on diesel generators, but generator fuel also depleted
- Traffic management: Collapsed. Police manually directing intersections where they operate

**Cascading effects:**
1. **Water:** Cuba's water distribution relies on diesel-powered pumping stations. Without fuel, tanks don't fill. ~1 million Cubans (estimate) lack running water consistently
2. **Food:** Refrigeration failure causes food spoilage on a national scale — both household and commercial food stocks. State food distribution (libreta rationing system) disrupted because distribution trucks have no fuel
3. **Medical:** Hospital generators running critically low. Surgeries requiring sustained electrical power postponed or cancelled. Blood storage compromised by refrigeration failure. Immunization cold chain broken — childhood vaccine program suspended (documented by WHO Cuba office)
4. **Agriculture:** Irrigation pumps offline during planting season → crop failures projected to worsen food shortages in 2026 H2
5. **Industry:** Cuba's already anemic industrial sector halted completely

UN Special Rapporteur on Unilateral Coercive Measures (Alena Douhan) issued a statement calling the US blockade a "collective punishment" of the Cuban civilian population — a characterization rejected by the US State Department.

### US-Cuba Diplomatic Dimension

**Axios reported (May 28, 2026):** US-Cuba talks aimed at resolving the fuel crisis have stalled. The Biden administration had partially eased some Cuba restrictions (2022–2024), but the Trump administration reversed many of these. The US position: Cuba must release political prisoners, allow free elections, and end support for Venezuela's Maduro government before any sanction relief.

**Cuba's position:** Considers the blockade a violation of international law; rejects US preconditions as interference in Cuban sovereignty; calls for UNSC action (vetoed by US).

**Mexico's position (Claudia Sheinbaum government):** Strongly critical of US pressure on Pemex; called the secondary sanctions an attack on Mexican sovereignty; but ultimately complied due to economic dependence on US markets. Mexico has appealed to the Organization of American States (OAS) without effect.

**Russia's position:** Russian Foreign Ministry condemned the "inhuman" US blockade in official statements; practical assistance has been limited by Russia's own sanctions constraints and logistics challenges in reaching Cuba.

### Historical Context: The "Special Period" Comparison

Cuba has endured severe energy crises before. The 1990–1993 "Special Period in Time of Peace" following Soviet collapse provides the closest historical parallel:

- **1990–1993:** Soviet oil subsidies ended; Cuba's oil imports fell from 13 million tons/year to 6 million tons. Food imports halved. GDP contracted 35–40% in three years. Blackouts (apagones) of 12–16 hours became normal
- **Response:** Bicycle imports (1 million from China), urban agriculture, rationing, substitution of oxen for tractors. Tourism industry developed rapidly (with human rights compromises — sex tourism)
- **Recovery:** Venezuela's PetroCaribe program (2005) ended the energy crisis with subsidized oil

**2026 comparison:**
- The 2026 crisis is more acute because: (1) Cuba's economy is weaker than in 1990; (2) Venezuela can no longer provide significant oil; (3) The blockade is actively enforced via secondary sanctions (not just passive withdrawal of subsidies); (4) Cuba's population is older (brain drain of youth has accelerated since 2020)
- Unlike 1990, there is no obvious alternative patron ready to rescue Cuba: China provides trade credits and limited goods but has explicitly declined to become Cuba's primary energy provider; Russia is preoccupied with Ukraine

### International Law and Legitimacy Debate

The US embargo on Cuba — in existence in various forms since 1962 — is voted against every year at the UN General Assembly by overwhelming margins (typically 185–2, only US and Israel supporting). The additional fuel-specific secondary sanctions of 2026 have generated even broader condemnation:

- **EU:** Officially opposes the US embargo under its "Blocking Statute" (1996); activated formal anti-coercion instrument against US secondary sanctions targeting European companies with Cuba business
- **Latin America:** ALBA member states (Venezuela, Bolivia, Nicaragua, etc.) issued joint condemnation; even US-aligned Colombia and Chile issued statements of concern
- **Cuba's legal argument:** Customary international law prohibits economic coercion; secondary sanctions on third countries (Pemex) violate territorial sovereignty of Mexico

**US legal argument:** The Cuba embargo is a lawful exercise of sovereign economic foreign policy; secondary sanctions on entities violating US law (Cuba sanctions regulations) are within US jurisdiction; Cuba's government bears responsibility for its population's suffering through decades of mismanagement.
