## Summary
The IMF's April 2026 World Economic Outlook projects global growth at 3.1% in 2026 — below the 3.8% pre-pandemic average and technically above the 2.5% recession threshold but in a fragile zone where multiple simultaneous shocks could trigger contraction. The global economy in 2026 navigates an unusual combination of pressures: persistent inflation (above 2% targets in most economies), geopolitical fragmentation accelerating de-globalization, an AI productivity promise that has yet to appear in GDP statistics, and the hangover of a decade of ultra-low interest rates giving way to structurally higher capital costs. The J.P. Morgan global recession probability stands at 35% — elevated but not imminent.

### Update — June 1, 2026

**IMF WEO April 2026 title: "Global Economy in the Shadow of War":** The IMF's April 2026 World Economic Outlook carries the explicit subtitle acknowledging the unprecedented multi-front geopolitical crisis: the US-Iran war, the continuing Ukraine conflict, and the India-Pakistan ceasefire have collectively created what the IMF describes as a "shadow of war" suppressing investment, disrupting supply chains, and elevating risk premiums globally. The IMF downgrades 2026 growth to **3.1%** (from an earlier 3.3% forecast), with headline inflation projected to **rise modestly in 2026** before resuming decline in 2027 — directly attributable to the Hormuz energy shock. **UN UNCTAD forecast more pessimistic at 2.7%** — below 2025 levels and well below the pre-pandemic average of ~3.5%.

**US mild recession risk in H1 2026 now explicitly modeled:** Multiple forecasters (Morgan Stanley, Deloitte, Meer) have published scenarios in which US real GDP growth turns **negative in Q1–Q2 2026** due to the combined effect of: (1) Trump tariff impacts on consumer goods and input costs; (2) energy price spike from Hormuz closure transmitting into US household spending and transportation costs; (3) immigration restriction effects on labor markets in construction and agriculture. The consensus remains that a US recession is a risk rather than a baseline, but the probability has risen materially from late 2025 forecasts.

**World Bank Global Economic Prospects — updated downward:** The World Bank's June 2026 Global Economic Prospects report is expected to show significant downward revisions from its January 2026 baseline, reflecting the mid-year geopolitical shocks. Developing economies are particularly exposed: higher energy import costs, reduced remittances (as advanced economies slow), and dollar strength (typical during geopolitical risk-off episodes) create a simultaneous triple squeeze.

## Key Points
- **Global growth**: IMF projects 3.1% GDP growth in 2026 (2025: 3.3%) — US 2.2%, Euro area 1.3%, China 4.5%, India 6.3%, Sub-Saharan Africa 4.2%
- **Inflation**: Not yet resolved; US core PCE at ~2.8% (Fed target: 2%), global headline inflation modestly rising in 2026 before declining 2027

- **AI and productivity**: No broad-based GDP impact visible yet; economic historians compare to 1990s IT productivity paradox; consensus expects productivity acceleration 2027–2030
- **Recession probability**: 30–35% over next 12 months (J.P. Morgan); primary risks: US tariff escalation, Middle East energy shock, China property sector deterioration

### The Growth Landscape: Divergence, Not Recession
The defining feature of 2026's economic landscape is **divergence**: robust in some regions, stagnant in others, with tail risks concentrated in specific sectors.

**United States — resilient but slowing**: The US economy decelerated sharply in Q1 2026 (GDP growth ~1.2% annualized) as the impact of 2023–24 rate hikes filtered through the housing and credit sectors. However, consumer spending remains positive, supported by strong labor markets (unemployment ~4.3%, elevated but not recessionary) and real wage growth. The Federal Reserve cut rates three times (25 bps each) in 2025; the Fed funds rate sits at ~4.0–4.25% in mid-2026 — still restrictive. Goldman Sachs projects 2.2% full-year US growth; Morgan Stanley 2.0%.

**Euro area — near-stagnation**: Germany (-0.5% in 2025, slight recovery in 2026), France (1.0%), Italy (0.7%), Spain (2.1%). The European growth divergence reflects: Germany's structural deindustrialization (energy shock from Russia gas cutoff permanently raised European industrial costs ~20–30%), aging demographics, and weak Chinese demand for European machinery and cars. ECB rate cuts have provided some stimulus. Average Euro area growth: 1.3%.

**China — structurally slower but not crisis**: China's growth target is 4.5–5%; IMF baseline 4.5%. The property sector crisis (Evergrande, Country Garden defaults; total residential construction declined 40% from peak) has permanently reduced investment-driven growth. Xi Jinping's "common prosperity" pivot reduced private sector dynamism. High youth unemployment (~15–18%) and weak consumer confidence persist. Exports remain strong (manufacturing dominance in electric vehicles, solar, and tech components) but face rising tariff barriers from US, EU.

**India — secular growth story**: 6.3% growth, driven by demographics (youngest large population in the world), infrastructure investment, and technology services sector expansion. India has overtaken China as the world's most populous country and is on track to become the world's third-largest economy by 2030. Key risks: climate vulnerability (monsoon agriculture), fiscal deficit management, geopolitical tensions with Pakistan/China.

**Emerging markets divergence**: 
- Southeast Asia (Vietnam, Indonesia, Philippines): benefiting from China+1 supply chain relocation; 5–7% growth
- Sub-Saharan Africa: 4.2% average, with wide variation; Nigeria (6.1%) and Kenya (5.8%) strong; Sahel region dragged by political instability
- Latin America: Mexico 2.0% (tariff uncertainty); Brazil 2.4% (commodities export resilience); Argentina -1.0% (Milei austerity program)

### Inflation: The Unfinished Battle
Inflation has not returned to central bank targets despite three years of restrictive monetary policy:

**Structural inflation drivers** that have not fully resolved:
1. **Services inflation**: wages in service sectors (healthcare, education, housing) have risen permanently; services CPI in the US running ~3.5–4.0%
2. **Energy transition costs**: carbon pricing, renewable infrastructure investment, and fossil fuel divestment have raised baseline energy costs in Europe
3. **Deglobalization**: shortening supply chains to domestic/near-shore production increases costs (labor costs in developed economies are 5–10× those in low-cost Asian producers)
4. **Defense spending**: military rearmament (see [[2026-05-30-europe-rearmament-nato-russia-threat]]) adds fiscal demand; Germany's defense spending increase alone adds ~0.3% to European demand

**Central bank policy**:
- Federal Reserve: 4.0–4.25% funds rate; cautious cuts; dual mandate tension (inflation above target but labor market cooling)
- ECB: 3.0% deposit rate; has cut more aggressively than Fed given weaker European growth
- Bank of England: 4.5%; inflation stickier than ECB given wage-price dynamics
- Bank of Japan: finally normalized to 0.5–0.75% after decades of zero/negative rates; yen stabilization has reduced imported inflation

**The "last mile" problem**: Reducing inflation from 3% to 2% appears much harder than from 8% to 3%. The Fed's "neutral rate" may be higher than pre-2020 estimates (~2.5% historically) — possibly 3.0–3.5% — meaning current policy may be less restrictive than the nominal rate suggests.

### Geopolitical Fragmentation and the Trade War's Economic Cost
The **geoeconomic fragmentation** trend is arguably the most significant structural shift in the global economy since the 1990s globalization revolution:

**Tariff landscape (2026)**:
- US tariffs on Chinese goods: 60% average (Trump 2.0, effective January 2025)
- US tariffs on EU goods: 10–15% average (negotiated truce lower than threatened 25%)
- EU countermeasures on US goods: 15% on agricultural and technology products
- China tariffs on US goods: 60–80% on agricultural, energy, aerospace products

**IMF fragmentation estimate**: Studies suggest fragmentation into two blocs (US-aligned vs. China-aligned) would reduce global output by 2.5–4% permanently. Including technology decoupling (dual semiconductor ecosystems, AI computing separation) adds another 1–3%. Total potential cost: 3–7% of global GDP.

**Semiconductor wars**: US CHIPS Act (2022) triggered $300B+ in semiconductor investment globally. Taiwan TSMC is building fabs in Arizona and Germany. Intel, Samsung, and TSMC are each spending $100B+ on next-generation fab capacity. But the most advanced chips (2–3nm) remain concentrated in Taiwan, creating geopolitical vulnerability (see [[2026-05-30-china-taiwan-strait-military-tensions]]).

**China+1 relocation**: Multinationals shifting supply chains out of China have primarily gone to: Vietnam (electronics, apparel), India (pharmaceuticals, electronics), Mexico (automotive, consumer goods), Indonesia (raw materials, basic manufacturing). China retains dominance in solar panels (80% of global supply), EV batteries (60%), rare earth processing (60%), and steel (50%).

**Currency fragmentation**: De-dollarization accelerated — BRICS payment systems, bilateral trade in local currencies (Russia-China, India-UAE, Brazil-China), Saudi Arabia accepting yuan for some oil sales. The dollar's global reserve share fell from 71% (2001) to ~58% (2026). However, no credible alternative reserve currency exists; the dollar's decline has slowed as the euro and yuan both face structural challenges.

### The AI Productivity Paradox
**The paradox**: AI capabilities are advancing exponentially (GPT-5 equivalent models generate trillion-dollar investment decisions; autonomous agents handle legal contracts, medical diagnosis, software development); yet measured GDP productivity growth has not accelerated.

**Historical parallel — the Solow Paradox (1987)**: "You can see the computer age everywhere except in the productivity statistics." IT investment in the 1980s–90s produced no measurable productivity improvement until 1995–2005, when complementary investments (network effects, organizational redesign, skill upgrading) catalyzed the breakthrough. Total TFP growth in 1995–2005: 1.0–1.5% annually vs. 0.3–0.5% before and after.

**Why AI hasn't hit GDP yet (2026)**:
1. **Deployment lag**: AI tools require 2–5 years for organizational adoption and complementary investment
2. **Measurement gaps**: GDP doesn't capture quality improvements well; time-savings that don't reduce prices aren't counted
3. **Complementary investments**: require new human skills, reorganized workflows, regulatory adaptation
4. **Distribution**: AI benefits are concentrated in high-skilled workers and capital owners; don't yet show in aggregate statistics

**Near-term outlook**: IMF "AI growth premium" scenario (2027–2030): if AI drives a 0.5–1.0% annual TFP improvement, global growth could reaccelerate to 3.8–4.5% — reversing the secular stagnation trend. The scenarios are wide because AI adoption rates across countries and sectors are highly uncertain.

### Key Risks and Scenarios
**Downside scenarios** (recession triggers):
1. **US tariff escalation**: 25% tariffs on all imports (campaign proposal partially implemented) would reduce US growth by 1.5–2.0%, Europe by 0.5–1.0%, and trigger retaliatory cycles
2. **Oil price spike**: Strait of Hormuz disruption (US-Iran conflict escalation) would add $30–50/barrel to oil prices, adding 0.5–1.5% to global inflation and reducing growth by 0.5–1.0%
3. **China financial stress**: Property sector losses have been absorbed at corporate level; a banking system crystallization of losses would require a $1.5–3T government backstop and reduce Chinese growth to 2–3%
4. **Credit market shock**: Leveraged loan defaults rising as floating-rate debt service increases; CLO stress could trigger credit spreads widening of 200+ bps, tightening financial conditions sharply

**Upside scenarios**:
1. **AI productivity breakthrough**: Earlier-than-expected TFP improvement adds 0.5–1.0% to global growth
2. **Geopolitical de-escalation**: Ukraine ceasefire + NATO-Russia de-escalation reduces defense spending premium, lowers European energy costs, and allows partial reconstruction of supply chains
3. **Chinese stimulus**: Large-scale household stimulus (consumption vouchers, healthcare expansion) could close the consumption gap and add 0.5–1.0% to global growth via trade channels

### Update — June 2, 2026

**IMF Spring Meetings 2026 explicit scenario matrix (IMF WEO April 2026, Press Briefing, April 14):** The IMF's April 2026 Spring Meetings press briefing made the scenario analysis explicit in a way the headline numbers don't capture. The three-scenario matrix:

| Scenario | 2026 Global Growth | 2026 Inflation | Trigger Condition |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reference** | 3.1% | 4.4% | Current policy baseline |
| **Adverse** | 2.5% | 5.4% | Sustained Hormuz disruption, US tariff escalation |
| **Severe** | 2.0% | >6.0% | Adverse + financial market stress, credit shock |

The reference forecast of 3.1% is described by the IMF as already incorporating significant geopolitical drag; the adverse and severe scenarios illustrate how quickly the global economy could approach or fall into recession territory. The 2.0% severe scenario is effectively a global recession (the IMF defines recession as <2.5% for the world economy).

**Iran negotiations collapse on June 1 — downside risk materializes:** The June 1–2 collapse of US-Iran MOU negotiations (Iran stopping exchanges with US and threatening complete Hormuz closure) directly activates the IMF's "adverse scenario" pathway. If Iran follows through on its June 1 threat to "completely block" the Strait and activate "other fronts including Bab al-Mandeb," the combined effect would remove approximately 20–25% of global seaborne oil trade — a shock with no modern precedent. Brent crude's 4.1% rise on June 2 to $99.44 is consistent with markets beginning to price a transition toward the IMF's adverse scenario.

**IMF blog: "War Darkens Global Economic Outlook" (April 14, 2026):** The IMF's official blog, posted on the day of the Spring Meetings, used the strongest language in the Fund's public communications: "War Darkens Global Economic Outlook and Reshapes Policy Priorities." The "reshapes policy priorities" component is significant — it signals that the IMF is recommending governments prioritize defense industrial investment, energy resilience, and fiscal buffers over the pre-2022 consensus framework of low-deficit, low-inflation normalization. This represents a genuine shift in the IMF's institutional advice: fiscal austerity as a response to economic stress is being replaced by a "wartime fiscal framework" that accepts higher deficits for defense, energy, and reconstruction spending.

**94-day Hormuz paralysis effect on Q1–Q2 2026 GDP now being quantified:** As the Hormuz closure enters its 94th day (June 2), economic models are beginning to capture the cumulative supply-chain disruption. The primary channel: US and European industrial production that relies on Gulf region petrochemicals (plastics, fertilizers, chemicals) is seeing input shortages as LNG and oil product flows from the Gulf region have been severely reduced. These secondary industrial effects — which are slower to materialize than headline energy prices but are more persistent — are what the IMF's adverse scenario models, and are the mechanism by which the Hormuz crisis translates from an energy market shock into a broader industrial recession risk.

### Update — June 3, 2026

**IMF April 2026 full projections now documented: global inflation 4.4%, Middle East at 1.9% growth:** The IMF April 2026 WEO's complete quantitative framework, now available through the official IMF publication and Al Jazeera reporting, shows: global inflation revised to **4.4%** (up 0.6 percentage points from January's 3.8% forecast), directly attributable to energy price pass-through from the 94-day Hormuz closure. The Middle East and Central Asia region faces the most severe growth revision: 2026 forecast cut to **1.9%** — a 2 percentage-point reduction — as oil-exporting economies paradoxically suffer from the geopolitical instability they helped create (reduced transit revenue, infrastructure damage, investor flight). The IMF's title "Global Economy in the Shadow of War" and the IMF blog's explicit "War Darkens Global Economic Outlook" language represent the Fund's strongest institutional acknowledgment of geopolitical causation since the post-2022 Russia-Ukraine forecasts.

**Trump's "next week" Iran deal announcement creates binary market scenario:** Trump's June 2 statement that an Iran deal was expected "over the next week" triggered immediate Brent crude volatility (a ~$3/barrel decline followed by partial recovery as Iran disputed the terms). The market's hypersensitivity to deal signals reflects the binary payoff embedded in current prices: a Hormuz reopening deal would release approximately $20–25/barrel of geopolitical risk premium from current Brent prices ($95–99 range), while deal failure would maintain the supply shock indefinitely. This binary structure — large upside price movement in either direction depending on a single diplomatic event — is itself a drag on investment decision-making across all energy-intensive industries, as capital allocation is frozen pending resolution. The IMF's adverse scenario models specifically include "prolonged conflict" as the trigger for global growth below 2.5% recession threshold.

**World Bank June 2026 Global Economic Prospects — further developing economy downward revisions expected:** The World Bank's June 2026 Global Economic Prospects report (scheduled for publication this week) will incorporate cumulative Hormuz disruption data and is expected to show significant additional downward revisions for developing economies. The "triple squeeze" mechanism — higher energy import costs + dollar strength (geopolitical risk-off premium) + reduced remittances from slowing advanced economies — creates simultaneous pressure on fiscal accounts, current accounts, and household purchasing power in low-income nations. Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia are the most exposed regions. The World Bank's January 2026 baseline already projected the weakest developing economy performance since the 2020 pandemic; the geopolitical shocks since January have made that baseline look optimistic.

### Saturday Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis

**Behavioral Economics and the 2026 Slowdown Psychology:**
The 2026 global slowdown is not merely a technical economic phenomenon — it is a behavioral one. John Maynard Keynes famously attributed investment slumps to "animal spirits" — the collapse of entrepreneur confidence that triggers self-fulfilling recessions even when fundamentals don't warrant them. The 2026 pattern exhibits classic animal spirits dynamics: trade policy uncertainty (escalating US tariffs, retaliatory measures from the EU and China) has elevated business investment uncertainty to post-2020 highs on the Federal Reserve's survey-based uncertainty indices, producing a "wait and see" investment pause that reduces growth even before any structural damage materializes. Robert Shiller's CAPE ratio analysis suggests equity valuations (US CAPE ~32 at end-2025) remain historically elevated relative to underlying earnings growth — creating vulnerability to narrative-driven corrections. Prospect theory predicts that firms facing uncertain tariff environments will over-weight potential losses relative to potential gains in their investment calculus, producing sub-optimal investment allocation. The behavioral framing has policy implications: central banks targeting credible inflation expectations and governments providing regulatory stability can partially substitute for the absent animal spirits, while policy unpredictability actively destroys confidence independent of fundamentals.

**Complexity Theory and Synchronized Deceleration:**
The synchronization of the 2026 slowdown across economies with very different domestic fundamentals (US near full employment, Germany in technical recession, China managing property sector deflation, India growing at 6%+) defies simple monocausal explanation. Complexity economics offers the more compelling framework: modern economies are deeply interconnected through trade networks, financial flows, and information systems, producing synchronized dynamics that emerge from network-level interactions rather than from any single country's fundamentals. Doyne Farmer's (Oxford) complexity models of supply chains demonstrate that shocks to highly connected nodes (US tariff policy affecting global supply chain coordination; China property sector affecting cement, steel, and copper demand globally) propagate through network links in ways that standard DSGE macroeconomic models — which treat national economies as largely closed systems with external trade as an add-on — systematically underestimate. The "bullwhip effect" (demand signal amplification through supply chains) means that modest consumer demand deceleration at end nodes produces inventory buildups and capacity under-utilization throughout the chain — visible in 2025–2026 manufacturing PMIs falling into contraction territory across Asia even while US consumer spending remained positive.

**Geopolitical Finance and the Reshoring Cost:**
The 2026 slowdown has a supply-side component that is analytically distinct from demand-side weakness: the reshoring/friendshoring transition is simultaneously raising productive capacity in new locations (US, India, Mexico) while stranding productive capacity in old locations (China's electronics export sector, which is running at 60–70% capacity utilization in 2026). This is a productive capital destruction event — analogous to Schumpeterian creative destruction but at the pace of policy mandates rather than market competition. The IMF has estimated the long-run efficiency cost of full trade fragmentation at 5–7% of global GDP; the 2026 partial fragmentation is imposing transitional costs estimated at 0.5–1.5% of global GDP annually as supply chains are rebuilt at higher cost in non-optimal locations. The financial sector is absorbing this as equity repricing in exposed sectors (Chinese electronics exporters' price-to-book ratios at 30-year lows; US industrial stocks elevated on reshoring optionality premium) and credit spread widening for EM economies dependent on trade-exposed sectors.

### Update — June 8, 2026: IMF "Shadow of War" Chapter Findings, Severe Scenario at 2% Growth, and the Trade Truce's Limited Relief

**IMF April 2026 Chapter 1 — "Global Economy Tested Again":**
The IMF's April 2026 World Economic Outlook Chapter 1, titled "Global Economy Tested Again," provides the most authoritative current assessment of the economic headwinds. The headline finding: assuming the US-Iran conflict "remains limited in duration and scope" (the reference scenario), global growth falls to **3.1%** in 2026 with headline inflation at **4.4%** — a sharp deviation from the global disinflation trend of 2023–2025. The IMF identifies the energy price shock (a "moderate 19% increase in energy commodities prices in 2026") as the primary transmission mechanism. Critically, the IMF outlines three scenarios with dramatically different outcomes: the **reference scenario** (3.1% growth) assumes the conflict stays contained; the **adverse scenario** (2.5% growth) assumes prolonged conflict keeping oil at ~$100/barrel; the **severe scenario** (2.0% growth — effectively a global recession) assumes extended conflict, much higher oil prices, and "major financial market dislocations." The Commonwealth Bank analysis of the IMF's scenarios describes this as the IMF "warning of potential global recession" — the first such warning since the 2020 pandemic. The 2% severe scenario is below the 2.5% threshold typically used to define global recession.

**The Trade Truce's Limited Economic Relief:**
The May 12, 2026 US-China trade truce (reducing US tariffs from ~145% to ~30%, and Chinese tariffs from ~60–80% to ~10%) provides meaningful but incomplete economic relief. The PIIE analysis identifies why: US-China bilateral trade at ~145% tariffs had collapsed to roughly 40% of its 2018 volume — the trade war had already done most of its damage. The truce partially reverses the most acute tariff-driven supply chain disruption, but the structural semiconductor technology decoupling (export controls remain fully in force), the supply chain diversification investment already made (Apple's India production ramp, etc.), and the 90-day expiration clock mean that the relief is temporary and conditional. Goldman Sachs revised its US growth forecast upward 0.2 percentage points on the trade truce announcement — modest relief relative to the 0.8-point downward revision from the Hormuz energy shock. The net economic position: slightly better than the worst-case tariff-war-plus-energy-shock scenario, but still materially worse than the January 2026 baseline that assumed neither.

**IMF Spring Meetings — "War Darkens Global Economic Outlook" (April 14, 2026):**
IMF Managing Director Kristalina Georgieva's Spring Meetings commentary — "War Darkens Global Economic Outlook and Reshapes Policy Priorities" — identified three distinct fiscal policy challenges created by the multi-war environment: (1) defense spending increases (multiple NATO members raising to 3%+ of GDP) crowding out productive investment; (2) energy price shocks requiring both monetary response (inflation) and fiscal response (subsidies for most-affected households and industries) simultaneously; (3) trade fragmentation reducing fiscal revenues from tariff-exposed sectors while raising costs of import-dependent government programs. The Georgieva framing — "reshaping policy priorities" — is precise: governments are being forced to redirect spending from climate transition and social investment to defense and energy security, compressing the fiscal space for long-run structural investments precisely when the energy transition requires them most.

### Update — June 10, 2026: IMF "Global Economy in the Shadow of War," 3.1% Growth, and the Hormuz Inflation Premium

**IMF April 2026 World Economic Outlook: "Global Economy in the Shadow of War"**

The International Monetary Fund's April 2026 World Economic Outlook, titled "Global Economy in the Shadow of War," revised global growth projections to 3.1% for 2026 — down from the January 2026 update's 3.3% forecast and the lowest baseline projection since the COVID recovery period. The IMF's Chief Economist Pierre-Olivier Gourinchas characterized the revision as driven "overwhelmingly by the Hormuz disruption and its energy price pass-through," estimating that the US-Iran conflict alone subtracts 0.4-0.7 percentage points from global GDP relative to the pre-conflict baseline. The April headline — "War Darkens Global Economic Outlook" (IMF Blog, April 14) — is the bleakest framing the IMF has used since the 2020 "Great Lockdown" designation.

The IMF's scenario analysis is particularly alarming for its downside cases: a "prolonged disruption" scenario (Hormuz restricted for 9-12 months) projects global growth at 2.4-2.7% — below the 3% threshold the IMF historically identifies as a "global recession" proxy. Emerging market and developing economies are projected to experience the sharpest growth compression: sub-Saharan Africa (oil import-dependent, already stressed by food inflation) at 3.5% (down from 4.2% baseline); South Asia at 5.1% (resilient but down from 6.2%); Latin America at 1.8% (Venezuela transition costs contributing).

**Global Inflation: 4.4% Headline, The Hormuz Premium**

Global headline inflation is projected at 4.4% for 2026 — a significant re-acceleration from the 2024-2025 disinflation trend that had brought global inflation toward 3.5%. The Hormuz conflict adds approximately 1.5-2.0 percentage points to global inflation through three channels: direct energy price increases (Brent at $127/barrel vs. $82 pre-conflict), shipping cost pass-through across all tradeable goods (container rates up 340% on some routes since Suez Canal diversion increased demand), and commodity price knock-on effects (fertilizer, plastics, chemicals with petroleum inputs). Central banks face the same "stagflationary supply shock" challenge as the 2022 Russia-Ukraine energy crisis: raising rates to fight inflation risks choking already-slowing growth, while accommodating inflation allows expectations to de-anchor.

The Federal Reserve held rates at 4.75% at its May 2026 meeting, with Chair Powell's statement walking the "watch and assess" line. ECB maintained its 2.5% rate in June. Both institutions are modeling scenario branches: a conflict-resolution scenario where Hormuz disruption ends by September 2026 (base case, allowing disinflation to resume) versus a prolonged scenario requiring active rate hikes to prevent inflation re-entrenchment.

**Recession Probability at 30%: The Markets' Assessment**

Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan's June 2026 economic reports independently arrive at a 28-32% probability of global recession by Q4 2026 — defined as three consecutive quarters of below-trend growth for three or more G7 economies. This exceeds the 2019 pre-COVID elevated risk period (15-20%) but remains below 2022 Eurozone recession probability peaks (~45% for Europe alone). The key threshold variable: duration of Hormuz disruption. The Oman-mediated negotiation channel remains active but deadlocked on the core asymmetry — Iran demands US naval withdrawal from Gulf waters before any agreement, a condition the US characterizes as "surrendering freedom of navigation." Each month without resolution adds approximately 0.2% to 2026 global GDP loss.
