## Summary
In 2026, the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) and Uganda are battling a significant outbreak of Ebola disease caused by the **Bundibugyo virus** — a rarer strain than the Zaire ebolavirus responsible for past mega-outbreaks. As of late May 2026, over 1,000 suspected cases and 121 confirmed cases have been reported across three DRC provinces, with spread to Uganda's capital Kampala. The WHO declared the outbreak a **Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC)** on May 17, 2026, its highest alert level. The response is complicated by attacks on health workers, remote terrain, and active conflict zones.

## Key Points
- **Virus strain**: Bundibugyo virus (BDBVy) — one of 4 orthoebolavirus species; rarer than Zaire strain; no approved vaccine
- **Affected areas**: Ituri, Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu provinces (DRC); Kampala (Uganda)
- **Scale as of May 26, 2026**: 121 confirmed cases (17 deaths) + 1,077 suspected cases (238 deaths) in DRC; 7 confirmed cases (1 death) in Uganda
- **WHO PHEIC declared**: May 17, 2026 — only the second Ebola PHEIC after the 2018–2020 DRC outbreak
- **Key risk**: No approved vaccine or therapeutic for Bundibugyo strain; vaccines used in previous outbreaks (rVSV-ZEBOV) target Zaire strain only
- **Complication**: Attacks on health workers by local communities and armed groups hampering containment
- Up to 10 neighboring countries considered at high risk of further spread

## Details

### Background: Ebola and the Bundibugyo Strain

**Ebola virus disease (EVD)** is a severe and often fatal hemorrhagic fever caused by viruses in the *Orthoebolavirus* genus. There are four species known to cause disease in humans:
1. **Zaire ebolavirus** (EBOV) — responsible for the deadliest outbreaks, including the 2014-2016 West Africa epidemic (11,000+ deaths) and the 2018-2020 DRC outbreak
2. **Sudan ebolavirus** (SUDV) — caused multiple outbreaks in Uganda; used in the 2022 Uganda outbreak
3. **Bundibugyo ebolavirus** (BDBV) — first identified in Bundibugyo District, Uganda, in 2007; caused a second outbreak in DRC in 2012; case fatality rate historically ~25-35% (lower than Zaire's ~40-90%)
4. **Taï Forest ebolavirus** (TAFV) — only one documented human case

The Bundibugyo strain is considered particularly challenging because:
- **No approved vaccine or specific therapeutic exists for it** — the rVSV-ZEBOV vaccine (Ervebo) and mAb114/REGN-EB3 therapeutics approved after the 2018-2020 DRC outbreak are specific to the Zaire strain
- It tends to cause less dramatically severe symptoms than Zaire, potentially leading to more missed cases and silent transmission chains
- It is less extensively studied than the Zaire strain

### The 2026 Outbreak: Timeline and Geography

**Ituri Province, DRC** (initial epicenter):
- The outbreak was first detected in Ituri Province in early 2026, in remote forest areas where hunting of bushmeat creates human-animal interfaces
- Ituri has a history of Ebola exposure and is a historically conflict-affected region with limited healthcare infrastructure

**Spread to Nord-Kivu and Sud-Kivu**:
- The eastern DRC provinces of Nord-Kivu and Sud-Kivu are among the most conflict-affected areas on earth, hosting dozens of armed groups and millions of internally displaced persons (IDPs)
- Population movement along conflict-driven displacement routes facilitated spread
- The 2018-2020 Zaire strain outbreak also occurred in Nord-Kivu and lasted 22 months — largely because active conflict made containment operations nearly impossible

**Spread to Uganda (Kampala)**:
- As of May 24, 2026: 5 initial confirmed cases in Uganda's capital Kampala — a city of approximately 3.6 million people
- Total Uganda cases: 7 confirmed, 1 death
- The spread to a major urban center dramatically raises the stakes of containment
- Uganda and DRC share a long, porous border with heavy cross-border trade and movement

### WHO PHEIC Declaration (May 17, 2026)

The World Health Organization's Emergency Committee convened and declared the outbreak a **Public Health Emergency of International Concern** — only the WHO's second Ebola PHEIC (after the 2018-2020 DRC outbreak). PHEIC status:
- Unlocks emergency funding mechanisms and expedited response protocols
- Triggers international travel and trade advisories
- Obligates member states to strengthen surveillance and reporting at ports of entry
- Facilitates emergency use authorization of experimental countermeasures

The WHO's temporary recommendations include enhanced surveillance in all neighboring countries, community engagement programs, and expedited access for experimental vaccines targeting Bundibugyo strain (currently in clinical trial phase).

### Response Challenges

**1. No Approved Vaccine or Treatment**
Unlike the 2018-2020 Zaire outbreak (where rVSV-ZEBOV was deployed under emergency use), there is no licensed vaccine or proven therapeutic for Bundibugyo. Experimental candidates include:
- **ChAd3-BDBV**: An Oxford/NIAID chimpanzee adenovirus vector candidate in Phase 2 trials
- **BDBV-specific mAbs**: Monoclonal antibody candidates showing promise in NHP studies
The WHO and Gavi are working to expedite compassionate/emergency use access.

**2. Attacks on Health Workers**
NPR reported (May 26, 2026) that health workers conducting outbreak response in Ituri have faced attacks from local residents and armed groups. This mirrors a pattern seen in the 2018-2020 DRC outbreak, where misinformation, mistrust of government authorities, and active conflict led to violent attacks on Ebola treatment centers and vaccination teams — significantly prolonging that outbreak.

**3. Conflict Zones**
Nord-Kivu and Sud-Kivu are active conflict zones, with the M23 rebel group (backed by Rwanda, per UN reports) controlling parts of the region. Competing armed actors, humanitarian access restrictions, and displaced populations create ideal conditions for undetected transmission chains.

**4. Urban Spread Risk**
Kampala's confirmed cases represent a worst-case scenario in the making. Urban Ebola transmission (as seen in the 2014-2016 West Africa outbreak spreading to Monrovia, Freetown, Conakry) is dramatically harder to contain than rural transmission due to population density, informal healthcare seeking, and international airport connectivity.

### Regional Risk

The ECDC and CDC assess the following neighboring countries at **high risk** of further spread due to cross-border mobility and trade:
- Uganda (already affected), Tanzania, Burundi, Rwanda, South Sudan, Central African Republic, Republic of Congo, Zambia, Angola

The risk to North America and Europe is assessed as **low** but not negligible, particularly given Kampala's international airport (Entebbe International). Travel screening protocols have been activated.

### Why It Matters

1. **First Bundibugyo outbreak in 14 years**: The 2012 DRC outbreak was relatively contained; the 2026 outbreak is substantially larger and has already crossed international borders
2. **Vaccine gap exposed**: The world invested heavily in Zaire-specific countermeasures after 2014-2016; the emergence of a Bundibugyo outbreak reveals the vulnerability of strain-specific vaccine strategies
3. **DRC as perpetual epicenter**: The DRC has experienced more Ebola outbreaks than any other country — structural issues (conflict, weak healthcare infrastructure, poverty) mean the country will continue to be a source of zoonotic spillover events
4. **Global health architecture test**: This is an early test of the post-COVID pandemic preparedness reforms (WHO pandemic treaty, 100 Days Mission) — whether the world can respond faster than in previous outbreaks
