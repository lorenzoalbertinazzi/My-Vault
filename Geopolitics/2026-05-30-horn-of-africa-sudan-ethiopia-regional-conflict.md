## Summary
Sudan's civil war, now entering its fourth year (began April 2023), has become one of the world's worst humanitarian catastrophes — with 150,000+ dead, 10 million displaced, and a country effectively partitioned between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF). In 2026, the conflict is metastasizing into a regional crisis as foreign powers — the UAE, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and Ethiopia — become increasingly entangled, raising the specter of a Horn of Africa "regional mega-war" that could redraw borders and destabilize the Red Sea trade corridor. The conflict is simultaneously a Sudanese civil war, a Gulf proxy war, a Nile water dispute, and a competition for Red Sea access.

## Key Points
- **Sudan de facto partitioned**: SAF controls the east (Khartoum, Kassala, Port Sudan); RSF controls Darfur and parts of Kordofan; contested zones in the center
- **UAE vs. Saudi/Egypt proxy dimension**: UAE has backed the RSF (weapons through Chad, Libya, UAE airlifts); Egypt and Saudi Arabia broadly aligned with SAF; this is partly a competition for Red Sea and Horn of Africa influence
- **Ethiopia entanglement**: Foreign fighters of apparent Tigrayan origin captured fighting alongside RSF in Blue Nile state (April 2026); Ethiopia's own stability deteriorating under PM Abiy Ahmed's ethnic tensions
- **Red Sea stakes**: Port Sudan is Sudan's only deep-water port — critical for SAF survival and global grain/commodity flows; RSF control of the port would threaten Red Sea shipping security
- **Humanitarian catastrophe**: 24 million Sudanese face acute food insecurity (UN OCHA, 2026); famine declared in multiple states; largest internal displacement crisis in the world

### Origins of the Conflict
Sudan's civil war erupted on April 15, 2023, when tensions between the SAF (under General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan) and the RSF (under General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo, known as "Hemeti") exploded into open warfare in Khartoum. Both forces had jointly staged the October 2021 coup against Sudan's civilian transitional government — a joint action that collapsed once power had been seized.

**The RSF's origins**: The RSF grew directly from the Janjaweed militias responsible for the Darfur genocide (2003–2008). Hemeti transformed them from armed bandits into a formalized 70,000–100,000-strong paramilitary force under his personal command, enriched through gold mining (Sudan is Africa's third-largest gold producer), UAE arms contracts, and mercenary deployments to Yemen (fighting for the UAE-Saudi coalition).

**The conflict structure**: Unlike a classic insurgency, Sudan's war is a direct military confrontation between two large conventional forces. The RSF's strength is dispersed, mobile infantry; the SAF has air power but weak ground forces. The RSF seized most of Khartoum and Omdurman in 2023, causing mass flight; the SAF retains the east. A stalemate has prevailed since late 2023 despite ongoing combat.

### The Foreign Proxy Dimension

**UAE's role**: The UAE has provided the RSF with weapons, logistics support, and financing. UAE-linked transport aircraft have delivered supplies through Chadian and Libyan airstrips; the UAE's Ports Corporation controls port assets in eastern Sudan. The UAE's motivation: access to Sudanese agriculture and gold, Red Sea port positions, and countering Egyptian/Saudi influence in the region. The Biden and Trump administrations publicly criticized UAE for arming RSF but imposed no sanctions.

**Egypt's alignment**: Egypt has openly backed the SAF, providing air defense systems, ammunition, and diplomatic support. Egypt's core interest is the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) — Ethiopia's massive Nile dam project that Egypt views as an existential water security threat. Egypt and Sudan share anxiety about GERD reducing Nile flows; keeping Sudan aligned with Cairo is essential to Egypt's Nile strategy.

**Saudi Arabia's position**: Saudi Arabia initially attempted mediation (Jeddah talks, 2023) alongside the US, which collapsed after both sides violated ceasefires repeatedly. Saudi Arabia prefers a negotiated outcome but leans toward SAF legitimacy.

**Ethiopia's entanglement**: PM Abiy Ahmed won the 2019 Nobel Peace Prize for the Eritrea peace deal but subsequently led a devastating civil war against the Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF) in 2020–2022 (estimated 300,000–500,000 dead). In 2026, evidence emerged of TPLF-linked fighters crossing into Sudan's Blue Nile state to fight alongside the RSF — suggesting the UAE's weapons supply chain runs partly through Ethiopian-Tigrayan networks. Ethiopia has demanded access to the Red Sea port of Assab (Eritrea) as a national security imperative, creating tensions with Eritrea (which backs SAF).

### Red Sea and Horn of Africa Geopolitics
The Horn of Africa is one of the world's most strategically important regions:
- **Bab al-Mandab Strait**: bottleneck through which ~12% of global trade passes (including European-Asian shipping). Houthi attacks from Yemen demonstrated the strait's vulnerability in 2023–24
- **Port Sudan**: the SAF's economic lifeline; loss to RSF would cut government revenues and potentially trigger collapse
- **UAE's base network**: UAE operates military bases in Eritrea (Assab), Somalia (Berbera agreement with Somaliland), and until recently in Djibouti — a forward presence surrounding the Horn
- **Chinese presence**: China operates its only overseas military base in Djibouti (established 2017); has major Belt and Road port investments in the region

**GERD escalation risk**: Ethiopia's Grand Renaissance Dam (on the Blue Nile) has been filling since 2020. When complete (~2027), it will hold 74 billion cubic meters — the largest hydroelectric dam in Africa. Egypt has repeatedly threatened military action to prevent the dam from reducing Nile flows below Egypt's "red lines." Sudan, downstream of Ethiopia and upstream of Egypt, is caught in the middle.

### Humanitarian Dimensions
Sudan 2026 represents a convergence of multiple crises:
- **Famine**: UN declared famine in North Darfur's Zamzam camp (April 2024) and multiple additional areas through 2025–26. RSF blockade of aid routes is a primary cause; aid organizations have been denied access or attacked
- **Mass displacement**: 10 million internally displaced, 1.5 million fled to Chad, Egypt, and South Sudan — the world's largest displacement crisis
- **Healthcare system collapse**: 80% of hospitals in conflict zones non-functional
- **Sexual violence**: UN reports widespread RSF-perpetrated sexual violence as a weapon of war, consistent with RSF/Janjaweed pattern from Darfur

International response has been inadequate: Sudan receives far less international aid per displaced person than Ukraine or Gaza, a disparity attributed to African conflicts' lower media attention in Western countries ("forgotten crisis" syndrome).

### Scenarios and Outlook (2026)
**Continued stalemate (most likely)**: Neither side can achieve decisive military victory; foreign arms supplies sustain both. Sudan remains functionally partitioned for years. Humanitarian situation worsens.

**SAF offensive success**: Egyptian air support + SAF consolidation allows recapture of Khartoum and Omdurman. RSF retreats to Darfur but holds it. Negotiated partition similar to South Sudan (2011) is possible long-term outcome.

**Regional escalation (growing risk)**: If Ethiopia-Tigray networks fighting in Blue Nile state are confirmed and escalate, Addis Ababa and Khartoum may openly confront each other. UAE-backed Ethiopia vs. Egypt-backed SAF would draw in Eritrea and potentially trigger full regional war — a scenario the Crisis Group's 2026 brief specifically identified as the highest-consequence risk.

**Port Sudan fall (tail risk)**: RSF control of Sudan's Red Sea coast would threaten maritime security, potentially triggering direct Egyptian military intervention and activating US/Saudi concern over Bab al-Mandab security.

### Update — June 1, 2026

**SAF retakes Khartoum — government returns from Port Sudan (major development, mid-2026):** In one of the most significant developments of the entire war, the Sudanese Armed Forces completed their reconquest of Khartoum — the capital — approximately one year after the war began. The Central Bank of Sudan announced the resumption of operations in Khartoum for the first time since April 2023. The SAF-aligned government declared its "complete return" to Khartoum, having operated from Port Sudan (on the Red Sea coast) as a temporary capital throughout the conflict. This represents a major symbolic and operational victory for the SAF, reversing the humiliating early loss of the capital. However, fighting continues in other regions.

**RSF counterattacks in Darfur and Kordofan:** Despite the SAF's Khartoum success, the RSF retook **Bara** and **Karnoi in North Darfur** (near the Chad border) in recent weeks — demonstrating that the war is far from over and that RSF remains capable of significant offensive operations in its western strongholds. The SAF repelled a large-scale RSF attack on **Wadi al-Hout in North Kordofan**, and RSF forces have encircled the city of **El Obeid in North Kordofan**, striking civilian targets including a kindergarten.

**UN fact-finding: "hallmarks of genocide" in Darfur:** A UN fact-finding mission issued a formal assessment identifying **"hallmarks of genocide"** in RSF-controlled Darfur — escalating the legal language beyond "ethnic cleansing" concerns. This assessment, citing systematic targeting of non-Arab populations, will form the basis for potential ICC referrals. The Darfur genocide of 2003–2008 (involving the RSF's predecessor Janjaweed) is being explicitly invoked by UN investigators as a structural parallel.

**Humanitarian funding collapse:** The UN Population Fund (UNFPA) reported that recent cuts to international aid funding have forced it to **withdraw from more than half of the 93 health facilities** it was supporting across Sudan. Total international humanitarian response funding stands at only **40% of requirements** for 2026 — leaving millions without adequate food, medical care, or shelter. This funding shortfall, driven partly by donor fatigue and the competition of other global crises (Ukraine, Gaza, Iran energy shock), is the single greatest accelerant of Sudan's humanitarian catastrophe.

**South Sudanese fighters captured with RSF (January 2026):** Sudan sources confirmed to Al Jazeera that South Sudanese fighters were among captured RSF members in Blue Nile state — extending the earlier evidence of foreign fighters (Tigrayan fighters) fighting alongside the RSF. The pattern of multiple nationalities fighting for the RSF suggests its force structure is now sustained by foreign recruitment beyond Darfur's local population, raising the conflict's regional entanglement. South Sudan's own fragile state, dependent on Sudan for oil pipeline access, is now directly implicated in the conflict.

## Cross-Disciplinary Connections

### Commodity Markets and the Sudan-Red Sea Nexus

Sudan's civil war, and particularly the risk of RSF control over Port Sudan and the Red Sea coast, connects directly to global commodity markets through two channels. First, the Bab al-Mandab Strait — through which approximately 12% of global trade passes — is already stressed by Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping (an Iranian proxy operation whose future is intertwined with the US-Iran MOU negotiations). A second destabilization node in the Horn of Africa would compound insurance risk on the entire east-African shipping corridor, raising shipping costs from Asia to Europe that are already elevated from the Yemen disruption. Second, Sudan itself is a significant agricultural exporter: sesame, gum arabic (used in candy, pharmaceuticals, and ink), and sorghum are exported primarily through Port Sudan. RSF control of the port would not only cut SAF government revenues but disrupt agricultural commodity flows that affect global food price indices — an understated market risk from a conflict that receives insufficient commodity market attention precisely because its primary victims are African.

### The Humanitarian Funding Crisis as a Systemic Risk

The collapse of Sudan's international humanitarian response — currently funded at only 40% of requirements — is a textbook illustration of what economists call "attention scarcity" in global governance: institutional donors (USAID, EU humanitarian office, UK FCDO) have finite budgets and finite political bandwidth, and in 2026 that bandwidth is saturated by Ukraine, Gaza, Iran, and the Ebola outbreak simultaneously. The 24 million Sudanese in acute food insecurity receive less per-capita international response than Ukrainian or Gazan civilians — a disparity that reflects the brutal political economy of humanitarian response, where media coverage, diaspora lobbying, and donor-country national interest drive resource allocation more than the scale of suffering. From a social psychology perspective, the psychic numbing effect documented by Paul Slovic is operating at institutional scale: each additional crisis added to the global portfolio reduces the marginal political energy devoted to each individual one, creating a systematic under-response to African conflicts that compounds their severity.
