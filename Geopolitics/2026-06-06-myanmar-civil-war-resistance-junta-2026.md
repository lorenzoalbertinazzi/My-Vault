## Summary
Myanmar's civil war, now entering its fifth year since the February 2021 military coup, has reached a decisive inflection point: the State Administration Council (SAC) junta controls fewer than 40% of Myanmar's townships and perhaps 21% of the country's territory — a historically unprecedented reversal of military supremacy in a country the Tatmadaw has dominated since 1962. The formation of the Steering Council for the Emergence of a Federal Democratic Union (SCEF) in March 2026 represents the most unified resistance coalition in Myanmar's post-independence history, bringing together ethnic armed organizations (EAOs) and the shadow National Unity Government (NUG) under a common political and military framework. Yet the international community has largely failed to respond to what is, by most metrics, one of the world's most serious ongoing humanitarian catastrophes.

## Key Points
- SAC junta controls only ~21% of territory; resistance forces control ~42%; contested zones cover the remainder
- SCEF formed March 30, 2026: unified coalition of NUG, KIO, KNU, CNF, KNPP — the most significant resistance consolidation since the coup
- 5.2 million displaced persons; 15,000+ killed in 2025 alone; aerial bombardment rose to 3,300+ incidents in 2025–2026
- Min Aung Hlaing staged a cosmetic "civilian" transition in April 2026 — installing himself as president in sham elections
- China backs the junta for border stability and economic interests; Russia provides arms; India maintains pragmatic neutrality
- ASEAN's "Five-Point Consensus" (2021) has completely failed; Myanmar effectively suspended from ASEAN participation since 2022
- The conflict has produced a "fragmented sovereignty" — no actor controls enough territory to govern Myanmar nationally

## Details

### Background and Root Causes

**Myanmar's Political History:** Burma gained independence from Britain in 1948 and immediately entered armed conflict with multiple ethnic minorities who had been promised autonomy under the Panglong Agreement (1947) signed with Aung San — who was assassinated before he could implement it. The Karen National Union (KNU, est. 1947) began armed resistance in 1949, making it one of the world's longest-running insurgencies.

**The Tatmadaw's Grip:** Ne Win's 1962 military coup established military dominance that persisted, in varying forms, for 60 years. The 2008 Constitution (drafted by the military) reserved 25% of parliamentary seats for the military and gave the military commander-in-chief control of key ministries (Defense, Home, Border Affairs). This constitutionally entrenched military power was the irreducible source of political tension.

**The "Democratic Decade" (2010–2021):** A managed transition from military rule allowed Aung San Suu Kyi's National League for Democracy (NLD) to govern from 2016. International sanctions were lifted; Myanmar attracted $7 billion in FDI annually. The genocide of the Rohingya Muslim minority (2016–2017), executed by the Tatmadaw with Suu Kyi's government refusing to acknowledge, complicated the democratic narrative.

**February 1, 2021 — The Coup:** Senior General Min Aung Hlaing, facing the prospect of prosecution under an elected government for the Rohingya genocide, staged a coup hours before the newly elected parliament was to be sworn in. He declared a year-long state of emergency (repeatedly extended), detained Aung San Suu Kyi (sentenced to 27 years on fabricated charges), and abolished the election results.

**The People's Defense Force (PDF) and "Spring Revolution":** Unlike the 1988 student protests (crushed with mass killings) or the 2007 Saffron Revolution (monks, crushed with minimal international response), the 2021 resistance was militarized from the outset. Young protesters — many of whom fled cities to ethnic-controlled territories — trained with ethnic armed organizations and formed the PDF under NUG command. For the first time in Myanmar's history, ethnically Bamar resistance fighters joined forces with ethnic minority armies in systematic military operations against the Tatmadaw.

### Timeline of the Civil War: Key Military Phases

**2021 (Phase 1 — Urban Crackdown and PDF Formation):**
- Protests suppressed with live ammunition; ~800 civilians killed in the coup's first year
- Junta declared the NUG a "terrorist organization"
- KNU, KIO, and other EAOs formally announced support for the NUG
- PDF formed (May 2021); fragmented across hundreds of local "People's Defense Forces"

**2022–2023 (Phase 2 — Territorial Attrition):**
- Resistance forces took control of large swaths of Kayah (Karenni), Chin, and Kachin states
- Junta increasingly relied on airstrikes and drone attacks on civilian infrastructure
- "Scorched earth" tactics: burning villages, mass atrocities documented by UN Special Rapporteur
- Operation 1027 (October 27, 2023): Three Brotherhood Alliance (3BHA) — MNDAA, TNLA, AA — launched coordinated offensive in Shan State. Within weeks, captured 70+ SAC military bases, dozens of towns, and shut down key China-Myanmar border trade routes
- China brokered an uneasy ceasefire (January 2024) protecting its border trade interests; fighting continued elsewhere

**2024 (Phase 3 — Junta's Multi-Front Collapse):**
- Rakhine State: Arakan Army (AA) captured nearly all of Rakhine State by mid-2024, including Maungdaw (site of 2017 Rohingya genocide) and Sittwe (the state capital, under siege)
- Northern Shan: MNDAA controlled the Kokang region and Lashio (the largest city in northern Shan, a major strategic and economic hub)
- Sagaing Region: NUG-affiliated PDF controlled hundreds of villages
- Total township control: Resistance held 60+ townships outright; junta controlled fewer than half of Myanmar's 330 townships
- Air campaign intensified: airstrike frequency increased 4x from 2023; civilian deaths from airstrikes exceeded 3,800 total

**2025 (Phase 4 — Humanitarian Catastrophe and Continued Junta Retreat):**
- 5.2 million internally displaced persons — one of the world's largest IDP crises
- Food insecurity: 13.3 million people food insecure (UN WFP estimate)
- Economy collapsed: kyat lost ~70% of value since 2021; foreign reserves near zero; GDP contracted ~18% since coup
- Mandalay: heavy fighting in Myanmar's second-largest city; first time urban combat reached Mandalay in the conflict
- SAC airstrike on NUG-held Dagon Myothit market: killed 100+ civilians — one of the deadliest single incidents of the war

### Key Actors

**State Administration Council (SAC) / Myanmar Military (Tatmadaw):**
- Commander-in-Chief: Senior General Min Aung Hlaing (self-appointed president, April 2026)
- Strength: ~150,000–200,000 effective troops (reduced from 350,000 pre-coup due to desertions and battlefield casualties)
- Backed by: China (pragmatic economic support and border stability), Russia (weapons including aircraft, artillery), North Korea (munitions reportedly)
- Key weakness: loss of Bamar ethnic nationalist legitimacy (historically the Tatmadaw's bedrock support base)

**National Unity Government (NUG):**
- Shadow government formed by elected parliamentarians and civil society (April 2021)
- Prime Minister: Mahn Win Khaing Than (Karen politician); President: Duwa Lashi La (Kachin)
- Recognized by: no UN member state as Myanmar's government; has diplomatic contact with US, EU, UK
- Controls People's Defense Force (PDF), coordinates with EAOs

**Ethnic Armed Organizations (EAOs):**
- **KNU (Karen National Union):** Oldest insurgency; controls large areas of Karen/Kayin State along Thai border; ~5,000 fighters
- **KIO (Kachin Independence Organization):** Controls significant portions of Kachin State bordering China and India; ~10,000 fighters
- **AA (Arakan Army):** Rapidly emerged as the dominant force; now controls ~80% of Rakhine State; ~30,000 fighters; not part of SCEF but aligned with NUG
- **MNDAA (Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army):** Controls Kokang region; close ties to China
- **TNLA (Ta'ang National Liberation Army):** Northern Shan State; part of Three Brotherhood Alliance
- **CNF, KNPP:** Chin National Front and Karenni National Progressive Party — SCEF members

**SCEF (Steering Council for the Emergence of a Federal Democratic Union):**
- Formed: March 30, 2026
- Composition: NUG + KIO + KNU + CNF + KNPP
- Objectives: End military rule, draft federal democratic constitution, establish federal union
- Missing: AA (significant absence — controls much of Rakhine but has complex relationship with China and NUG)
- The SCEF represents the most substantial political unification of Myanmar's resistance since independence

### Background and Root Causes

**China's Position — The Decisive External Actor:**
China shares a 2,185 km border with Myanmar and is its largest trading partner and arms supplier. Beijing's Myanmar policy balances several competing interests:
- Border security and stability: armed conflict destabilizes Yunnan Province
- Economic interests: China-Myanmar Economic Corridor (CMEC), Kyaukphyu deep-sea port (access to Indian Ocean, critical for bypassing the Malacca Strait)
- Anti-drug operations: Shan State was the world's largest heroin producer; scam compound industry (largely targeting Chinese citizens) grew explosively in resistance-controlled zones
- BRI leverage: Myanmar is central to China's Indian Ocean access strategy

China's response to the coup was ambivalent: it blocked UNSC condemnation, supplied arms, but pressured the junta on the scam compound crackdown and brokered the Shan State ceasefire. Beijing works with both the SAC and major EAOs, using leverage over all parties to preserve economic interests.

**India's Pragmatic Neutrality:**
India shares a 1,643 km border with Myanmar and had significant infrastructure investments. India has maintained diplomatic relations with the SAC while also maintaining contact with NUG. Indian concerns: Hindu nationalist politics in Manipur and Mizoram are inflamed by Chin and other refugees crossing the border; Northeast India's security is affected by armed groups using Myanmar territory.

**ASEAN's Failure:**
The Five-Point Consensus (April 2021) called for: cessation of violence, constructive dialogue, ASEAN Special Envoy access, humanitarian assistance delivery, and diplomatic engagement. None of these points has been implemented. Myanmar has been excluded from ASEAN leaders' meetings since 2022. The consensus-based ASEAN structure prevented any binding response. Indonesia (2023 Chair) and Laos (2024 Chair) made no material progress.

### Current Status (June 2026)

**Min Aung Hlaing's Cosmetic "Civilian Transition":**
Facing the prospect of total military collapse, the SAC staged elections on January 4, 2026 — widely condemned as fraudulent by international observers, NUG, and most resistance organizations. A military-backed party won a sham majority. In April 2026, Min Aung Hlaing removed his military uniform and assumed the presidency as "civilian leader" — a thinly veiled attempt to claim legitimacy and possibly reduce international isolation. Analysts from Crisis Group, The Diplomat, and FDD (Foundation for Defense of Democracies) assessed this as "military consolidation, not transition."

**Ceasefire of April 2–22, 2026:**
The SAC announced a ceasefire coinciding with the Thingyan (water festival) period. The ceasefire was violated repeatedly — documented airstrikes occurred throughout. The resistance largely continued operations, viewing the ceasefire as a tactical pause to consolidate junta forces.

**Ongoing Humanitarian Crisis (June 2026 estimates):**
- Displaced: 5.2 million (UNHCR)
- Food insecure: 13.3 million
- Children out of school due to conflict: ~7 million
- Total killed since February 2021: 50,000–100,000 (estimates vary; junta does not release data; AAPP Burma tracks reported deaths)
- Airstrike incidents since February 2021: ~9,400

### Implications

**For Myanmar's Future:** The SCEF represents the most credible alternative governance framework in Myanmar's history. If resistance forces continue territorial gains and the SCEF develops effective governance institutions, Myanmar could transition toward a genuine federal democratic union — the aspiration of the Panglong process since 1947. However, significant challenges remain: SCEF member EAOs have historical conflicts with each other; economic governance is rudimentary; and the absence of the Arakan Army from SCEF creates a major gap in western Myanmar.

**For Regional Security:** Myanmar has become a narco-state in conflict-affected regions. Methamphetamine production has increased dramatically — "Yaba" and crystal meth flooding into Thailand, China, India, and Australia. Scam compound operations (targeting Chinese, Taiwanese, Southeast Asian citizens in forced-labor telecom fraud operations) have become a regional criminal economy worth billions.

**For ASEAN Centrality:** Myanmar's crisis represents the greatest test of ASEAN's relevance and unity. The total failure of the Five-Point Consensus, combined with member state divisions (Thailand maintains junta links; Malaysia and Indonesia have been more critical), has severely damaged ASEAN's credibility as a regional security architecture.

**For Great Power Competition:** Myanmar is becoming a proxy for Chinese influence consolidation in mainland Southeast Asia. India and the US have limited leverage due to geographic and strategic constraints. A collapsed Myanmar state would have profound implications for Chinese BRI infrastructure investments and Indian Northeast security.

### Competing Analyses

**"The Junta Will Survive" View (ICG, some ASEAN-aligned analysts):** Despite territorial losses, the Tatmadaw has survived multiple crises since 1948. China will not allow total state collapse on its border. The resistance is fragmented (SCEF is significant but incomplete), lacks air power and heavy weapons, and ASEAN normalization could provide the junta a diplomatic lifeline.

**"The Junta is Terminally Weakened" View (Irrawaddy, NUG, many Western analysts):** No military force in history has recovered from controlling 40% of territory to 100% without massive external intervention that simply isn't materializing. The Tatmadaw's legitimacy has permanently collapsed among ethnic Bamar youth — its historical base. Economic collapse means the junta cannot pay soldiers adequately. Desertion rates are high. SCEF provides the political framework that was previously missing.

### Saturday Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis: Myanmar and the Limits of Humanitarian Intervention

**Connection to International Law — R2P and Non-Intervention:**
Myanmar presents the Responsibility to Protect (R2P) doctrine's fundamental contradiction in its starkest form: the international community has both the stated commitment (UN World Summit 2005) to protect populations from genocide, ethnic cleansing, war crimes, and crimes against humanity when their government fails to do so — and the practical inability to act without Security Council authorization that China and Russia systematically block for Myanmar. The UN Special Adviser on the Prevention of Genocide has documented ethnic cleansing operations against Karen, Kachin, and Rohingya populations that meet multiple legal criteria for crimes against humanity. The legal-political gap: R2P exists as a norm but not as an operational framework — there is no mechanism for "coalition of the willing" R2P intervention without UNSC authorization, and China treats any Myanmar intervention as a precedent threat to its own sovereignty claims (Taiwan, Xinjiang, Hong Kong).

**Connection to Political Economy — Sanctions Effectiveness and Gray Markets:**
Western sanctions against Myanmar's military government (individual designations, sectoral sanctions on jade and gems, investment restrictions) have been ineffective in changing regime behavior for the same structural reasons that sanctions theory predicts: China and Thailand provide sufficient non-sanctioned economic access to prevent regime survival threat. The jade trade alone (~$31B/year, largely unmonitored) provides substantial off-budget revenue for military commanders. Myanmar's gray economy — informal cross-border trade with China, Thailand, and India — is large enough relative to the formal economy that sanctioning formal channels has limited impact. The political economy lesson: sanctions work when the target state has limited alternative economic relationships and when the sanctions coalition includes all major trading partners; Myanmar fails both conditions.

**Connection to Sociology — Ethnic Federalism and State-Building:**
Myanmar's civil war is the continuation of a 75-year state-building failure: the inability to construct a national identity that accommodates the country's extraordinary ethnic diversity (135 officially recognized groups) under a unitary state framework inherited from British colonial governance. The post-2021 coup resistance coalition (National Unity Government + People's Defence Forces + Ethnic Armed Organizations) is attempting to build an alternative federal democratic vision — but the ethnic armed organizations (EAO) have decades of history of competing with each other as well as against the Burmese military. The sociology of state-building (Tilly, 1990; Scott, 2009 "The Art of Not Being Governed") suggests that sustainable state construction requires either coercive unification (the Tatmadaw's failed approach) or genuine power-sharing federalism that accommodates ethnic autonomy — which the NUG's Federal Democracy Charter is attempting but faces enormous coordination challenges.

### Update — June 8, 2026: Min Aung Hlaing's "Civilian" Presidency, SCEF Military Operations, and International Recognition Dynamics

**Min Aung Hlaing's April 11 "Civilian" Transition — A Cosmetic Presidency:** On April 11, 2026, Min Aung Hlaing completed a carefully orchestrated political transition — resigning as military commander-in-chief and assuming the presidency of a nominally civilian government following staged elections in which SAC-approved parties ran against each other with no genuine opposition permitted. The transition was universally condemned by Western governments, the National Unity Government, and international human rights organizations as a "sham" designed to provide the junta with international legitimacy and sanctions relief without genuine democratic reform. The tactical logic: if a sufficient number of countries recognize the new "civilian" government, Myanmar could re-enter ASEAN formal participation, re-access international financial systems, and begin normalizing the economic consequences of the coup.

**International Non-Recognition — ASEAN's Continued Exclusion:** Despite the April 11 transition, ASEAN has maintained Myanmar's exclusion from formal participation — the organization continues its unprecedented policy of inviting "a non-political representative" from Myanmar rather than accepting SAC/new government delegates. The European Union, United States, United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia have all refused to recognize the new government, maintaining their designation of Min Aung Hlaing as the author of the coup and his new administration as illegitimate. Japan — which has historically maintained the closest ties among democracies with Myanmar's military — issued a deliberately ambiguous statement declining to explicitly recognize the new government while expressing hope for dialogue. The recognition gap matters economically: Myanmar remains cut off from World Bank, IMF, and ADB financing, significantly constraining any reconstruction or governance capacity that the new administration could develop.

**Resistance Forces Retake Thabeikkyin — Counter-Offensive Dynamics:** In a significant military reversal of the SAC counter-offensive gains of late 2025, PDF and allied forces retook **Thabeikkyin town** in Mandalay Region in May 2026 — a town the military had recaptured from resistance forces in July 2025. The recapture demonstrates that resistance forces can recover lost ground, and that the SAC's late-2025 counter-offensive gains were not decisive. The SCEF coordination framework is enabling better-coordinated joint operations: PDF units from different regions and ethnic armed organizations have conducted simultaneous pressure campaigns that prevent SAC from concentrating forces against any single front. The critical variable: the resistance's lack of air power and anti-aircraft capability remains the most significant asymmetry — SAC's ability to conduct air strikes on resistance-held territory with relative impunity limits the pace of territorial expansion.

**The SCEF Political Program — Federal Democracy Charter in Practice:** The SCEF's political platform, centered on the National Unity Government's Federal Democracy Charter, proposes a federal Myanmar state with: (1) ethnic states with genuine autonomy over taxation, education, and policing; (2) a federal parliament where ethnic representation is proportional; (3) a civilian-controlled military accountable to democratic government. This platform directly addresses the root cause of Myanmar's 75-year civil war — the denial of ethnic minority autonomy by a Burman-dominated central state. Its implementation challenge: the EAOs that make up SCEF's military backbone have been governing their territories as de facto independent states for decades; transitioning from armed autonomous governance to participation in a shared federal framework requires each EAO to accept constraints on its sovereignty that it currently enjoys unconditionally.

**Humanitarian Crisis — Scam Compounds as Political Economy:** Myanmar's conflict has generated an extraordinary secondary criminal economy: forced-labor telecom fraud compounds in conflict zones (primarily Shan State and Karen State border areas with China and Thailand). These compounds — where Chinese, Taiwanese, Malaysian, and other nationals are trafficked and forced to conduct cryptocurrency fraud and romance scams targeting victims globally — generate an estimated $7–10 billion annually in illicit proceeds that finance both EAO operations and organized crime networks. China's Operation 1027 (which triggered the Three Brotherhood Alliance offensive) was partly motivated by pressure on Myanmar border EAOs to close Chinese-citizen-targeting scam compounds — demonstrating that China's Myanmar policy is driven as much by domestic public outrage at scam compound victimization as by geopolitical calculation.

### Update — June 6, 2026: SCEF Unity Alliance, SAC Territorial Collapse, and China's CMEC Dilemma

**SCEF Formation — The Resistance Unifies (April 2026):**
The formation of the **Steering Council for the Emergence of a Federal Democratic Union (SCEF)** in April 2026 represents the most significant political development in Myanmar's resistance since the 2021 coup. SCEF unifies the National Unity Government (NUG — the shadow government of elected officials), the major Ethnic Armed Organizations (EAOs) including the Kachin Independence Organisation, Karen National Union, Restoration Council of Shan State (RCSS), and the National Progressive Party, alongside People's Defence Force (PDF) operational commanders under a single coordinating body.

The SCEF's operational significance: previously, Myanmar's resistance was fragmented into 20+ independent actors with competing territorial claims, ethnic identities, and political visions. This fragmentation allowed the SAC to pursue divide-and-rule tactics — negotiating separate ceasefires with some EAOs while fighting others. SCEF's unified structure aims to prevent this by committing all member organizations to a federal democracy platform, mutual non-aggression, and coordinated military operations. The body is explicitly modeled on the NUG's federal charter vision: a democratic federal union where ethnic states have genuine autonomy — addressing the root cause of Myanmar's 70-year civil war (denial of ethnic minority political rights by the Burman-dominated central state).

**SAC Territorial Collapse — Only 21% Remaining:**
The military junta's territorial position as of mid-2026 has collapsed to approximately **21% of Myanmar's territory** — compared to ~70% at the time of the February 2021 coup. The extraordinary pace of territorial loss reflects: the SAC's depleted manpower (defections, combat casualties, and draft resistance have reduced effective fighting strength from ~350,000 pre-coup to an estimated 60,000–80,000 functional troops); the EAOs' superiority in territorial knowledge and local legitimacy; and the decisive effect of the **Operation 1027 offensive** (Three Brotherhood Alliance, October 2023) that demonstrated the SAC could be defeated in conventional engagements — breaking the psychological barrier against confronting the military head-on.

SAC territory as of June 2026: primarily **Naypyidaw** (the purpose-built capital), parts of the **Irrawaddy Delta** (rice-producing heartland), and isolated urban centers in Mandalay and Bago. The SAC has lost control of virtually all border regions: the entire border with China (Shan State), India (Chin/Sagaing), and Bangladesh (Rakhine State) is now under EAO control. This border control has critical implications: the SAC cannot effectively tax trade, control smuggling, or enforce conscription in the majority of Myanmar's territory.

**China's CMEC Dilemma — Economic Investment Meets Security Reality:**
China's **China-Myanmar Economic Corridor (CMEC)** — a $15 billion Belt-and-Road Initiative project connecting Yunnan Province to the deep-water port at Kyaukpyu on the Bay of Bengal — is China's most important Myanmar strategic investment. CMEC infrastructure runs through territory now controlled by multiple EAOs: the Arakan Army controls the Kyaukpyu port environs; the Three Brotherhood Alliance controls the northern border crossing at Chinshwehaw; the Kachin Independence Organisation controls areas along the pipeline route.

China's response has been characteristically pragmatic: engaging with both the SAC government (which nominally approves CMEC) and the EAOs controlling relevant territory. China has provided tacit support to the Three Brotherhood Alliance's Operation 1027 offensive — which targeted Chinese-linked online scam compounds in Kokang — while simultaneously maintaining nominal diplomatic relations with the SAC. The result is an ambiguous Chinese position: de facto acceptance of EAO governance in CMEC-relevant areas in exchange for EAO commitments to protect Chinese-invested infrastructure. This approach has preserved Chinese economic interests more effectively than rigid SAC alignment would have — but has produced deep SAC resentment and signals the end of unconditional Chinese backing for Myanmar's military.

**Humanitarian Crisis Scale:**
ACLED estimates **96,000+ killed** since the coup (February 2021 – June 2026). UN OCHA estimates **5.2 million internally and externally displaced**. In northern Rakhine State (controlled by the Arakan Army since 2023), famine conditions have been documented by MSF and WFP — the Arakan Army's land blockade preventing aid from reaching Muslim Rohingya communities in northern townships. **18+ million people** require humanitarian assistance across Myanmar — in a country of 54 million, this means one in three persons.

**↔ Psychology: Resistance Psychology, Junta Brutality, and Ethnic Coalition Dynamics**

Myanmar's resistance movement — the People's Defence Force (PDF) in coalition with ethnic armed organizations (EAOs) — represents a fascinating [[social-psychology-and-group-dynamics]] experiment in coalition formation across historically antagonistic ethnic groups. The February 2021 coup triggered a "rally against threat" effect that temporarily overcame Burman-minority ethnic tensions going back decades — [[evolutionary-psychology-and-human-behavior]] explains this as classic out-group threat activation of in-group cooperation mechanisms. Junta brutality — documented massacres, torture, and aerial bombardment of civilian areas — produces [[trauma-resilience-and-post-traumatic-growth]] patterns in resistance communities: shared trauma creates cohesion while also generating intergenerational psychological damage. Myanmar military (Tatmadaw) leadership demonstrates classic [[psychology-of-leadership]] authoritarian pathologies: increasing isolation from reality, elimination of internal dissent, and magical thinking about international response. Civil disobedience movement participants show sophisticated [[negotiation-tactics]] and non-violent resistance strategy influenced by Gene Sharp's frameworks — essentially applied [[game-theory-and-strategic-thinking]] for asymmetric conflict. [[Cialdini-influence]] analysis of junta messaging reveals heavy use of authority and fear — the only Cialdini levers available when social proof, liking, and reciprocity have been destroyed by regime violence.

**↔ Tech & AI: Drone Warfare Democratization, Social Media Resistance, and Surveillance**

Myanmar's conflict has become a demonstration case for warfare technology democratization.
