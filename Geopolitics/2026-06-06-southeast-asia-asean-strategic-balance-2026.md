## Summary
Southeast Asia in 2026 stands at one of the most consequential strategic crossroads in its modern history. Ten countries across 680 million people navigate the intersection of US-China great power competition, a humanitarian catastrophe in Myanmar entering its fifth year of civil war, dramatic political transitions in Thailand and Vietnam, and a South China Sea that remains the world's most actively contested maritime space. The Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) — the region's cornerstone multilateral institution — faces existential strain: its foundational "ASEAN Way" (consensus, non-interference, neutrality) was designed for a different era and is poorly suited to an era of sharp geopolitical bifurcation. Yet ASEAN countries are demonstrating remarkable strategic sophistication, using their collective position between two superpowers to extract maximum economic benefit from both while preserving political independence. This "multi-alignment" strategy — being everybody's friend, nobody's ally — defines Southeast Asian geopolitics in 2026.

## Key Points
- ASEAN: 10-member bloc (Brunei, Cambodia, Indonesia, Laos, Malaysia, Myanmar, Philippines, Singapore, Thailand, Vietnam); combined GDP $3.6 trillion; world's 5th largest economy if treated as a unit
- "ASEAN centrality" is the bloc's strategic doctrine: ASEAN should be the hub of regional architecture, not a spoke in either US or Chinese strategic wheel
- Myanmar civil war: military junta (SAC) has lost control of ~50% of the country to ethnic resistance organizations (EAOs) and People's Defence Forces (PDF); humanitarian crisis with 2.6M internally displaced
- South China Sea: China controls approximately 70% of all artificial island features; Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia all experience regular Chinese Coast Guard harassment; the US-Philippines Enhanced Defence Cooperation Agreement (EDCA) sites are now operational
- Vietnam: party congress (January 2026) consolidated leadership under To Lam; economic growth 6.8% in 2025 driven by export manufacturing; delicate balance with China (party-to-party ties) and US (strategic partnership since 2023)
- Thailand elections (February 2026): Pheu Thai coalition won; Paetongtan Shinawatra (daughter of Thaksin) became PM — restoring the Thaksin political dynasty after 18 years of military-aligned governance
- Indonesia under Prabowo Subianto (inaugurated Oct 2024): maintained strategic autonomy while pursuing Chinese BRI investment AND US defense cooperation
- The "China+1" supply chain strategy is reshaping manufacturing flows: Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia, and Malaysia are the primary beneficiaries of manufacturers diversifying away from China

## Details

### The ASEAN Strategic Framework: "Multi-Alignment" as a Doctrine

ASEAN was founded in 1967 during the Cold War precisely to avoid being drawn into superpower competition. The Bangkok Declaration establishing ASEAN explicitly committed members to "neutrality and freedom from outside interference." This institutional DNA shapes regional responses to 2026's great power competition.

**The "ASEAN Way" in practice**:
- **Consensus decision-making**: All 10 members must agree; a single veto blocks any statement. Cambodia and Laos (heavily dependent on Chinese investment) regularly prevent ASEAN from issuing strong South China Sea condemnations
- **Non-interference**: ASEAN formally does not comment on members' internal affairs — this principle has made the bloc's Myanmar response largely toothless
- **Constructive engagement**: Maintain economic ties with all partners regardless of political differences

**Why multi-alignment works for ASEAN members**:
1. **Economic leverage**: ASEAN is simultaneously the US's 4th largest trading partner and China's largest regional trading partner. Both superpowers need ASEAN market access and production capacity — giving the region genuine negotiating leverage
2. **Security asymmetry**: ASEAN countries cannot militarily balance China independently; aligning too closely with the US risks Chinese economic retaliation (as Australia discovered in 2020 when China placed tariffs on $20B in Australian exports). The solution: take US security guarantees without formal treaty obligations that would trigger Chinese response
3. **Functional specialization**: Singapore hosts both the US 7th Fleet's logistics base and the largest Chinese state bank operations outside China. Indonesia drills with the US Pacific Fleet and simultaneously hosts Chinese-funded nickel smelting operations. Vietnam receives US weapons while maintaining the Communist Party's "Three Nos" policy (no military alliances, no foreign military bases, no siding with one power against another)

**Limits of the ASEAN Way**: When China physically occupies and militarizes disputed features in the South China Sea, "constructive engagement" and "dialogue" produce no behavioral change — only legal rulings (the 2016 UNCLOS arbitration, which China rejected) and military deterrence through US-Philippines cooperation have altered calculus at the margins.

### Myanmar: Civil War and the Collapse of Military Authority

Myanmar's military coup of February 2021 triggered a civil war that, by 2026, has become one of the world's most severe and underreported humanitarian crises.

**The conflict structure**:
- **SAC (State Administrative Council)**: The military junta; commands approximately 100,000–150,000 troops (reduced from pre-coup 350,000+ due to defections and battlefield losses)
- **NUG (National Unity Government)**: The shadow government of elected officials deposed in the coup; directs the People's Defence Forces (PDF)
- **Ethnic Armed Organizations (EAOs)**: Pre-existing ethnic militias representing Karen, Kachin, Shan, Rakhine, Chin, and other ethnic minorities; some aligned with NUG, some neutral, some historically allied with the SAC

**Territorial control as of mid-2026**: Independent military analysis (ACLED, ISW) estimates:
- SAC fully controls: ~30% of Myanmar territory (mainly Naypyidaw, parts of Irrawaddy Delta, central Myanmar)
- Contested: ~25%
- EAO/PDF control: ~45% — including most of Chin State, large portions of Shan, Karen, Kayah, Sagaing, and Mandalay regions

**Operation 1027 (October 2023)**: A coordinated offensive by Three Brotherhood Alliance (TBA: Arakan Army, Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army, Ta'ang National Liberation Army) was the turning point — capturing Chinshwehaw (a major border town) and proving the SAC could be defeated in conventional engagements. Subsequent offensives continued through 2024–2026.

**Humanitarian impact**: UN OCHA estimates 2.6 million internally displaced persons (IDPs) as of April 2026 (double the 2022 figure); 18+ million requiring humanitarian assistance; famine conditions in northern Rakhine State. The SAC has systematically bombed civilian villages and used air power against resistance-held towns.

**ASEAN's Myanmar failure**: ASEAN's "Five-Point Consensus" (April 2021) — calling for dialogue, humanitarian access, and a ceasefire — has been completely ignored by the SAC. Myanmar's junta seat at ASEAN summits has been suspended; only "non-political representatives" allowed. In practice, ASEAN has no mechanism to compel compliance or resolve the crisis.

**China's position**: China borders Myanmar with 2,185 km of frontier; the SAC is not China's preferred outcome (the previous elected government was also China-friendly). China has pragmatically engaged with both the SAC and the EAOs with border presence, seeking to protect the China-Myanmar Economic Corridor (CMEC) — a $15B BRI project connecting Yunnan province to Kyaukpyu deep-sea port on the Bay of Bengal. Multiple attacks on CMEC infrastructure by resistance forces have complicated China's engagement.

### Vietnam: The Bamboo Diplomacy Model

Vietnam is the most successful practitioner of "bamboo diplomacy" — flexible but rooted, bending in the wind without breaking. The metaphor, popularized by former General Secretary Nguyen Phu Trong, describes Vietnam's ability to maintain strong relations with both China and the US despite their fundamental antagonism.

**January 2026 Party Congress**: Vietnam's Communist Party (VCP) 14th National Congress (January 2026) consolidated power under General Secretary To Lam (former Minister of Public Security, who oversaw the prosecution of multiple high-profile corruption cases). The Politburo Standing Committee was reconstituted with a security/law enforcement orientation — suggesting Vietnam's leadership transition favors internal stability over economic reform in the near term.

**US-Vietnam relations**: Upgraded to "Comprehensive Strategic Partnership" (September 2023) — the highest diplomatic tier in Vietnam's foreign relations framework. The US is now Vietnam's largest export destination ($120B in 2025). Vietnam receives US Coast Guard cutters, F-16 maintenance training, and cybersecurity cooperation. A defense cooperation agreement signed in 2024 allows "operational activities" at Cam Ranh Bay — a former US Navy base now a commercial port — without constituting a permanent "foreign military base" (consistent with the Three Nos).

**South China Sea tensions**: Vietnam maintains the largest presence of all claimants in the Spratly Islands after China (21 occupied features). Vietnamese fishing boats in Vietnam's EEZ are regularly harassed by Chinese maritime militia vessels and Coast Guard. In 2025, a Chinese Coast Guard vessel used water cannons on a Vietnamese resupply mission in the Spratlys — one of multiple confrontations that year. Vietnam protests diplomatically but does not escalate militarily.

**Economic model**: Vietnam's GDP growth (6.8% in 2025) is driven by export manufacturing — electronics ($87B exports, led by Samsung), garments, and machinery. Samsung manufactures ~50% of its global smartphone production in Vietnam. The "China+1" diversification trend has made Vietnam the primary beneficiary: FDI inflows reached $38.5B in 2025, the highest in ASEAN outside Singapore.

### The Philippines: Marcos and the US-Philippines Alliance Revival

The Marcos Jr. administration (inaugurated June 2022) reversed the Duterte-era pivot toward China and has become the most actively US-aligned government in Southeast Asia.

**EDCA expansion (2023)**: The Enhanced Defence Cooperation Agreement, originally signed 2014 but implemented slowly under Duterte, was expanded to 9 EDCA sites (from 5) — including three in northern Luzon facing Taiwan, and one in Palawan facing the South China Sea. These sites now host US pre-positioned equipment, logistics, and rotating forces.

**South China Sea confrontations (2024–2026)**: Chinese Coast Guard vessels have used water cannons, sonic devices, and physical obstruction against Philippine resupply vessels to Second Thomas Shoal (Ayungin Shoal) — where a deliberately grounded Philippine Navy ship (BRP Sierra Madre) serves as a territorial marker. The US has explicitly invoked the US-Philippines Mutual Defense Treaty (1951) as covering Philippine vessels in the South China Sea — a significant commitment previously left ambiguous.

**Tension with the 2016 UNCLOS arbitration ruling**: The Permanent Court of Arbitration ruled 15-0 that China's "nine-dash line" claims have no basis in international law. China has ignored the ruling entirely. The US and EU back the ruling as legitimate international law; China characterizes it as "null and void." The legal limbo continues.

### Indonesia: The Giant's Strategic Weight

Indonesia (275 million people; 4th most populous country; world's 16th largest economy; world's largest Muslim democracy) is the geopolitical prize of Southeast Asia — large enough that both the US and China must treat it as a consequential strategic actor.

**President Prabowo Subianto** (inaugurated October 2024): Former military general with a controversial human rights record (alleged role in 1998 abductions of activists). Has surprised observers by pursuing a more pragmatic and multi-directional foreign policy than feared.

**Key strategic positions**:
- Maintains Indonesian commitment to "bebas-aktif" (free and active) foreign policy doctrine — non-alignment since 1945
- Deepened China economic ties: signed $12B in BRI infrastructure agreements (2025); Chinese investment in Kalimantan nickel processing
- Simultaneously: upgraded to "Comprehensive Strategic Partnership" with the US (November 2023 summit); hosted first US-Indonesia defense industry conference
- Critical minerals: Indonesia has 26% of world's known nickel reserves (essential for EV batteries); using this leverage to demand domestic processing requirements from both US and Chinese investors — refusing to export raw ore (export ban in place since 2020)

**The Natuna Islands dispute**: China's nine-dash line overlaps with Indonesia's Exclusive Economic Zone around the Natuna Islands. Indonesia does not claim the islands are disputed but has deployed military assets and renamed the relevant EEZ waters "North Natuna Sea." The dispute illustrates that even officially neutral Indonesia cannot escape South China Sea tensions.

### South China Sea: The Slow-Motion Military Build-Up

The South China Sea dispute involves overlapping claims by China, Vietnam, Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan. China's nine-dash line (claiming ~90% of the sea) has no basis in UNCLOS but China asserts "historic rights" dating back centuries.

**China's island-building program (2013–2016)**: China constructed 3,200 acres of artificial islands on submerged reefs in the Spratlys and Paracels, installing airstrips (capable of hosting H-6K bombers), radar systems, surface-to-air missiles (HQ-9), anti-ship missiles (YJ-12B), and naval facilities. The Fiery Cross, Mischief Reef, and Subi Reef artificial islands are now functional military bases.

**US FONOPs (Freedom of Navigation Operations)**: The US Navy conducts approximately 6–10 FONOPs per year within 12 nautical miles of Chinese-controlled features, asserting freedom of navigation under UNCLOS. China protests each FONOP but does not militarily intercept US warships. The FONOPs have become a routinized signaling mechanism rather than a deterrent against further Chinese activity.

**AUKUS and Southeast Asian reactions**: The AUKUS submarine deal (US + UK provide Australia with nuclear-powered submarines) was announced September 2021 and has implications for Southeast Asian security architecture. Indonesia and Malaysia issued formal protests over "nuclear proliferation concerns" (objecting to the principle of sharing nuclear propulsion technology outside the NPT framework). Singapore and the Philippines were supportive. ASEAN solidarity fractured along the traditional China-friendly vs. US-aligned axis.

**The 2025–2026 military balance**: China's People's Liberation Army Navy (PLAN) now operates approximately 355 ships — larger than the US Navy by hull count (though the US leads in tonnage and air power). China's DF-21D and DF-26 "carrier killer" anti-ship ballistic missiles are believed to threaten US carrier strike groups within the first and second island chains. This anti-access/area denial (A2/AD) capability — not land invasion — is the primary military challenge for US regional deterrence.

### The "China+1" Economic Transformation

The US-China trade war (tariffs imposed 2018–2019, maintained and deepened through 2025), COVID-19 supply chain disruptions, and rising Chinese wages have accelerated manufacturing diversification away from China.

**ASEAN as the primary beneficiary**:
- Vietnam: Electronics, garments ($28B in exports to US, 2025)
- Thailand: Hard disk drives, automotive components (Thailand is the world's 9th largest automobile producer)
- Malaysia: Semiconductor assembly and testing ($68B electronics exports in 2025)
- Indonesia: Nickel-based EV battery components
- Cambodia: Garments (heavily dependent on US market; 35% of GDP from garments)

**The "China+1" dynamic**: Major companies (Apple, Samsung, Nike, IKEA, HP) are not entirely leaving China — they are building parallel supply chains in Southeast Asia as insurance against geopolitical disruption or tariff escalation. Apple: ~5% of iPhones now assembled in India; 8–12% planned by 2026; Vietnam accounts for AirPod assembly. Samsung: 50% of smartphones in Vietnam; China <5%.

**Constraints on diversification**: Southeast Asia's infrastructure lags China's; the supply chain ecosystem (specialized suppliers, logistics, trained workforce) built over 30 years in China cannot be replicated quickly. Thailand's automotive industry, for example, relies on 2,000+ Japanese and Korean Tier-2 suppliers that will not relocate simultaneously. The China+1 strategy implies supplementing, not replacing, China.

### Expert Analysis: Will ASEAN Survive the Geopolitical Pressure?

**The pessimistic view** (Bilahari Kausikan, former Singapore diplomat): ASEAN's consensus model is structurally unable to manage great power competition. China has effectively weaponized Cambodia and Laos as "blocking minorities" to prevent ASEAN from taking positions on the South China Sea. The result is either institutional paralysis or lowest-common-denominator statements that satisfy no one. ASEAN's "centrality" is increasingly ceremonial.

**The optimistic view** (Kishore Mahbubani, NUS): ASEAN has succeeded precisely because it avoids making irreversible strategic choices. The EU chose sides in the Cold War; ASEAN did not. The payoff: Southeast Asian economies grew at among the world's fastest rates during the same period. ASEAN centrality reflects a genuine preference by both the US and China to not see the other achieve hegemony in the region — making ASEAN a structural beneficiary of great power competition.

**The pragmatic middle** (Ian Bremmer): ASEAN's diversity — from Singapore's financial hub to Myanmar's failed state, from the Philippines' military alliance with the US to Cambodia's financial dependency on China — means there is no single "ASEAN foreign policy." Individual members are pursuing their own optimal multi-alignment strategies, and ASEAN institutions are the platform for coordination rather than the locus of real strategic decision-making.

### Saturday Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis: ASEAN as the Archetype of Multipolar Hedging

**Connection to International Relations Theory — ASEAN Centrality and Non-Alignment:**
ASEAN's "centrality" doctrine — the principle that ASEAN should be the hub of regional security and economic architecture, rather than being organized through bilateral great-power relationships — is a formal institutionalization of hedging strategy. ASEAN-led platforms (ARF, ADMM+, EAS, RCEP) give smaller states agenda-setting influence they cannot exercise bilaterally with China or the US. The institutional design is deliberately consensus-based (all 10 members must agree) and non-binding — making ASEAN decisions less constraining than US alliance commitments but more legitimate for members uncomfortable with explicit alignment. The strategic logic: consensus requirements prevent any external power from capturing ASEAN for anti-others purposes; non-binding decisions prevent ASEAN from becoming a military alliance that would force members to choose sides in US-China competition. This institutional design is rational hedging formalized as international governance.

**Connection to Development Economics — ASEAN's Growth Model and Supply Chain Reconfiguration:**
The "China+1" supply chain diversification strategy (companies adding a non-China manufacturing location to reduce concentration risk) has benefited ASEAN more than any other region. Vietnam has emerged as the primary beneficiary: $40B+ in manufacturing FDI annually (2023-2025), export-led growth at 6-7% GDP growth, and emerging capability in electronics, semiconductors, and EV components. The supply chain migration is creating a regional manufacturing ladder: labor-intensive work migrating from China to Vietnam/Bangladesh/Cambodia; intermediate manufacturing migrating from China to Thailand/Malaysia/Indonesia; advanced manufacturing (semiconductors, precision components) migrating to Malaysia, Vietnam, and Singapore. The development economics insight: the "flying geese" pattern of East Asian development (Japan → Korea/Taiwan → China → ASEAN) is repeating, driven by supply chain diversification rather than purely wage-driven competitive migration.

**Connection to Technology and AI — ASEAN Digital Economy and AI Governance:**
Southeast Asia has emerged as one of the world's fastest-growing digital economies ($218B in 2023, projected $600B by 2030, Google-Temasek-Bain e-Conomy SEA Report). The digital growth is creating AI governance challenges at the same moment as capability: Singapore is establishing itself as Asia's AI governance hub (Smart Nation initiative, AI governance framework, AI Verify testing toolkit); Vietnam is developing a national AI strategy; Indonesia is navigating AI regulation with the world's 4th largest population and fast-growing tech ecosystem. The ASEAN Guide on AI Governance (2020, updated 2022) is a voluntary framework that has not yet translated into mandatory regulation — creating a governance vacuum that is being filled by different national approaches. The geopolitical dimension: Chinese AI platforms (TikTok's ByteDance, Alibaba Cloud, Tencent) have strong ASEAN market positions that US platforms are competing against — making AI governance choices in ASEAN countries a de facto US-China competitive battleground.

### Update — June 8, 2026: Philippines ASEAN Chairmanship Stakes, Indonesia's Nickel Battery Strategy, and Singapore's AI Governance Hub

**Philippines ASEAN Chairmanship — The Code of Conduct Last Chance:** The Philippines' 2026 ASEAN chairmanship has placed Manila at the center of the most consequential ASEAN diplomatic push in years. With the South China Sea Code of Conduct negotiations ongoing for 26 years and expert consensus now firm that a binding text is "simply not achievable in 2026," the Philippines has shifted strategy: rather than pursuing a completed CoC, Marcos's team is building a **"Chair's Statement"** that documents what principles consensus exists on and where Chinese positions remain incompatible with UNCLOS. This documentation approach transforms the CoC process from a search for agreement into a public record of Chinese obstructionism — a different diplomatic objective that serves the Philippines' legal strategy better than an unenforceable agreement that China would negotiate to its advantage.

**Indonesia's Nickel EV Battery Industrial Policy — The $21 Billion Downstream Push:** Indonesia under Prabowo has maintained Joko Widodo's landmark policy of **downstream nickel processing** — banning raw nickel ore exports (since January 2020) and requiring domestic value-added processing before export. The policy, initially contested at the WTO by the EU (Indonesia won), has attracted $21 billion in Chinese investment in nickel smelting and battery manufacturing in Sulawesi (IMIP — Indonesia Morowali Industrial Park) and Halmahera. The result: Indonesia is now one of the world's largest producers of **nickel sulfate** (EV battery cathode precursor) and Class 1 nickel (used in premium batteries). The geopolitical dimension: China controls approximately 60–70% of Indonesian nickel smelting capacity — creating exactly the supply chain dependency that the US is trying to build alternatives to through the IRA's critical mineral provisions. The Biden-era $600 million DFC commitment to Indonesian non-Chinese battery processing has been continued by the Trump administration, reflecting bipartisan recognition of nickel's strategic importance.

**Singapore — Asia's AI Governance Hub in Practice (2026):** Singapore's **Smart Nation 2.0** initiative and its National AI Strategy 2.0 (published January 2024) have positioned the city-state as the de facto regional hub for AI governance standard-setting. Singapore's AI governance approach — voluntary frameworks rather than mandatory regulation, interoperability with both US and EU standards, and business-friendly deployment environments — reflects its multi-alignment strategic doctrine applied to technology. Key 2026 developments: Singapore hosted the **AI Safety Summit Asia** in March 2026 (parallel to the Seoul follow-up to the UK Bletchley Park summit), bringing together 28 nations' AI agencies; Singapore's **AI Verify** testing toolkit has been adopted by 40+ companies across ASEAN as a voluntary AI deployment assurance standard; and Singapore's **FinTech Festival** (2025) featured the first regional deployment of agentic AI trading systems — with Singapore's MAS as the world's first financial regulator to issue operational guidelines for autonomous AI financial agents.

**Vietnam's "China+1" Manufacturing Boom — Supply Chain Numbers (2026):** Vietnam's "China+1" manufacturing boom has produced concrete supply chain numbers that define ASEAN's relationship with global trade. Samsung's Vietnamese production plants — located primarily in Bắc Ninh and Thái Nguyên provinces — now account for approximately **50% of global Samsung smartphone production**, contributing roughly $65 billion in annual export value (about 20% of Vietnam's GDP). Apple's contractor Foxconn has shifted iPad and MacBook assembly lines to Vietnam; Intel operates its largest global assembly and test facility in Hà Nội. The shift is structural, not cyclical: Vietnamese manufacturing has developed genuine process competency and scale that makes relocation economically painful. For geopolitics: Vietnam's deep integration into US and South Korean electronics supply chains creates a mutual economic vulnerability that constrains Hanoi's diplomatic freedom with China even as party-to-party ties continue — the "bamboo diplomacy" flexibility is narrowing as supply chain dependency deepens.

**The US-China Competition for ASEAN Digital Infrastructure:** The competition for ASEAN digital infrastructure — submarine cables, cloud platforms, 5G networks — is the newest theater of great power rivalry in Southeast Asia. China's **Peace Cable** (connecting Southeast Asia, Middle East, and Europe bypassing India) and Huawei Marine Networks' submarine cable projects offer ASEAN countries 30–50% lower cost connectivity than alternatives. US and allied responses: the **Blue Pacific Cable** consortium (US-Australia-Japan-Singapore), the **Echo Cable** (US-Singapore, with Google), and active pressure on ASEAN governments to exclude Huawei from 5G core network equipment. Singapore, Vietnam, and the Philippines have largely excluded Huawei from 5G cores; Indonesia, Thailand, and Malaysia have been more accommodating. The digital infrastructure competition has direct security implications: whoever controls the physical cable infrastructure has intelligence access to data transiting it — making "clean cable" a geopolitical category as important as "clean network."

### Update — June 6, 2026: Myanmar's SCEF Alliance, Thailand Transition, and Vietnam's To Lam Era

**Myanmar's Resistance Consolidates — SCEF Formation (April 2026):**
In a strategically significant development for Myanmar's civil war, a new umbrella body — the **Steering Council for the Emergence of a Federal Democratic Union (SCEF)** — was formed in **April 2026**, unifying the major resistance organizations around a federal democracy platform. SCEF brings together the National Unity Government (NUG), several of the largest Ethnic Armed Organizations (including the Kachin Independence Organisation, Karen National Union, and Restoration Council of Shan State), and People's Defence Force (PDF) commanders under a single coordinating structure for the first time since the coup.

The formation of SCEF represents an important maturation of the resistance: rather than numerous independent actors competing for resources and diplomatic recognition, SCEF provides a negotiating interlocutor and a unified command structure for post-junta governance planning. This reduces one of the major obstacles to international recognition — the fragmentation that allowed the SAC to claim that no legitimate alternative existed. SAC Territory control as of mid-2026: approximately **21%** of Myanmar's territory, down from ~70% at the coup. EAO/PDF control: approximately **42%**. Total casualties since 2021: ACLED estimates **96,000+ killed**; **5.2 million** internally and externally displaced.

**Thailand — The Return of the Thaksin Political Dynasty:**
Thailand's **February 2026 parliamentary elections** produced a decisive victory for the **Pheu Thai coalition**, with **Paetongtan Shinawatra** — the daughter of former Prime Minister Thaksin Shinawatra — becoming Prime Minister. This completes the extraordinary political rehabilitation of the Thaksin family: Thaksin was overthrown in a military coup in 2006, spent 15 years in self-imposed exile (Dubai), returned to Thailand in 2023, was granted a royal pardon, and now governs through his daughter. The February 2026 election victory is the Pheu Thai's fifth government since 2001 — demonstrating the party's durability among Thailand's rural and lower-income voter base despite periodic military interruption.

For Thailand's foreign policy: the Pheu Thai's traditional China-friendly economic orientation and emphasis on ASEAN centrality over explicit US alignment provides continuity with ASEAN's multi-alignment doctrine. Thailand remains a major US treaty ally (Mutual Defense Treaty 1954) while maintaining the largest Chinese tourism and investment presence in mainland Southeast Asia. Paetongtan faces the same structural tension as every Thai PM: managing the military's institutional power alongside democratic governance.

**Vietnam — To Lam's Security-Oriented Leadership:**
Vietnam's **Communist Party 14th National Congress in January 2026** consolidated power under General Secretary **To Lam** — the former Minister of Public Security who oversaw the prosecution of multiple high-profile corruption cases in Vietnam's anti-corruption "blazing furnace" campaign. To Lam's Politburo Standing Committee reflects a security and law enforcement orientation, signaling that Vietnam's near-term leadership priorities are internal stability and party discipline rather than economic liberalization.

For foreign policy, Vietnam under To Lam is expected to maintain the "bamboo diplomacy" doctrine — flexible but rooted — pursuing the **Comprehensive Strategic Partnership** with the US (upgraded September 2023) and party-to-party ties with China simultaneously. Vietnam's GDP growth of **6.8% in 2025** was driven by Samsung manufacturing (~50% of global Samsung smartphone production in Vietnam) and electronics exports. Vietnam's "China+1" manufacturing position has made it the primary beneficiary of supply chain diversification, with FDI inflows reaching **$38.5 billion in 2025** — the highest in ASEAN outside Singapore. The To Lam era's emphasis on internal security may modestly slow the institutional reforms that foreign investors want, but Vietnam's fundamental cost and geographic advantages maintain the FDI trajectory.

**Indonesia's Prabowo — Strategic Autonomy with Chinese Investment:**
President Prabowo Subianto (inaugurated October 2024) has maintained strategic autonomy while pursuing maximum economic benefit from both superpowers: **Chinese BRI investment** in nickel smelting and infrastructure (Indonesia controls 40%+ of global nickel reserves, critical for EV batteries) alongside **US defense cooperation** (joint exercises, F-15EX fighter procurement discussions). Indonesia's **"million-friend, zero-enemy" foreign policy doctrine** represents the most explicit formulation of ASEAN multi-alignment — and Prabowo's military background (former Kopassus special forces general) gives him the credibility to conduct robust US defense exercises without being characterized as pro-Western.

[[Negotiation-tactics]] within ASEAN are dominated by face-saving frameworks: direct confrontation is avoided through procedural delay, technical working groups, and "agreed language" that allows all parties to claim victory — an approach that works for trade disputes but strains under security pressure.
