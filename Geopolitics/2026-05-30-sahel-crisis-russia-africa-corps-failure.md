## Summary

The Sahel — a vast semi-arid belt stretching from Senegal to Sudan across West and Central Africa — has become the world's fastest-deteriorating security crisis. Three military juntas in Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso, collectively constituting the Alliance des États du Sahel (AES), expelled French forces and Western partners between 2021 and 2023 and invited in Russia's Africa Corps (successor to the Wagner Group). By May 2026, the gamble has spectacularly failed: coordinated jihadist and Tuareg insurgent attacks in April 2026 exposed the AES-Russia partnership as incapable of stabilizing the region, with Bamako itself under effective siege, Mali's defense minister assassinated, and Russian forces driven from Kidal in a humiliating withdrawal. The Sahel crisis is simultaneously a counterterrorism failure, a proxy conflict theater, a humanitarian catastrophe displacing millions, and a cautionary tale about the limits of mercenary security partnerships.

---

## Key Points

- **April 25, 2026 offensive**: JNIM (al-Qaeda affiliate) and FLA (Tuareg separatists) launched the most audacious simultaneous attacks in a decade on Kidal, Gao, Sévaré, and Kati — all major military bases.
- **Bamako siege**: Jihadist forces severed fuel supply corridors from Senegal, Mauritania, and Guinea, effectively blockading Mali's capital. Junta leader Assimi Goïta controls only Bamako and Kati as of late May 2026.
- **Russia expelled from Kidal**: Africa Corps forces withdrew from the northern stronghold of Kidal under pressure, characterized by observers as a "humiliating blow to Moscow's prestige."
- **Defense minister assassinated**: Malian Defense Minister Sadio Camara — a Russian-trained officer and key architect of Mali's Russia pivot — was killed in a suicide vehicle bombing near Bamako.
- **AES in crisis**: All three junta-led states face existential security challenges, with Burkina Faso's capital Ouagadougou itself threatened by JNIM encirclement.
- **State collapse risk**: Multiple institutions (ISPI, International Crisis Group, Foreign Policy) warn Mali may be approaching state collapse, potentially the first in a Sahel domino sequence.
- **Russia's limits exposed**: Africa Corps deployed approximately 2,500 personnel in Mali (with 1,000 more expected), but the force has proven insufficient against a highly mobile, locally embedded insurgency with deep root causes.

## Details

### Historical Deep Dive: How the Sahel Became a Permanent Crisis Zone

The Sahel's modern security crisis has roots traceable to multiple converging forces operating across decades:

**Colonial Legacy and State Weakness (1960s–2000s)**  
Post-independence states across the Sahel inherited artificial borders drawn by France and Britain that bisected ethnic and nomadic communities — particularly the Tuareg, a Berber-descended pastoralist people spanning Mali, Niger, Algeria, Libya, and Burkina Faso. The Malian state, centered on the agricultural south and the capital Bamako, never successfully integrated the vast desert north (Azawad), treating it as a peripheral hinterland from which taxes were extracted but services rarely delivered. The first Tuareg rebellion erupted in 1963, followed by major uprisings in 1990–1995 and 2006–2009, each time suppressed militarily but never resolved politically.

**The Libya Collapse Multiplier (2011)**  
Muammar Gaddafi had absorbed thousands of Tuareg fighters into his military forces over decades as a deliberate counterweight to sub-Saharan states he sought to manipulate. When NATO-backed rebels toppled Gaddafi in 2011, these forces — battle-hardened, heavily armed with MANPADS, anti-tank weapons, and heavy machine guns from Gaddafi's arsenals — returned to Mali, Niger, and other Sahel states. This transformed the qualitative capability of Tuareg insurgents overnight. The Mouvement National pour la Libération de l'Azawad (MNLA) launched a major offensive in January 2012 that quickly overran northern Mali.

**The 2012 Malian Coup and French Intervention (2012–2013)**  
The MNLA's offensive triggered a coup in Bamako in March 2012, as Captain Amadou Sanogo overthrew President Amadou Touré, blaming the government for inadequate support of the military. The coup paradoxically accelerated the northern collapse. By June 2012, jihadist groups — particularly Ansar Dine (affiliated with AQIM) and the Movement for Oneness and Jihad in West Africa (MUJAO) — had allied with then split from the MNLA, seizing the northern cities of Kidal, Gao, and Timbuktu and implementing strict Sharia law. France intervened militarily in January 2013 under Operation Serval, halting jihadist advances toward Bamako and retaking major northern cities. This evolved into Operation Barkhane (2014), the largest French military operation since Algeria, eventually involving 5,500 troops across five Sahelian countries.

**The Decade of Escalating Insurgency (2014–2021)**  
Despite Barkhane's presence, the security situation worsened steadily. Key factors:
- **JNIM formation (2017)**: Multiple Sahel jihadist groups unified under Iyad Ag Ghaly's leadership into Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin (JNIM), an al-Qaeda affiliate that proved far more strategically coherent than its predecessors.
- **Islamic State Sahel Province (ISSP)**: IS established a competing Sahelian franchise that fought JNIM for territory while both fought government forces.
- **Burkina Faso collapse (2015–present)**: Previously stable, Burkina Faso became the world's fastest-growing displacement crisis between 2019–2022 as JNIM swept through the Sahel interior.
- **G5 Sahel failure**: The regional security force combining forces from Mali, Niger, Burkina Faso, Chad, and Mauritania struggled with funding shortfalls, coordination failures, and corruption.

**The Coup Wave (2020–2023)**  
Mali experienced two coups in rapid succession (August 2020 and May 2021). Burkina Faso suffered coups in January and September 2022. Niger's elected President Mohamed Bazoum was overthrown in July 2023. Each coup reflected genuine popular frustration with failing security, French military presence, and corrupt governments, but also reflected deliberate destabilization campaigns including Russian disinformation operations exploiting anti-French sentiment.

### The Wagner Group/Africa Corps Pivot

**Origins of the Russia-Sahel Relationship**  
Russia's interest in sub-Saharan Africa intensified under Putin's post-2014 revisionist foreign policy. The Wagner Group, the private military company founded by Yevgeny Prigozhin under Kremlin direction, served as Russia's deniable proxy, combining combat operations with resource extraction and political influence operations. Wagner entered the Central African Republic (CAR) in 2018, providing security to President Touadéra's government in exchange for mining concessions.

**Wagner Enters Mali (2021)**  
Following Mali's second coup in May 2021, the transitional government led by Colonel Assimi Goïta opened negotiations with Wagner as a replacement for French forces. Wagner deployed approximately 1,000 personnel to Mali by late 2021, officially as "instructors." The Malian government paid Wagner an estimated $10.8 million per month, supplemented by access to gold mining operations in the Kéniéba region. The Wagner deployment coincided with Barkhane's drawdown, which France began in 2022 amid diplomatic pressure from the junta.

**Moura Massacre and International Isolation (March 2022)**  
Wagner's operational debut in Mali became immediately controversial. In March 2022, Malian armed forces (FAMa) and Wagner personnel conducted a five-day operation in Moura, a town in central Mali, killing an estimated 500 civilians — the UN Human Rights Office called it "the worst single atrocity recorded in Mali's decade-long armed conflict." The massacre deepened Mali's international isolation and undermined any legitimacy argument for the junta's Wagner strategy.

**From Wagner to Africa Corps (2023–2024)**  
Wagner's June 2023 mutiny — when Prigozhin led an armed march on Moscow — fundamentally altered the organization. Prigozhin's death in a plane crash in August 2023 triggered a reorganization. The Russian Ministry of Defense absorbed Wagner's Africa operations, rebranding them as the Africa Corps under the General Staff's Military Intelligence Directorate (GRU). Roughly 70–80% of staff were retained Wagner veterans. The transition entailed significant disruption: recruiting shortfalls, loss of Prigozhin's entrepreneurial leadership, and increased bureaucratic friction.

**Deployment Levels and Structure (2025–2026)**  
- **Mali**: ~2,500 Africa Corps personnel, with 1,000 additional expected (from Jeune Afrique, April 2026)
- **Niger**: ~100 personnel
- **Burkina Faso**: 100–300 personnel
- Total Sahel deployment: ~3,000 personnel — small relative to the operational theater
- Operations include joint FAMa-Africa Corps patrols, training, and occasional offensive operations against JNIM/ISSP targets

### Key Actors Analysis

**JNIM (Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin)**  
- Founded 2017 by merging AQIM, Ansar Dine, al-Mourabitoun, and Macina Liberation Front
- Leadership: Iyad Ag Ghaly (Tuareg, former MNLA figure turned jihadist), Amadou Koufa (Fulani preacher commanding Macina Liberation Front)
- Strategy: Exploit Fulani-Dogon-Bambara ethnic tensions, recruit from marginalized pastoralist communities, impose "Islamic governance" as alternative to corrupt state
- Territorial control: Large swaths of central and northern Mali, substantial areas of Burkina Faso, Liptako-Gourma tri-border zone
- Revenue: Kidnapping-for-ransom (dozens of millions annually), taxation of trade routes, cattle theft, illicit mining taxation
- April 2026 offensive represents strategic escalation: shift from rural insurgency to urban siege warfare

**FLA (Azawad Liberation Front)**  
- Successor to the MNLA and CMA (Coordination des Mouvements de l'Azawad)
- Tuareg-led, secular nationalist organization seeking either Azawad independence or genuine federal autonomy
- Temporary alliance with JNIM for the April 2026 offensive represents opportunistic coalition: shared interest in defeating Russian-backed government, though fundamentally incompatible long-term visions
- Controls Kidal and extensive northern territory since April 2026 offensive

**Russia/Africa Corps**  
- Strategic motivation: Displace French/Western influence, secure resource concessions, demonstrate Africa as viable sphere of influence, use Africa as leverage in confrontations with Europe
- Operational motivation: Gold mining access, disinformation platforms, training regional forces loyal to Moscow
- Constraint: Force size insufficient for the operational theater; Africa Corps optimized for regime protection and urban security, not counterinsurgency across 1.2 million km²
- May 2026 assessment: Facing "signposts of failure" per Small Wars Journal analysis

**AES Juntas**  
- **Mali (Goïta)**: Consolidating power in Bamako while losing peripheral control; increasingly dependent on Africa Corps for regime survival
- **Burkina Faso (Traoré)**: Captain Ibrahim Traoré, the world's youngest head of state at 36, faces JNIM within 50km of Ouagadougou
- **Niger (CNSP junta)**: Expelled US forces from Agadez air base in 2024, reducing ISR capacity; Russia-aligned but with limited Russian deployment
- Collective strategy: Use anti-Western nationalism and pan-African solidarity rhetoric to maintain domestic legitimacy while security deteriorates

**France and Western Powers**  
- France expelled from Mali (2022), Burkina Faso (2023), Niger (2023) — Operation Barkhane ended
- EU training mission (EUTM Mali) terminated 2023
- US expelled from Niger Agadez base (April 2024)
- Current posture: Regional engagement through Mauritania, Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire; MINUSMA (UN peacekeeping) also expelled from Mali in 2023
- Dilemma: Re-engagement risks backlash; non-engagement allows jihadist expansion

### Economic Dimensions

**Sahel's Structural Underdevelopment**  
The Sahel is among the world's poorest regions by virtually every metric:
- Mali GDP per capita: ~$840 (2025 estimate, declining)
- Burkina Faso GDP per capita: ~$770
- Niger GDP per capita: ~$580 — among the world's lowest
- Population growth among highest globally (Niger TFR ~6.7 children per woman)
- Climate change reducing agricultural productivity: rainfall declining, desertification advancing
- Unemployment: Youth unemployment exceeds 70% in rural areas, providing jihadist recruitment pools

**Resource Extraction and the AES Economy**  
Despite poverty indices, the Sahel sits atop significant resources:
- **Gold**: Mali is Africa's third-largest gold producer (~60 tonnes/year). Africa Corps reportedly controls access to artisanal mining zones in return for security.
- **Uranium**: Niger holds 7.5% of global uranium reserves; Orano (French) and Chinese operations continue under junta oversight
- **Oil**: Niger's Agadem oil field connected to CNPC pipeline to Benin coast (operational 2024); junta retains Chinese partnership
- **Revenue diversion**: Junta governments divert resource revenues to security spending and patronage rather than social investment, deepening the governance gap exploited by insurgents

**Humanitarian-Economic Cascade**  
The fuel blockade imposed by JNIM on Bamako in April 2026 illustrates the insurgency's economic warfare capability:
- 80% of Mali's fuel imports transit through corridors from Senegal/Mauritania/Guinea
- Severing these routes triggers fuel rationing, generator shutdowns, hospital crises, food cold-chain failures
- World Food Programme estimates 7.5 million Malians face acute food insecurity (2026), up from 4.2 million in 2023
- UNHCR: 2.8 million internally displaced in Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger combined

### Military Situation

**Jihadist Military Capabilities**  
JNIM has evolved significantly from the asymmetric guerrilla forces of 2012:
- Operates with battalion-sized formations (300–500 fighters) capable of simultaneous multi-city attacks
- Uses motorcycles and modified pickup trucks (technicals) for rapid mobility across terrain
- Sophisticated use of IEDs, including complex ambush sequences
- Growing urban warfare capability (demonstrated in April 2026)
- Supply chains through Libya and Algeria (AQIM networks)
- Estimated 10,000–15,000 total fighters across JNIM (various estimates, some higher)

**Africa Corps Limitations**  
- **Scale mismatch**: 2,500 personnel cannot provide meaningful presence across Mali's 1.24 million km²
- **Skill gap**: Counterinsurgency in Sahelian terrain requires cultural fluency, language skills, and community engagement — not Africa Corps' strengths
- **Kinetic approach**: Africa Corps/Wagner tactics emphasize firepower and mass killing, which increases civilian casualties and drives recruitment to JNIM
- **Kidal withdrawal**: Forced from a key northern stronghold in May 2026 after JNIM-FLA coalition attacks overwhelmed the position
- **Camara assassination**: Loss of Mali's Russia-trained defense minister represents both a security intelligence failure and a political blow

**FAMa (Forces Armées Maliennes)**  
- Chronically under-resourced, poorly paid, low morale
- Equipment: Soviet-era tanks and artillery supplemented by Russian deliveries
- Desertions and defections continue
- Core reliable force: Presidential Guard and small special forces units trained with African partners

### Diplomatic Track

**AES Bloc Formation (2023–2024)**  
Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger formalized the Alliance des États du Sahel in September 2023 and withdrew from ECOWAS (Economic Community of West African States) in January 2024 — a profound break with regional integration. The AES issued a common passport and announced eventual currency integration (departing from the CFA franc).

**ECOWAS-AES Tensions**  
- ECOWAS imposed sanctions on Mali and Burkina Faso following coups, lifted under negotiation
- Niger sanctions more severe; ECOWAS threatened military intervention before backing down
- AES withdrawal from ECOWAS creates legal and economic complications: trade tariffs, migration barriers, passport validity

**Russia's Diplomatic Positioning**  
- Russia has used AES alignment to create a bloc of UN General Assembly abstention/opposition to Ukraine-related resolutions
- Bilateral defense treaties signed with all three AES states
- Russia-Africa Summit (Sochi, July 2023) promoted a counter-narrative of "neo-colonialism versus sovereignty"
- Diplomatic returns diminishing as battlefield failures become evident

**UN and Humanitarian Response**  
- MINUSMA expelled from Mali in December 2023 after junta demanded withdrawal; 13,000 peacekeepers departed
- OCHA (UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs) operational in all three states but with restricted access
- WFP access corridors frequently disrupted by fighting

### Scenarios and Forecasts

**Scenario 1: Mali State Collapse (High Risk — 35–45% probability, 12-month horizon)**  
If JNIM maintains the Bamako fuel blockade and the April 2026 offensive consolidates territorial gains, the Malian state faces potential collapse: government unable to pay salaries, military morale collapses, Goïta falls. Consequences: massive refugee flows (millions toward Senegal, Mauritania, Algeria), potential JNIM governance experiment, further destabilization of Burkina Faso and Niger. West Africa's coastal states (Côte d'Ivoire, Ghana, Togo, Benin) face insurgent pressure already expanding southward.

**Scenario 2: Grinding Stalemate (Most Likely — 40–50%)**  
Africa Corps reinforcement (1,000 additional personnel expected) combined with FAMa reconstitution stabilizes Bamako's perimeter. Government retains capital and key cities; insurgents control rural areas. This mirrors Afghanistan's trajectory: contested countryside, barely-viable government zones. Protracted humanitarian crisis continues.

**Scenario 3: Russian Escalation and Doubling Down (Low Risk — 15–20%)**  
Russia dramatically expands Africa Corps to 8,000–10,000 (similar to Syria surge), brings air power (helicopters, drones), and attempts a decisive offensive. Costly and unlikely given Russia's Ukraine commitments and reputational damage, but possible if Putin views Africa abandonment as strategically unacceptable.

**Scenario 4: AES Rapprochement with West (Very Low Risk — 5–10%)**  
Junta governments, facing existential threats, quietly signal willingness to re-engage France/EU/US for security assistance. Political costs domestically are prohibitive; timing may eventually force this.

**Coastal Contagion Risk**  
JNIM has already begun penetrating northern Côte d'Ivoire, Togo, Benin, and Ghana. The "Sahel-ification" of coastal West Africa — previously stable, democratic, and economically dynamic — represents a 5–10 year strategic threat to the entire sub-region.

### Expert Analysis

**Realist/Structural Perspective (John Mearsheimer school)**  
The Sahel crisis is primarily a consequence of great power competition: France's decades-long CFA franc neocolonialism, extraction-focused relationships, and military presence that prioritized French interests over Sahelian governance legitimacy created conditions for the anti-French backlash. Russia exploited this vacuum cynically. Neither France nor Russia offers genuine development; both are extractive security actors. Resolution requires sustained governance investment, which no external power is willing to provide.

**Counter-Insurgency School (David Kilcullen, John Nagl tradition)**  
The Sahel crisis demonstrates classic insurgency fundamentals: insurgents win by not losing; governments lose by not winning. The fundamental failure is the "security first" approach — attempting to defeat insurgents militarily before addressing governance and economic grievances. Every civilian killed by Africa Corps creates ten new JNIM recruits. Sustainable stabilization requires community legitimacy and political inclusion of marginalized groups (Tuareg, Fulani), which neither Russian-backed juntas nor the previous French-supported governments pursued.

**Development Economics Perspective (Paul Collier, Daron Acemoglu)**  
The Sahel is a "conflict trap" (Collier's term): states with low income, high primary commodity dependence, and history of conflict face 50%+ probability of conflict recurrence. External military interventions without institutional development are statistically unlikely to produce durable peace. The AES juntas' extraction-focused governance replicates colonial patterns, maintaining the extractive institutions that Acemoglu and Robinson identify as the root cause of underdevelopment.

**Critical Security Studies**  
Western and Russian framing of the Sahel crisis as "counterterrorism" obscures the political and economic drivers that produce the insurgencies. JNIM and ISSP exploit real grievances (land, water, ethnic marginalization, state predation) and offer genuine governance services in areas they control — dispute resolution, taxation with predictability, physical security. Defeating them militarily without addressing underlying causes ensures regeneration.
