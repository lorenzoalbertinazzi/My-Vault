## Summary
The rivalry between the United States and China is the defining geopolitical contest of the 21st century, reshaping trade, technology, military alliances, and the global institutional order. As of 2026, the competition has intensified across every domain — tariffs and supply chain decoupling, semiconductor restrictions, Taiwan Strait tensions, and competing visions for global governance. The World Economic Forum's Global Risks Report 2026 identified "geoeconomic confrontation" as the single risk most likely to trigger a global crisis, with 68% of respondents expecting a multipolar or fragmented world order over the next decade.

## Key Points
- The US-China rivalry is structural and bipartisan — it persisted through Trump and Biden administrations and continues under the current US government
- Technology decoupling is the sharpest front: US semiconductor export controls have severely restricted China's access to advanced chips
- Taiwan remains the most dangerous flashpoint; Beijing considers reunification non-negotiable, Washington maintains "strategic ambiguity"
- China is building an alternative institutional architecture (BRI, AIIB, SCO) to reduce dependence on Western-dominated systems
- 68% of WEF survey respondents (2026) expect a fragmented multipolar world — the post-WWII liberal order is no longer the consensus baseline
- The Global South is increasingly a contested battleground, with both powers competing for influence via trade, infrastructure, and diplomatic alignment

### Key Actors

**Joe Biden (2021–2025):** Maintained tariffs; multilateral approach (AUKUS, CHIPS Act); October 2022 semiconductor export controls — the decisive technology containment move.

**Donald Trump (2025–):** Raised tariffs to ~145%; then agreed May 2026 truce at ~30%. Combines confrontation with transactional deal-making. Accompanied by Musk, Cook, Fink, Ortberg to Beijing — signaling corporate re-engagement.

**Xi Jinping:** Consolidated power (removed term limits 2018); "Made in China 2025"; Taiwan reunification as historical mission; "no limits" partnership with Russia (2022). Has stalled Power of Siberia-2 pipeline to extract leverage from Moscow.

**TSMC / Taiwan Semiconductor:** The single most strategically important private company. Produces ~90% of world's most advanced chips. TSMC Arizona fab (2025: 4nm; 2026: 2nm) represents deliberate US-encouraged geographic diversification.

**NVIDIA:** US AI chip maker whose A100/H100 chips are restricted from China. China-specific H800 created then also restricted. Central to the compute dimension of the AI competition.

### Historical Background: From Engagement to Competition

For three decades after Nixon's 1972 opening to China, US policy was built on the "engagement thesis": integrating China into global trade and institutions (WTO, 2001) would gradually liberalize its political system. The theory failed. China's economic rise was spectacular; its political liberalization did not materialize. By the 2010s, under Xi Jinping, China became more authoritarian domestically and more assertive internationally.

Key turning points:
- **2012**: Xi Jinping comes to power; begins consolidating authority and abandoning Deng Xiaoping's "hide and bide" foreign policy doctrine
- **2013**: Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) launched — China's global infrastructure investment strategy spanning 150+ countries
- **2017**: Trump administration publishes National Security Strategy explicitly naming China a "revisionist power" and "strategic competitor" — first time either party framed it this way officially
- **2018–2019**: US-China trade war; tariffs on $360B+ of Chinese goods; China retaliates
- **2020**: US bans Huawei from 5G networks in allied countries; begins semiconductor export controls
- **2022**: Biden administration drastically expands chip export controls (October 2022 regulations considered the most sweeping technology export restrictions since the Cold War)
- **2023–2026**: Controls expanded; China accelerates domestic semiconductor development (SMIC, Huawei's Kirin chips); Taiwan tensions escalate periodically

### The Technology War: Semiconductors as the Choke Point

Advanced semiconductors — particularly chips below 10nm node size — are the oil of the modern economy. They power AI, military systems, telecommunications, and nearly every advanced industrial process.

The US strategy has been to cut China's access to:
1. Advanced chips (NVIDIA A100/H100 → restricted; China-specific versions created then also restricted)
2. Chip manufacturing equipment (ASML's EUV lithography machines — the Netherlands complied with US pressure to restrict exports)
3. The talent pipeline (restrictions on Chinese nationals working in US advanced semiconductor facilities)

**China's response**:
- Massive state investment in domestic chip manufacturing (SMIC)
- Huawei's Kirin 9000s chip (7nm, made domestically) surprised analysts in 2023
- Long-term vulnerability remains: China cannot yet produce EUV machines or match TSMC/Samsung at the frontier

The semiconductor battle is widely seen as the most consequential front because advanced chips are necessary for military AI, hypersonic weapons, nuclear modernization, and economic competitiveness.

### Taiwan: The Most Dangerous Flashpoint

Taiwan is a self-governing democracy that produces ~90% of the world's most advanced chips (TSMC). Beijing views it as a breakaway province; Taiwan functions as a de facto independent state.

**The strategic triangle**:
- **Beijing**: Considers unification non-negotiable; has refused to rule out force; military exercises around Taiwan have intensified (2022 exercises after Pelosi visit, 2024 exercises after Lai Ching-te's inauguration)
- **Washington**: Maintains "strategic ambiguity" — officially does not support independence, commits to providing Taiwan defensive weapons, but does not guarantee military intervention; Biden verbally committed to defend Taiwan multiple times; official policy unchanged
- **Taipei**: Under President Lai Ching-te (DPP, pro-sovereignty), pursues pragmatic independence without formal declaration

Most analysts assess a Chinese military takeover by 2027–2030 as possible but not inevitable. The PLA is modernizing rapidly; US military planners are concerned about the timeline converging with Chinese capabilities. A Taiwan conflict would be catastrophic: potential for US-China direct military conflict, destruction of TSMC (global chip production collapse), and economic disruption dwarfing the COVID-19 shock.

### The Global Institutional Battle

China is building parallel institutions to reduce dependence on Western-dominated ones and offer alternatives to Global South countries:

| Western Institution | Chinese Alternative |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| World Bank, IMF | Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB), New Development Bank |
| WTO trading rules | BRI bilateral arrangements, RCEP |
| SWIFT (payment messaging) | CIPS (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System), digital yuan (e-CNY) |
| G7 | G20 + SCO (Shanghai Cooperation Organisation), BRICS |
| US dollar reserve system | Petro-yuan arrangements, bilateral currency swaps |

BRICS expansion (2024) added Saudi Arabia, UAE, Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, and Argentina (though Argentina later reversed under Milei), creating a bloc encompassing ~45% of global population.

### Economic Interdependence vs. Decoupling

Despite the rivalry, the US and Chinese economies remain deeply intertwined:
- US-China bilateral trade: ~$575B (2024), making China the US's third-largest trading partner
- Apple manufactures ~90% of iPhones in China (though diversifying to India, Vietnam)
- American pension funds hold significant Chinese equities; Chinese entities hold ~$800B in US Treasuries

Both sides practice "decoupling" rhetoric but implement it selectively. The US term "de-risking" (vs. "decoupling") reflects the acknowledgment that full separation is economically catastrophic. The real strategy: reduce dependence in critical sectors (chips, telecom, pharmaceuticals, rare earths) while maintaining trade relationships in others.

### The Global South as Contested Space

Across Africa, Southeast Asia, Latin America, and the Middle East, both powers compete for alignment:
- China offers infrastructure investment (BRI), no-strings-attached lending, and diplomatic non-interference
- The US offers security guarantees, democratic governance frameworks, and access to Western financial markets

Many Global South countries pursue strategic non-alignment, extracting benefits from both powers. India, in particular, has positioned itself as a leader of this "multi-alignment" approach.

### Outlook for 2026–2030

The structural trajectory favors continued intensification of competition:
- China's GDP may surpass the US (at market exchange rates) around 2030–2035, though its growth has slowed significantly
- PLA modernization on track; significant capabilities improvement expected by late 2020s
- US domestic consensus on China competition is one of the few areas of genuine bipartisan agreement
- Risk of miscalculation is high: spy balloons, military near-misses over the South China Sea, and cyber incidents create escalation pathways

The critical question for the next five years is whether the rivalry remains managed competition or tips toward direct confrontation — most likely trigger being Taiwan or a severe economic shock blamed on the other side.

### Update — May 28, 2026

**Trump-Xi Beijing Summit (May 14, 2026)**: Presidents Trump and Xi met in Beijing — the first direct summit since Trump's return to office. Agenda included:
- Chinese purchases of US agricultural and industrial goods
- Tariff rollback framework
- Taiwan (no public statement of any agreement)
- Rare earths (China controls ~80% of global processing; leverage in negotiations)
- Iran: Both sides have divergent interests — the US is in conflict with Iran; China is Iran's largest oil customer

**Tariff truce — May 12, 2026**: Following intensive pre-summit negotiations, the US and China reached a formal trade truce on May 12, reducing US tariffs on Chinese goods to approximately 30% (down from ~145% peak tariff levels during 2025 escalation) while China reduced tariffs on US products to 10%. Both sides agreed to discuss a reciprocal tariff reduction framework for products worth $30 billion or more. US Treasury Secretary Bessent indicated China "would accept the restoration of prior rates as long as they don't go higher." Business leaders including Elon Musk (Tesla), Tim Cook (Apple), Larry Fink (BlackRock), and Boeing CEO Kelly Ortberg accompanied Trump to Beijing, signaling renewed corporate appetite for China engagement. NPR's comparison of US and Chinese summit readouts identified "minor inconsistencies" on agriculture, tariffs, and rare earths — suggesting underlying tensions persist beneath the improved diplomatic surface.

**Supply chain decoupling accelerating**: Apple has moved a significant portion of iPhone production to India; Nike has shifted to Vietnam. The structural supply chain diversification out of China is gaining momentum, with companies treating China+1 strategies as permanent policy rather than contingency planning.

**Iran as a wedge**: The US-Iran conflict (see separate note) is creating a significant point of friction. China has major oil purchase agreements with Iran and has been calling for restraint. The conflict tests whether China will use its leverage with Iran to support a diplomatic solution (gaining US goodwill) or protect its energy interests (maintaining Iran relationship). As of late May 2026, China is pursuing back-channel mediation while continuing to purchase Iranian oil.

**Assessment**: The Trump-Xi summit and May 12 trade truce signal both sides prefer "managed rivalry" to open confrontation, but structural tensions in technology, Taiwan, and now Iran remain unresolved. The new tariff floor of ~30% (US on China) and ~10% (China on US) represents a significantly lower level than the 2025 escalation peak — a genuine de-escalation in the trade dimension, even as technology decoupling and military posturing continue to intensify.

## Cross-Disciplinary Connections

### Currency War, Dollar Hegemony, and the Architecture of Financial Power

The US-China competition is, at its deepest level, a contest over who writes the rules of the global financial system — and currency markets are both the battleground and the scorecard. The dollar's "exorbitant privilege" (a term coined by French Finance Minister Valéry Giscard d'Estaing in the 1960s) allows the United States to run persistent current account deficits, borrow at lower rates than any other sovereign, and impose crippling financial sanctions via SWIFT access. China's multi-decade strategy to internationalize the renminbi (RMB) — through CIPS, bilateral currency swap agreements, petroyuan arrangements with Saudi Arabia and Russia, and the digital yuan (e-CNY) — represents a systematic effort to erode this privilege rather than directly challenge it in a single confrontation. The May 2026 Trump-Xi summit trade truce, which reduced US tariffs from ~145% to ~30%, illustrates the paradox of economic interdependence as a weapon: both sides have inflicted genuine damage (supply chain disruption, inflation, investment uncertainty), but neither can push past a threshold without self-harm that exceeds the cost imposed on the adversary. The ~$800B in Chinese holdings of US Treasuries creates what strategists call a "financial mutually assured destruction" — a deterrence logic structurally similar to nuclear deterrence but operating through bond markets rather than warheads.

From a portfolio theory perspective, the US-China competition has fundamentally altered the efficient frontier for globally diversified investors. The correlation structure between US and Chinese equities — previously a diversification benefit — has become increasingly unreliable as policy risk dwarfs fundamental valuation signals. The VIX (volatility index) spikes that accompany Taiwan Strait exercises or tariff announcements represent priced geopolitical risk that did not exist in the pre-2017 era of "engagement." Behavioral finance concepts apply directly: investors exhibit recency bias (overweighting the benign 1990-2015 period and underpricing structural decoupling risk), while institutional investors in US pension funds holding Chinese equities face the classic anchoring problem — anchored to pre-tension valuations in markets that have structurally changed.

### The Semiconductor Chokepoint as Industrial Policy Economics

The US semiconductor export controls represent the most consequential application of export control theory since the CoCom (Coordinating Committee for Multilateral Export Controls) regime of the Cold War. The economic logic is straightforward in outline but complex in execution: semiconductor manufacturing involves a global supply chain with extraordinary concentration at specific nodes — ASML's monopoly on EUV lithography machines, TSMC's dominance of advanced fabrication, and American dominance of electronic design automation (EDA) software from Synopsys and Cadence. By restricting access at these chokepoints, the US can theoretically prevent China from achieving frontier semiconductor capability indefinitely, as each node is sufficiently complex and capital-intensive that China cannot replicate it quickly despite unlimited state investment. China's SMIC achieving 7nm production (announced in 2023 via Huawei's Kirin 9000s chip) proved that workarounds are possible — but also illustrated the limitation: SMIC used older DUV machines in multiple patterning steps, a process that is slower, lower yield, and higher cost than EUV-based 7nm, making it uncompetitive at scale. The macroeconomic implication is significant: Chinese AI and military systems built on domestically produced 7nm chips will operate at meaningfully higher cost per unit of compute than US systems on TSMC 3nm, creating a structural military and commercial disadvantage that compounds over time.

This connects directly to machine learning fundamentals: compute is the binding constraint on AI capability at the frontier. The scaling laws documented by researchers at OpenAI, DeepMind, and Anthropic show that model capability is roughly a power-law function of training compute. A persistent 2–3 generation disadvantage in chip capability translates, via these scaling laws, into a compounding disadvantage in AI capability that cannot be overcome by architectural cleverness alone at scale. This is why the semiconductor war is, at its root, an AI war — and why both Beijing and Washington treat chip access as a national security matter of the first order.

### Tech Decoupling and the Containerization of Supply Chains

Apple's migration of iPhone production from China to India, Nike's shift to Vietnam, and the broader "China+1" strategy visible in corporate supply chains as of 2026 can be analyzed through the lens of systems architecture — specifically, the same containerization logic that transformed software deployment. Docker and Kubernetes enable applications to run in modular, portable, loosely-coupled units that are not tied to a single server. The "China+1" strategy applies an analogous logic to physical supply chains: build modularity and redundancy into manufacturing networks so that no single geopolitical node (China) represents a critical dependency. The irony is that China's own economic success was built on the *opposite* principle — hyper-concentrated, deeply integrated supply chains that achieved efficiency through geographical co-location. The geopolitical decoupling reverses the last 30 years of supply chain optimization, accepting permanent efficiency losses in exchange for resilience — a trade-off that only makes sense if one assigns sufficient probability weight to the tail risk of Taiwan conflict or trade war rupture.

### The Thucydides Trap, Cognitive Biases, and Conflict Escalation Risk

Graham Allison's "Thucydides Trap" — derived from Thucydides' account of why Sparta went to war with Athens ("It was the rise of Athens and the fear that this inspired in Sparta that made war inevitable") — provides the structural framework, but cognitive biases at the leadership level determine whether structural pressures translate into actual conflict. The availability heuristic causes American strategists to overweight recent Chinese assertiveness (South China Sea islands, Taiwan exercises, Xinjiang) and underweight the enormous costs China has absorbed to maintain economic integration. Conversely, Chinese strategists show classic loss aversion around Taiwan: the CCP frames reunification not as a gain to be pursued when optimal but as a loss to be prevented (loss of face, historical mission unfulfilled), making the calculus more aggressive than a pure expected-value calculation would suggest. Mental models matter here: the risk management framework that worked for the US-Soviet relationship (nuclear deterrence, clear red lines, back-channel communication) does not transfer cleanly to the US-China relationship, where economic interdependence creates ambiguous incentives that nuclear-only deterrence frameworks cannot capture.

### Update — May 29, 2026

**Xi-Putin meeting in Moscow (May 20, 2026)**: On the sidelines of the Trump-Xi Beijing summit's concluding days, Xi Jinping met Russian President Vladimir Putin in Moscow. The two leaders reaffirmed their "comprehensive strategic partnership" and issued statements about multipolarity and resistance to Western hegemony — standard language for their summits. Significantly, however, they failed to reach agreement on the **Power of Siberia-2 pipeline** — a proposed $10 billion natural gas pipeline that would connect Russia's Siberian gas fields to China's northern markets and would represent Russia's single most important alternative to its lost European energy market. The failure reflects ongoing Chinese leverage over Russian desperation: Beijing is in no hurry to lock in long-term gas commitments at prices that favor Moscow, and Russia cannot afford to walk away. The episode is a microcosm of the relationship: Russia increasingly dependent on China, China extracting better terms by not rushing.

**Taiwan excluded from WHO World Health Assembly for 10th consecutive year (May 2026)**: The World Health Organization rejected Taiwan's bid to participate in the World Health Assembly (WHA) for the tenth consecutive year, following sustained Chinese opposition. The US State Department and the EU's External Action Service both expressed public support for Taiwan's inclusion — meaningful diplomatic support, but insufficient to overcome Beijing's institutional blocking power within the WHO governance structure. The recurring exclusion of Taiwan from global health governance is particularly visible in 2026 given the simultaneous Ebola PHEIC and the ongoing Iran-related health and humanitarian system stress: Taiwan, despite its exemplary public health record (notably during COVID-19), remains structurally excluded from the global health architecture by Chinese political pressure.

**Japan deepening security network across Indo-Pacific (May 2026)**: Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi met South Korean President Lee Jae Myung on May 19, affirming security cooperation including trilateral US-Japan-South Korea coordination. Japan also signed a **Reciprocal Access Agreement (RAA) with the Philippines** — allowing Japanese and Philippine forces to operate on each other's territory — a remarkable expansion of Japan's security footprint that would have been politically impossible under previous Japanese constitutional interpretations. Japan has now signed RAAs with Australia, the UK, and the Philippines, building a web of defense partnerships that operationally strengthens the US-led Indo-Pacific coalition without requiring formal treaty changes. This network directly addresses China's anti-access/area denial (A2/AD) strategy in the South and East China Seas.

**Russia-Ukraine: Oreshnik hypersonic strikes on Kyiv (May 23–24)**: Russia's use of the Oreshnik hypersonic ballistic missile in its largest single attack on Kyiv of the war (600 drones, 90 missiles, 4 killed, ~100 injured, 30+ buildings destroyed) is relevant to US-China dynamics in a specific way: it demonstrates that Russia is conducting major military escalation simultaneously with the US-China trade truce and the Iran negotiations, reinforcing the convergent instability that characterizes the current multipolar moment. Europe's condemnation of the strike as "nuclear brinkmanship" — the Oreshnik is nuclear-capable — adds to the global alarm about threshold-crossing behavior by nuclear-armed states, a concern that applies with equal or greater force to the Taiwan scenario.

## Related
### Update — June 1, 2026

**"America's narrative on Taiwan needs an update" (Brookings, 2026):** Brookings Institution analysis argues that the US strategic communication framework around Taiwan — built on Cold War deterrence concepts — is failing to account for the full spectrum of Chinese coercive options. Specifically, the conventional deterrence discourse focuses on invasion scenarios while underplaying gray zone erosion of Taiwanese sovereignty (diplomatic isolation, economic coercion, cognitive warfare). The Brookings recommendation: reframe the US posture from "defend against invasion" to "prevent coercion across all domains."

**War on the Rocks — "Deterrence Won't Fail, It Will Be Bypassed" (February 2026):** This widely-read analysis from War on the Rocks argues that the PRC's most rational path to Taiwan control is not a direct military assault (which risks catastrophic US intervention) but rather a multi-domain pressure campaign that achieves strategic objectives below the threshold of triggering Article 51 collective self-defense. Methods include: seizing Kinmen or Matsu (outlying islands with ambiguous US defense commitments), imposing a "quarantine" (not a blockade, to avoid the legal definition) on Taiwan's ports, or engineering domestic political conditions in Taiwan that bring a Beijing-friendly government to power. The analysis has triggered significant debate in US strategic planning circles.

**Fourth Taiwan Strait Crisis dynamics (Wikipedia / CFR, 2026):** The cumulative ADIZ violations, median-line crossings, and carrier exercises of 2025–2026 are being characterized as the **Fourth Taiwan Strait Crisis** — an ongoing, low-intensity version of the 1954, 1958, and 1995–96 crises. Unlike previous crises, which had clear precipitating events and ended, the Fourth Crisis is defined by **continuous, normalized pressure** rather than discrete escalation. This definitional shift matters: if the crisis never "ends," deterrence never fully "holds" — the PRC establishes a new baseline of aggressive behavior.

**WEF 68% fragmentation forecast still operative:** The WEF Global Risks Report 2026's finding — 68% of respondents expect a multipolar or fragmented world order — is reinforced by the events of the first half of 2026: the US-Iran war, the Venezuela intervention, the India-Pakistan conflict, and the parallel Ukraine war have together demonstrated that the liberal rules-based order is under simultaneous pressure on four fronts. No single power — not the US, not China — has the capacity or will to maintain order globally. The multipolar transition is not hypothetical; it is happening in real time.

**China's Latin America pivot deepens post-Venezuela (2026):** China's diplomatic response to the US Venezuela intervention — strong condemnation, UNSC resolution veto by US, expanded Chinese presence as a counterweight narrative — has accelerated Chinese diplomatic outreach in Latin America. Brazil's Lula government has positioned China as a partner in the "anti-interventionist bloc." This represents a direct consequence of the US-China competition extending into the Western Hemisphere: actions taken under purely regional US domestic rationale (narco-terrorism) have global strategic consequences that advantage China's positioning.

### Update — June 2, 2026

**US-China trade war: 30% effective tariff rate after May 2026 Geneva truce (PIIE / Tax Foundation, 2026):** Following the dramatic tariff escalation of spring 2025 — when Trump raised tariffs on China by 145 percentage points, causing US imports from China to fall ~50% year-on-year — a May 2026 **Geneva trade truce** (coinciding with the Trump-Xi Beijing summit) established a 60-day pause on the highest tariff tiers. The current effective tariff rate on most Chinese goods is approximately **30%** (20% "fentanyl" tariff + 10% "reciprocal" tariff), compared to the 145%+ peak. This still represents the highest effective US tariff on any country in the current global trading system. The PIIE's analysis of 2025 import data identifies five key lessons from the first year of full escalation: (1) US importers bore most of the tariff cost through higher consumer prices; (2) Chinese exports rerouted through Vietnam, Mexico, and India, partially circumventing tariffs; (3) US manufacturing did not revive as promised — domestic production filled less than 20% of the import gap; (4) China's export sector contracted but did not collapse; (5) third-country beneficiaries (Vietnam, India, Mexico) gained most from the trade war's supply-chain diversion.

**US-China Section 301 investigation of Phase One deal compliance (October 2025):** The USTR launched a Section 301 investigation in October 2025 examining whether China fulfilled its 2020 Phase One trade deal purchase commitments ($200B in additional US goods over 2020–2021). China fell substantially short of those commitments (by an estimated $150–170B, per Peterson Institute data), citing COVID disruptions. The investigation, if it produces a determination of non-compliance, gives the Trump administration legal authority to impose additional tariffs outside the 60-day pause framework — a potential trigger for re-escalation even if the current truce holds. The investigation represents the structural tension in US-China trade: Trump simultaneously wants a deal (for political credit) and maximum leverage (for negotiating future terms), creating a strategy that is internally contradictory and produces uncertainty for businesses planning US-China supply chains.

**Trump's Beijing visit (May 14, 2026) and the Iran-China overlap:** Trump's May 14 Beijing summit with Xi Jinping occurred simultaneously with the US-Iran MOU negotiations — creating a unique diplomatic moment where the world's two most consequential bilateral relationships were being managed in parallel. The Beijing meeting reportedly included discussions of: China's role in pressuring Iran toward the nuclear deal (China is Iran's largest oil customer and holds significant leverage); fentanyl precursor chemical flows from Chinese manufacturers to Mexican cartels (the "20% fentanyl tariff" trigger); and Taiwan — where Xi reiterated Chinese sovereignty claims and Trump gave no new commitments. The convergence of Iran negotiations and China trade talks in the same week of May 2026 reflects the administrative ambition of the Trump second term: manage multiple global crises simultaneously through personal summit diplomacy rather than sustained multilateral processes.

**PIIE analysis: "Five Takeaways from US Imports in 2025":** The Peterson Institute for International Economics published a comprehensive analysis of the full-year 2025 import data, documenting the trade war's measurable effects: (1) US goods trade deficit with China fell from $380B to approximately $220B — a significant reduction, but achieved through demand destruction rather than US manufacturing revival; (2) Chinese smartphone and electronics imports fell 60%+ but consumer electronics prices in the US rose 15–20% as supply chains scrambled; (3) rare earth and critical mineral imports from China fell despite no tariffs on these categories — a voluntary Chinese supply restriction (confirmed by Commerce data) testing US vulnerabilities; (4) agricultural exports to China collapsed by 40% before the trade truce, harming US farmers who are a core Trump political constituency; (5) the supply chain rerouting through Vietnam and Mexico has created new "trans-shipment" vulnerabilities that USTR is investigating under Section 301.

### Update — June 3, 2026

**WEF analysis: "Future of China trade policy as US-China relations evolve" (June 2026):** The World Economic Forum published a forward-looking assessment of US-China trade policy trajectories in light of the May 2026 Geneva truce, identifying three possible pathways: (1) **Managed decoupling** — tariffs stabilize at ~30%, supply chains continue diversifying over 5–10 years, maintaining economic engagement without full normalization; (2) **Transactional re-engagement** — Trump extracts specific Chinese concessions (Boeing aircraft orders, agricultural purchases, rare earth access) in exchange for further tariff reductions, producing episodic economic normalization without strategic trust; (3) **Renewed escalation** — USTR Section 301 non-compliance findings or Taiwan incident triggers a second tariff war, potentially worse than 2025. WEF's expert polling places pathway 1 (managed decoupling) as most likely at 50–55%, with pathway 2 at 25–30% and pathway 3 at 20%. The June 2026 environment — Iran breakdown threatening oil markets, Taiwan tensions continuing, USTR Section 301 investigation ongoing — makes pathway 3 more probable than its 20% WEF base case suggests.

**CFR: "China and the US Agreed to 'Strategic Stability' — They Don't Define It the Same Way" (May 15, 2026):** The Council on Foreign Relations published an incisive analysis of the Trump-Xi summit's "strategic stability" language — which both sides publicly endorsed but interpreted differently. The US definition: strategic stability = predictable rules of the road, reduced risk of miscalculation, maintained US technological edge, no military action on Taiwan. The Chinese definition: strategic stability = US respects China's core interests (Taiwan, Hong Kong, Xinjiang), ends semiconductor export controls, reduces tariffs to near-zero, and withdraws strategic ambiguity on Taiwan. The two definitions are essentially incompatible — China's stability requires the US to end its competitive posture, while America's stability requires China to abandon its revisionist objectives. The CFR analysis concludes that "strategic stability" is diplomatic language obscuring a structural competition that both sides prefer to manage rather than resolve.

**White House fact sheet: Historic Trump-Xi deals — Boeing, agriculture, rare earths (May 2026):** The White House published a formal fact sheet documenting the specific commitments extracted at the Beijing summit: (1) China approved an initial purchase of **200 Boeing aircraft** — the first Chinese commitment to buy American-made Boeing planes since 2017, worth approximately $20–25 billion in list-price value; (2) China committed to purchase **at least $17 billion per year in US agricultural products** through 2028; (3) China agreed to address US concerns about **rare earth and critical mineral supply restrictions** — including yttrium, scandium, neodymium, and indium — which Beijing had been restricting as leverage in the trade war. These are concrete economic deliverables that Trump can present to domestic constituencies as "wins." But they do not touch the structural issues (Taiwan, semiconductors, military expansion) that define the strategic competition.

**US-China Board of Trade and Board of Investment — new institutional architecture:** The summit established two new bilateral institutions: the **US-China Board of Trade** (managed by Commerce and USTR) and the **US-China Board of Investment** (managed by Treasury). These bodies are designed to create a regularized bilateral economic dialogue mechanism — similar in concept to the Strategic and Economic Dialogue (S&ED) that existed under Obama/Biden, which Trump eliminated in his first term and is now partially recreating. The creation of new institutionalized dialogue is a signal that both sides prefer managed engagement to confrontation — but the body's effectiveness will depend entirely on its mandate and staffing, and it lacks the comprehensive scope of pre-2017 engagement frameworks.

### Saturday Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis: The US-China Competition as Civilizational System Contest

**Connection to Political Philosophy — Liberal International Order vs. Tianxia:**
The deepest dimension of US-China competition is a philosophical contest between two different theories of international order. The liberal international order (Ikenberry, 2011) is built on sovereignty, multilateral rules-based institutions (UN, WTO, IMF), universal human rights norms, and democratic governance legitimacy. China's implicit alternative draws from the Confucian "tianxia" concept (all under heaven) — a hierarchical world order where relationships are defined by proximity to a benevolent central power (China), obligation rather than rights, and harmony through hierarchy rather than competition through equality. Xi Jinping's "Community of Shared Human Destiny" is the contemporary tianxia restatement — a vision of world order that explicitly rejects universal norms in favor of civilizational pluralism (i.e., Chinese governance is legitimate by Chinese norms, not universal ones). This philosophical competition matters because it determines whether a Chinese-influenced international order would maintain the institutional infrastructure (WHO, WTO dispute resolution, IMF conditionality, UNHCR) that the liberal order created.

**Connection to Complexity Theory — Complex System Interdependence and Decoupling:**
The US-China economic interdependence (bilateral trade ~$575B/year; mutual financial exposure through Treasury holdings, equity cross-listings, and technology supply chains) is a complex system where decoupling attempts produce second-order effects that planners cannot fully anticipate. The 2018–2022 tariff war demonstrated this: US tariffs intended to reduce China's trade surplus instead redirected trade through third countries (Vietnam, Mexico) while raising US consumer prices. The semiconductor export controls intended to slow Chinese AI development have stimulated a domestic Chinese chip industry that is accelerating faster than expected. Complexity theory (Holland, Axelrod, Epstein) predicts that interventions in complex adaptive systems produce unexpected emergent consequences — including the emergence of new stable configurations (Chinese semiconductor ecosystem) that were not desired by the intervening party. The strategic implication: confident decoupling plans systematically underestimate the second and third-order adaptation they trigger.

**Connection to Energy Geopolitics — The Solar Panel Dependency:**
The most acute US-China dependency that strategic planners consistently underweight is clean energy manufacturing. China produces ~85% of global solar panels, ~75% of lithium-ion batteries, and ~60% of wind turbines. The US energy transition depends on Chinese manufacturing to decarbonize American electricity generation — creating a structural tension between the climate imperative (buy cheap Chinese solar panels) and the strategic imperative (reduce dependency on Chinese manufacturing). The IRA's domestic content provisions (additional ITC credits for US-manufactured solar components) are attempting to bifurcate this dependency — but in 2026, US domestic solar panel manufacturing capacity meets less than 20% of annual installation requirements. The energy transition is, involuntarily, a deepening of the US-China economic relationship even as strategic decoupling is pursued in chips and advanced technology.

### Update — June 6, 2026: Trump's "Negotiating Chip" Doctrine, Trade Truce Economics, and the Technology War Intensifies

**Trump's "Negotiating Chip" Doctrine — Taiwan Arms as Diplomatic Currency:**
The Trump administration's post-Beijing summit decision to **pause a $14 billion Taiwan arms package** and explicitly characterize it as a "negotiating chip" with China represents the most significant single departure from bipartisan US Taiwan policy in 45 years. Since the Taiwan Relations Act (1979), successive US administrations maintained the principle that Taiwan arms sales were not negotiable with China — that discussing them with Beijing would validate China's claim that they are China's internal affairs. Trump's transactional framing inverts this: Taiwan's security is now positioned as a variable in the US-China relationship rather than a fixed commitment independent of diplomatic give-and-take. Congressional reaction: Senior Republican senators (Tom Cotton, Marco Rubio in his legislative capacity, Lindsay Graham) publicly condemned the pause, creating rare bipartisan criticism of Trump's China policy. The arms backlog stands at **$31.72 billion** in approved-but-undelivered weapons — a separate embarrassment for US credibility regardless of the current pause.

**The May 12, 2026 Trade Truce — Economics and Limitations:**
The US-China trade truce of May 12, 2026 reduced US tariffs from ~145% to approximately **30%** and Chinese tariffs from ~60–80% to approximately **10%** — a significant de-escalation from the tariff war peak. The truce was partly enabled by the Trump-Xi Beijing summit (May 13–15), where China agreed to purchase **200 Boeing aircraft** and US agricultural goods. The economic significance: at ~145% tariffs, US-China bilateral trade was collapsing to ~$250B annually, threatening supply chains across electronics, consumer goods, and pharmaceuticals. At ~30%, trade will partially recover, though the 2018-era $600B+ volume is unlikely to return given structural decoupling in semiconductors, defense-related technologies, and strategic minerals.

Critical limitation: the trade truce explicitly excluded the technology war. US semiconductor export controls (the October 2022 rules, expanded in 2023 and 2024) remain fully in force — NVIDIA's most advanced AI chips remain prohibited to China; ASML EUV machines remain restricted; and the Biden-era "chip and science act" restrictions on US companies partnering with Chinese advanced semiconductor firms remain operational. The trade truce is therefore an economic normalization of consumer goods trade within the framework of continued technological competition and decoupling.

**Xi's September 2026 US Visit — Strategic Significance:**
A **Xi Jinping state visit to the United States** is scheduled for **September 2026** — the first state visit by a Chinese president in approximately a decade (Xi's last US visit was 2015). The scheduling of this visit — announced at the Beijing summit — reflects both sides' interest in managed competition: neither China nor the United States wants a hot conflict over Taiwan in the current 18-month window when the US is also managing the Iran nuclear negotiation, the Ukraine war, and multiple domestic pressures. The September visit will include a formal summit, likely addressing: Taiwan (managing the arms pause expectation), technology cooperation areas (potentially on climate and AI safety), fentanyl precursor chemical controls (a US domestic political priority), and North Korea (where US pressure on China to restrain Kim Jong-un's nuclear expansion has produced minimal results).

Xi's "fire and water" warning at the Beijing summit — that **Taiwan independence and cross-strait peace are irreconcilable** — is intended to set the stakes for September negotiations: the price of the diplomatic track is continued US ambiguity on Taiwan's status. The Trump administration's willingness to adopt the "negotiating chip" framing suggests Xi's strategic messaging is having its intended effect.
