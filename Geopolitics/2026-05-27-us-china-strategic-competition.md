## Summary
The rivalry between the United States and China has emerged as the dominant axis of 21st-century geopolitics, encompassing trade, technology, military posture, currency competition, and ideological contest. Unlike the Cold War's US-Soviet binary, the US-China relationship is defined by deep economic interdependence simultaneously entangled with strategic antagonism — making a full decoupling impossible but continued integration increasingly untenable. As of 2026, the competition has intensified across every dimension with Taiwan remaining the highest-stakes flashpoint.

## Key Points
- US-China bilateral trade exceeds $500B/year despite significant tariff barriers — economic interdependence persists even as strategic decoupling advances in key sectors
- The tech war — semiconductors, AI, quantum computing — has become the central front, with the US restricting chip exports and China racing toward self-sufficiency
- Taiwan is the most dangerous flashpoint: China views reunification as a core national objective; the US maintains strategic ambiguity about its defence obligation
- China has built the world's largest navy by vessel count; the US retains superior carrier strike capability and global basing network
- Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) has given China economic leverage across Africa, Central Asia, and Southeast Asia; the US and allies are countering with alternative infrastructure programs
- The relationship follows the "Thucydides Trap" dynamic: a rising power (China) challenging a ruling power (US) has historically led to war in 12 of 16 historical cases (per Graham Allison's analysis)

### Historical Background

**The Rise of China:**
After Deng Xiaoping's 1978 "Reform and Opening Up," China sustained 30+ years of ~10% annual GDP growth, lifting 800 million people out of poverty and becoming the world's largest manufacturer and second-largest economy. Under Hu Jintao (2003–2013), China began quietly building military capacity while maintaining a low profile ("hide your strength, bide your time" — Deng's dictum).

The strategic shift came under **Xi Jinping** (2013–present), who consolidated power (eliminating presidential term limits in 2018), launched "Made in China 2025" to dominate 10 strategic industries, escalated in the South China Sea, and began openly asserting China's model as an alternative to Western liberal democracy.

**US-China Engagement Era (1972–2016):**
The Nixon-Kissinger opening to China in 1972, and China's WTO accession in 2001, were premised on the "liberal convergence" hypothesis — that trade integration and economic development would produce political liberalisation. By the mid-2010s, the bipartisan Washington consensus concluded this bet had failed: China had grown powerful without converging toward democracy, and its economic practices (subsidies, IP theft, forced technology transfer) were undercutting US industry.

**The Trump Era and Tariff War (2018–2020):**
The Trump administration broke with 40 years of engagement doctrine, imposing sweeping tariffs on $360B of Chinese goods. China retaliated. Phase One trade deal (2020) paused escalation but left structural issues unresolved.

**Biden Continuation and Chip Controls (2021–2024):**
The Biden administration largely maintained Trump's tariffs while building a multilateral approach — AUKUS (US-UK-Australia security pact), CHIPS Act ($52B for domestic semiconductor manufacturing), and October 2022 export controls on advanced semiconductors that the Netherlands and Japan later joined. The controls targeted Nvidia's A100/H100 chips and the equipment to make leading-edge chips (ASML EUV machines).

### The Technology War

The semiconductor conflict is the most consequential dimension. The logic:
- Advanced semiconductors are the "oil of the digital age" — necessary for AI, missiles, surveillance, manufacturing automation
- TSMC (Taiwan) produces ~90% of the world's most advanced chips; TSMC, Samsung, and Intel are the only fabs capable of <5nm production
- The US controls critical chokepoints: chip design software (Synopsys, Cadence), certain materials, and advanced packaging — creating leverage over the entire global semiconductor supply chain

**China's response:**
- Massive state investment in SMIC (China's leading fab) — as of 2026, SMIC has achieved 7nm production but remains 2–3 generations behind TSMC's leading edge
- Huawei's "Kirin" chip surprise in 2023 showed China can work around some restrictions
- China controls ~80% of global rare earth processing — a potential counter-leverage card
- "AI self-sufficiency" declared a national security priority; Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent, and dedicated AI champions (SenseTime, Zhipu AI) racing to develop alternatives to US models

### The Taiwan Question

Taiwan sits at the intersection of every thread of US-China competition:
- **Economic**: TSMC is globally irreplaceable; a Taiwan conflict would immediately trigger a global chip shortage dwarfing 2020–2022
- **Military**: Taiwan is 100 miles from China's coast; the PLA has developed a "joint blockade and invasion" doctrine; US commitment requires projecting power across the Pacific
- **Symbolic**: Xi has explicitly called reunification a "historical mission"; for the CCP, it represents completing China's "century of humiliation"

**US strategic ambiguity**: Washington sells defensive weapons to Taiwan (Taiwan Relations Act, 1979) but has not made a treaty commitment to defend it. Multiple times since 2021, US presidents have appeared to commit to defending Taiwan before aides walked back the statements.

**Cross-Strait military balance** (2026):
- China has achieved local air superiority within 500 miles of its coast
- The PLA Navy is the world's largest by number of ships; US maintains carrier advantage
- US bases in Guam, Japan, South Korea, and Philippines form the first and second island chains
- War games (Rand, CSIS) suggest a Taiwan conflict would be costly for all parties and the outcome remains uncertain

### The South China Sea

China claims ~90% of the South China Sea via the "nine-dash line" — rejected by a 2016 UN tribunal ruling that China ignores. China has built and militarised artificial islands (Spratly and Paracel chains) with runways, radar, and missile systems. The sea carries $5 trillion in annual trade. The Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan all have competing claims. US freedom of navigation operations regularly challenge Chinese claims.

### Economic and Financial Dimensions

**Decoupling vs. De-risking:**
Full decoupling is economically suicidal for both sides. "De-risking" — removing dependence in strategically critical sectors while maintaining trade in non-sensitive goods — is the dominant US/EU policy framework as of 2026.

**Currency competition:**
China is internationalising the yuan (RMB) through bilateral trade agreements, digital yuan (e-CNY), and expanding CIPS (China's alternative to SWIFT). BRICS+ countries have explored alternatives to dollar settlement. The dollar's reserve currency status — and its associated "exorbitant privilege" — is a long-term battleground.

**Belt and Road Initiative (BRI):**
Launched 2013, BRI has deployed ~$1 trillion in infrastructure lending across 150+ countries. Critics note the debt diplomacy dimensions ("debt trap"). Several countries have renegotiated loans or seen Chinese-built ports face operational difficulties. The US/G7 countered with the Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment (PGII).

### Current Status (2026)

As of mid-2026, US-China relations remain in a state of "managed strategic rivalry":
- Regular military-to-military communication channels have been partially restored after being severed in 2022 (Pelosi Taiwan visit)
- AI governance dialogues have begun but produced little binding agreement
- Tariffs remain at elevated levels on both sides
- Taiwan Strait tensions remain elevated with regular PLA military exercises
- The US is deepening security relationships with Philippines, Japan (historic expansion of Japanese military capability), South Korea, India (Quad), and AUKUS partners
- China is deepening ties with Russia, Iran, and working to strengthen BRICS+ as an alternative pole

### Why This Matters

The outcome of US-China competition will determine:
- The governance model of the global internet and AI (open vs. state-controlled)
- The future of Taiwan's democracy and self-governance
- Whether the rules-based international order (UN, WTO, IMF) remains US-anchored or fractures into spheres of influence
- The pace of global economic growth (any Taiwan conflict would trigger a depression)
- The future of dollar hegemony and global financial architecture

Both sides appear to view AI governance talks more as a communication channel to reduce misunderstanding than as a path to enforceable rules.

## Cross-Disciplinary Connections

### Currency Competition, Dollar Dominance, and the Long Game of Financial Architecture

The US-China strategic rivalry plays out with particular intensity in the currency and financial system arena, where structural power is harder to observe than military force but more durably consequential. The dollar's reserve currency status — underpinned by the depth of US Treasury markets, the rule of law in American financial institutions, and the network effects of dollar-denominated commodity pricing — represents an asymmetric advantage that China has spent two decades attempting to erode. The renminbi's internationalization has been deliberate but slow: RMB's share of global payments (per SWIFT data) remains below 4%, compared to the dollar's ~42%, reflecting the fundamental tension between China's capital controls (necessary for domestic financial stability and CCP control over credit allocation) and the openness required for a credible reserve currency. The digital yuan (e-CNY) is an attempt to thread this needle — enabling international RMB payments through a state-controlled digital infrastructure that avoids full capital account liberalization — but as of 2026, adoption outside China has been limited primarily to bilateral arrangements with economies highly dependent on Chinese investment.

The May 2026 tariff truce (US tariffs from ~145% to ~30%; Chinese tariffs on US goods to ~10%) reveals important asymmetries in currency dynamics. A 30% tariff on Chinese goods is effectively a negative supply shock to US consumers, generating inflationary pressure that the Federal Reserve must weigh against growth concerns — a situation that constrains Fed policy independence and represents a form of indirect Chinese influence over US monetary conditions. Meanwhile, the yuan's managed float means China can absorb some tariff impact through controlled depreciation, though the PBOC faces its own constraints: excessive depreciation would trigger capital flight, which is why China's foreign exchange reserves (still approximately $3.2 trillion) function partly as a credibility buffer for the managed exchange rate regime. For fixed-income investors, this dynamic is captured in the yield spread between US Treasuries and Chinese government bonds — a spread that has widened and narrowed with trade war escalations and truces in ways that directly reflect geopolitical risk pricing by bond markets.

### AI Supremacy as the Decisive Front

The competition's technology dimension maps precisely onto machine learning fundamentals in ways that both sides understand with unusual clarity. China's "New Generation AI Development Plan" (2017) established explicit national targets for AI leadership by 2030, treating AI capability as the determining factor in 21st-century economic and military power. This strategic clarity is well-founded: the scaling laws governing large language models and multi-modal AI systems mean that compute availability (a function of chip access and energy infrastructure), data quality and scale (where China's large population and limited privacy regulation provide structural advantages), and talent density (a function of educational investment and migration policy) determine who leads. The US semiconductor export controls are an attempt to block the compute axis — the one China cannot easily substitute domestically at frontier levels. China's counter-strategy has been to pursue efficiency-focused architectures that achieve competitive capability at lower compute levels. Huawei's investment in domestic AI chips (Ascend series) and Chinese researchers' focus on model distillation and efficient training represent a genuine effort to compete at lower compute — with uncertain long-term success.

The implications extend to the transformer architecture that underlies virtually all frontier AI systems. The attention mechanism — first described in the 2017 "Attention Is All You Need" paper by Google researchers — is not subject to export controls, but the compute to implement transformers at frontier scale is. This creates an unusual situation where scientific knowledge is freely shared (the papers are published openly) but the industrial capability to exploit it at scale is a controlled resource — a distinction that has no precedent in prior technology competitions.

### The BRI as Applied Macroeconomics and the Limits of Debt Diplomacy

China's Belt and Road Initiative can be analyzed through the lens of development economics with unusual precision. The BRI's model — state-directed lending by China Development Bank and Export-Import Bank of China for infrastructure projects in recipient countries, often with Chinese labor and materials — follows a pattern closer to mercantile economics than the Bretton Woods development finance model. The World Bank and IMF lend in dollars on standardized concessional terms with governance conditions; China lends in RMB or dollars on project-specific terms, typically requiring resource offtake agreements or port access rights as collateral. The "debt trap" critique (exemplified by Hambantota Port in Sri Lanka, ceded to a Chinese company on a 99-year lease after Sri Lanka defaulted) overstates the degree of deliberate strategic planning — many BRI projects reflected local political corruption and poor project selection rather than a coordinated Beijing strategy. Nevertheless, the infrastructure investments create genuine switching costs: once a country's rail network, port operations, or power grid depends on Chinese equipment and maintenance contracts, changing providers involves enormous transition costs, creating the kind of lock-in effects familiar from platform economics. The EU's Global Gateway initiative and the US-led Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment (PGII) represent belated attempts to offer a competitive alternative — but they face the same structural challenge that private capital faces vs. state-directed lending: private investors require commercial returns on infrastructure projects that often have long payback periods and public-good characteristics that private finance systematically undervalues.

### Negotiation Theory and the "Two-Level Game" in US-China Diplomacy

The Trump-Xi summit dynamic and the trade truce process illustrates Robert Putnam's "two-level game" theory of international negotiations with remarkable clarity. Each leader is simultaneously negotiating internationally (with the other side) and domestically (with their own political base). Trump faces a Republican Party that views China as an existential threat and would regard significant concessions as weakness; Xi faces a CCP system that has staked nationalist legitimacy on resistance to American hegemony and cannot publicly accept US "decoupling" narrative without losing face. The result is a pattern familiar from repeated negotiations: public toughness, back-channel pragmatism, and deals framed in domestically compatible language. The "minor inconsistencies" in US and Chinese summit readouts documented by NPR are not a diplomatic failure — they are a deliberate feature of the two-level game, where each side interprets the same agreement in the terms most acceptable to its domestic audience.

The inclusion of processing equipment in the agreement signals that China's rare earth leverage extends to the entire supply chain, not just the minerals themselves. The first Virginia-class submarines sold to Australia under the arrangement are expected to transfer in the late 2020s. China's repeated condemnation of AUKUS as "nuclear proliferation" reflects Beijing's assessment of its strategic impact.

The amounts remain small relative to global trade — estimated at $5–10 billion in transactions in 2025, compared to the dollar's multi-trillion global settlement role — but the institutional infrastructure is being systematically built. The US Treasury and SWIFT have publicly expressed concern that the mBridge platform (connecting central bank digital currencies across multiple Asian states) could eventually provide the architecture for dollar-circumventing settlements at a scale that current RMB internationalization cannot achieve. This is a long-term structural risk to dollar hegemony, not an immediate challenge.

### Saturday Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis: Strategic Competition Mechanisms and Instruments

**Connection to Game Theory — Two-Level Games and Domestic Constraints:**
Robert Putnam's (1988) "two-level games" framework explains why US-China negotiations consistently fail to produce durable agreements: each party's negotiators operate simultaneously at the international level (bilateral deal) and the domestic level (domestic political ratification). For the US, any China deal must survive congressional scrutiny (bipartisan China hawkishness), business lobby review (competing between market-access-seekers and decoupling-advocates), and alliance management (ensuring Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan are not undermined). For China, any agreement must survive Politburo validation, nationalist public sentiment management, and PLA institutional interests. The "win-set" — the set of international agreements that can ratify domestically on both sides — has been shrinking since 2017 as domestic political constraints have tightened in both countries, explaining why the structural incentives for US-China economic cooperation (mutual gains from trade) have not translated into stable agreements.

**Connection to International Law — Sanctions Architecture and Legal Extraterritoriality:**
US sanctions extraterritoriality (applying US law to non-US companies doing business with sanctioned entities) is one of the most contested areas of international law. The EU, China, Russia, and most of the global south formally reject US extraterritorial jurisdiction — but comply in practice because the dollar's centrality in global finance makes exclusion from the US financial system commercially catastrophic for most global companies. The 2024 EU-China dispute over secondary sanctions (EU companies facing US OFAC enforcement for China transactions) is producing pressure on EU legal framework development: the EU has a "blocking statute" (Regulation 2271/96) theoretically protecting EU companies from US secondary sanctions compliance, but enforcement is inconsistently applied. The broader legal question: whether the dollar's role as global reserve currency constitutes a form of ongoing legal extraterritoriality that is incompatible with sovereign equality under international law — a question that drives de-dollarization efforts in BRICS states.

**Connection to History — Imperial Transition Risks:**
Every hegemonic transition in history (Athenian vs. Spartan, Dutch vs. British, British vs. American) has either involved direct conflict or near-conflict — the "Thucydides Trap" (Allison, 2017). Of 16 historical cases where a rising power challenged a ruling one, 12 resulted in war. The 4 that avoided war (most recently, the British-American transition, 1895–1945) share common factors: genuine cultural and institutional affinity, absence of an acute crisis triggering mobilization, and shared security threats that created incentive for cooperation over confrontation. The US-China case has none of these pacifying factors: deep cultural divergence, an acute Taiwan crisis catalyst, and no shared external threat. This historical pattern analysis suggests the current trajectory is more likely to involve some form of conflict than not — though the nuclear deterrence factor (absent in all pre-nuclear historical transitions) may be sufficient to prevent major war while not preventing proxy conflicts, sanctions wars, and gray zone operations.

### Update — June 6, 2026: The Japan-Philippines Reciprocal Access Agreement, Trade Truce Structure, and Xi-Putin Moscow Meeting

**Japan-Philippines Reciprocal Access Agreement — Quadrilateral Security Architecture:**
On **May 19, 2026**, Japan and the Philippines signed a **Reciprocal Access Agreement (RAA)** — the first such bilateral defense agreement between Japan and a Southeast Asian state. The RAA allows each country's military forces to enter the other's territory for training and operations, creating a framework for coordinated defense activities without a formal mutual defense treaty. The Japan-Philippines RAA, combined with Japan's existing defense agreement with Australia (2022) and developing agreement with the UK, is part of Japan's deliberate construction of a **quasi-alliance architecture** that extends its defense perimeter south of the Senkaku/Diaoyu Islands into the South China Sea — precisely the area where Philippine-China confrontations over Second Thomas Shoal and Scarborough Shoal are most acute.

The May 19 Japan-South Korea **trilateral security summit** (US-Japan-South Korea) — held the same day — reinforced the emerging pattern of interlocking bilateral and trilateral security arrangements across the Indo-Pacific that collectively function as an integrated deterrence architecture even without a formal treaty. China's response: strong condemnation of what it characterized as "bloc confrontation" and "Cold War mentality." The simultaneous Japan-Philippines and US-Japan-ROK institutional strengthening represents the most significant structural expansion of US-aligned security architecture in the Indo-Pacific since the 2021 AUKUS agreement.

**Xi-Putin Moscow Meeting (May 20, 2026) — No Pipeline Deal:**
Xi Jinping and Vladimir Putin met in Moscow on **May 20, 2026**, reaffirming the "comprehensive strategic partnership" and issuing a joint statement on the need for "multipolar world order." However, the **Power of Siberia-2 pipeline deal** — Russia's most important infrastructure initiative to route Siberian natural gas to China — **failed again** to reach agreement. China has been deliberately slow-walking the Power of Siberia-2 negotiations since 2022, using Russia's energy export isolation to extract maximum price concessions. Russia's financial need to lock in long-term gas sales to China has not been matched by China's willingness to pay prices that make the pipeline economically viable for Russia.

The stalled Power of Siberia-2 deal is analytically significant: it demonstrates that the Sino-Russian "no limits partnership" (declared February 2022) has limits precisely defined by China's economic interests. China buys Russian oil at a $10–15/barrel discount; Russia has no alternative buyer with comparable capacity. Xi is extracting maximum economic rents from the relationship while providing diplomatic and rhetorical support — the clearest evidence that the relationship is asymmetrically favorable to China, with Russia as the supplicant rather than the equal partner the "no limits" framing implies.

**Rare Earth Counter-Leverage — China's Most Powerful Economic Weapon:**
China controls approximately **80% of global rare earth element processing** — the refining capacity that converts raw ore into the neodymium, dysprosium, and lanthanum compounds used in wind turbines, EV motors, precision-guided weapons, and semiconductor manufacturing. Unlike oil (for which multiple suppliers exist), rare earth processing capacity is highly concentrated in China because it is environmentally damaging, labor-intensive, and requires decades of industrial expertise to establish. In response to US semiconductor export controls, China has progressively signaled willingness to restrict rare earth exports to the US and allies: **export licensing requirements** were placed on gallium and germanium (June 2023), antimony (September 2024), and graphite (December 2024). In March 2026, China placed **export controls on 31 additional rare earth-related materials** — the most sweeping exercise of rare earth leverage to date. The US, Japan, EU, and Australia are racing to establish alternative processing capacity (primarily in Australia, Canada, and the US), but estimates suggest building genuine processing supply chain independence will take **10–15 years** and cost $50–100 billion in industrial investment.

### Update — June 8, 2026: Trade Truce Expiration Clock, mBridge Expansion, and AUKUS Submarine Milestone

**The 90-Day Trade Truce Expiration — August 2026 Decision Point:**

The Chatham House assessment ("managing rivalry, not resolving it") captures the structural condition: the truce is a mechanism for temporizing, not for resolving the underlying competition.

**mBridge Platform and e-CNY International Architecture:**
The PBOC's digital yuan (e-CNY) international deployment is advancing through the **mBridge platform** — a multi-central-bank digital currency arrangement connecting Saudi Arabia, UAE, Hong Kong, Thailand, and China through a shared distributed ledger for cross-border settlement. In 2025, mBridge processed an estimated $5–10B in transactions; the Q1 2026 figure is running ahead of the full-year 2025 total. Pakistan joined the mBridge pilot framework in April 2026 — the first South Asian participant. The strategic significance: mBridge creates a dollar-bypass settlement infrastructure that is state-controlled (unlike Bitcoin or stablecoins), interoperable between Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs), and designed from inception to operate outside SWIFT. The BIS (which previously co-facilitated mBridge's development) partially withdrew from the project in 2024, citing governance concerns — reflecting Western concern that mBridge is evolving from a neutral infrastructure project into a Chinese geopolitical instrument. The US Treasury's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) has flagged mBridge as a potential vehicle for US sanctions circumvention — the most direct official US government acknowledgment that the platform represents a structural threat to dollar-based financial infrastructure.

**AUKUS Submarine Cooperation — Defense Technology Transfer Milestone:**
AUKUS Pillar I submarine cooperation — the US-UK-Australia nuclear-powered submarine arrangement — hit a significant milestone in May–June 2026: Australian AUKUS personnel are now **embedded at both US submarine bases** (Naval Submarine Base New London, Connecticut and Naval Base Kitsap, Washington) conducting full integration training, and the **legal frameworks** for Virginia-class submarine transfer to Australia in the late 2020s have been finalized. This is the most consequential military technology transfer since the US extended its nuclear umbrella to Western Europe in the 1950s — representing a decision to share naval nuclear propulsion technology (the most sensitive non-weapons nuclear technology) with a close ally for explicit strategic competition purposes against China. China's characterization of AUKUS as "nuclear proliferation" is legally unfounded (nuclear-powered submarines are not nuclear weapons) but strategically coherent: AUKUS directly addresses China's A2/AD strategy in the Indo-Pacific by introducing a long-endurance, stealthy submarine platform into the theater that China's anti-submarine warfare cannot currently reliably detect. The timing — advancing through AUKUS milestones simultaneously with the US-China trade truce — illustrates the dual-track US approach: economic normalization in trade while deepening the security architecture that contains Chinese military expansion.

**War on the Rocks Assessment — Semiconductor Controls vs. Rare Earth Leverage:**
War on the Rocks' analysis ("The Burn and the Choke") provides the most analytically precise framework for assessing which side holds the stronger long-term position in the strategic technology competition. The "burn" (US semiconductor controls) is a slow-acting structural constraint: China can access 7nm chips at significant efficiency cost for 2–3 years, but the gap compounds over time as US frontier chips advance to 2nm and below. The "choke" (Chinese rare earth controls) is rapid-acting: rare earth magnet supply interruption affects defense production within weeks, not years. But the asymmetry favors the US in the long run: semiconductor capability compounds; rare earth substitution (synthetic magnets, alternative materials, processing capacity expansion in Australia and Canada) is technically achievable within 10–15 years. The War on the Rocks conclusion: China's rare earth leverage is the most powerful near-term tool, but the semiconductor controls are the most consequential long-term weapon — and both sides understand this.
