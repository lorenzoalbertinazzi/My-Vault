## Summary

Geopolitical risk — the threat of adverse consequences arising from political acts, conflicts, and disruptions in the international system — generates a measurable "geopolitical risk premium" embedded across virtually all financial asset classes. The 2020s have witnessed a structural regime shift in geopolitical risk: from the "end of history" low-volatility environment of 1991–2016 to a persistent high-risk multipolar world characterized by great power competition, regional wars, sanctions-based financial warfare, and supply chain fragmentation. Understanding how geopolitical risk translates into asset prices requires integrating geopolitical analysis with financial modeling — a discipline increasingly central to institutional investment management. The Geopolitical Risk (GPR) Index, developed by Caldara and Iacoviello at the Federal Reserve, provides a quantitative measure showing that GPR spikes correlate with investment declines, flight-to-quality dynamics, and commodity price volatility, with asymmetric effects across asset classes and geographies. The 2022–2026 period — spanning Russia's Ukraine invasion, the Iran conflict, the India-Pakistan crisis, and Venezuela's US intervention — offers an extraordinary real-world laboratory for geopolitical risk pricing.

## Key Points

- **GPR Index spikes** of 100+ points (from baseline of ~100) correlate with 3–5% equity market declines and significant commodity price volatility within 30 days
- **Oil is the primary geopolitical transmission mechanism**: Every $10/barrel increase in oil price lowers global GDP by ~0.1–0.5% (IMF estimates vary by economy's oil dependence)
- **Sanctions weaponization** has transformed financial risk: Russia's $300 billion reserve freeze (2022) demonstrated that sovereign reserves are not safe from geopolitical confiscation
- **Flight-to-quality hierarchy**: US Treasuries → German Bunds → Swiss Franc → Gold → JPY is the standard safe-haven cascade during geopolitical shocks
- **Emerging market vulnerability**: EM sovereign spreads widen 100–300 bps during major geopolitical shocks; currencies depreciate 5–15%
- **Country Risk Premium formula**: CRP = (Spread on sovereign bonds) × (Equity market volatility / Bond market volatility)
- **Supply chain geopoliticization**: Companies pay an estimated 15–25% premium for "friend-shoring" vs. lowest-cost sourcing — a structural geopolitical risk pricing in corporate margins
- **MSCI War Premium**: MSCI has documented that countries within 500 miles of active conflict face a persistent 2–4 percentage point discount on equity valuations

## Details

### Theoretical Foundations: Why Geopolitical Risk Has a Price

**Expected Value Framework**  
Financial assets are claims on future cash flows. Geopolitical risk reduces expected cash flows (through physical destruction, disruption, confiscation) and increases the uncertainty of remaining cash flows, which demands a higher discount rate. The fundamental valuation equation:

```
P = Σ [E(CF_t) / (1 + r + GRP)^t]
```

Where:
- P = asset price
- E(CF_t) = expected cash flow in period t
- r = risk-free rate
- GRP = geopolitical risk premium (additional discount rate demanded)

GRP is not directly observable but can be estimated by comparison: the yield spread between a geopolitically exposed asset and a comparable "safe" asset.

**The Caldara-Iacoviello GPR Index**  
Dario Caldara and Matteo Iacoviello (Federal Reserve Board) developed the Geopolitical Risk Index by counting articles related to geopolitical tensions and uncertainty in major newspapers (Washington Post, New York Times, Wall Street Journal, The Times, Financial Times, Chicago Tribune, LA Times, Miami Herald) as a share of total news articles. The index spans 1900–present.

Key properties:
- Baseline (1985–2019 average): ~100
- Korean War peak: ~500
- Cuban Missile Crisis: ~600
- 9/11: ~400
- Iraq War 2003: ~250
- COVID-19 (initial, 2020): ~120
- Russia-Ukraine invasion (Feb 2022): ~450
- 2026 sustained level: ~280–350 (elevated baseline due to multiple concurrent conflicts)

Their research finds:
- 1 standard deviation increase in GPR → 1.5% decline in real investment
- 1 standard deviation increase in GPR → 1% decline in employment growth
- GPR effects are larger in emerging markets than advanced economies
- Higher uncertainty about geopolitical events (GPT index) matters more than actual events

### Asset Class Analysis: How GRP Manifests

**Equities**  
Geopolitical shocks affect equities through multiple channels:

*Direct exposure*:
- Companies with operations in conflict zones face physical asset risk, supply chain disruption, demand destruction
- Defense contractors gain (Raytheon, Lockheed Martin, BAE Systems — all reached all-time highs post-Ukraine invasion)

*Indirect exposure*:
- Input cost inflation (energy, commodities) compresses margins
- Supply chain disruption (semiconductor shortage post-Ukraine/Taiwan tensions)
- Consumer confidence decline reduces demand

*Market-wide repricing*:
- VIX (equity volatility index) spikes during geopolitical events; each 10-point VIX increase corresponds approximately to a 2–3% equity decline
- P/E multiple compression: Higher uncertainty → lower willingness to pay for future earnings

**Empirical case study — Russia-Ukraine invasion (Feb 24, 2022)**:
- MSCI World: -5% in the week following invasion
- European equities (STOXX 600): -7% in one week; German DAX -8% (German energy exposure to Russian gas)
- Russian MOEX index: -33% in a single day (Feb 24); halted for 3+ weeks
- S&P 500: Surprisingly resilient — fell ~3% initially but recovered within 2 weeks (US less energy-dependent on Russia)

**Bonds and Sovereign Credit**  
Sovereign bonds of affected countries see yield spikes (price declines) as credit risk increases. The spread relative to "risk-free" benchmark (US Treasuries for dollar-denominated bonds, German Bunds for euro-denominated) is the "country risk premium" or "sovereign spread."

Sovereign Spread Analysis:
- Russia spreads: Pre-invasion ~200 bps; post-invasion → effective default (sanctions made debt service impossible); bonds trading at 10–15 cents on dollar
- Ukraine spreads: Pre-invasion ~700 bps; post-invasion → 3,500+ bps (near-default territory)
- Nearby unaffected countries: Poland, Czech Republic saw 50–100 bps widening (contagion)

**Country Risk Premium Formula (Damodaran method)**:
```
Country Risk Premium = Sovereign Spread × (σ_equity / σ_bonds)
```

Where σ_equity and σ_bonds are the standard deviations of the equity market and bond market respectively. Example for Ukraine 2022:
- Sovereign spread: 3,500 bps = 35%
- σ_equity / σ_bonds: ~1.5 (equity more volatile)
- CRP: 35% × 1.5 = 52.5%
- This CRP is added to the equity cost of capital for Ukrainian companies — making most investments economically inviable

**Commodities: The Primary Transmission Belt**  
Commodities are the most direct channel from geopolitical disruption to economic impact:

*Oil*:
- The world consumes ~102 million barrels/day (2026)
- Supply disruptions → immediate price spike (inelastic short-run demand)
- Rule of thumb: $10/barrel oil price increase → 0.3–0.5% global GDP reduction (IMF, 2022)
- Historical geopolitical oil spikes:
  - 1973 Yom Kippur War/OPEC embargo: Oil 4x in 4 months
  - 1979 Iranian Revolution: Oil 2x
  - 1990 Gulf War: Oil 2x briefly
  - 2022 Russia-Ukraine: Brent from $76 to $128 (March 2022 peak)
  - 2025 Iran-US conflict: Brent briefly above $130; Strait of Hormuz closure risk premium spiked

*Natural Gas*:
- Europe's dependence on Russian gas (40% of supply pre-2022) made it uniquely vulnerable
- TTF natural gas: €25/MWh (Jan 2022) → €350/MWh (Aug 2022) — 14-fold spike
- Triggered European industrial shutdown, demand destruction, manufacturing relocation

*Wheat and Food*:
- Russia and Ukraine together: ~30% of global wheat exports
- Post-invasion, wheat futures: +60% in 3 months
- 15+ countries dependent on Ukraine/Russia wheat (Egypt, Lebanon, Libya, Yemen, Somalia)
- Food price inflation triggered social unrest in North Africa and Middle East (echoing 2011 Arab Spring food price dynamics)

*Precious Metals*:
- Gold is the archetypal geopolitical safe haven: rises during fear periods
- Gold +8% in week following Russia-Ukraine invasion
- Gold +12% during Iran-US crisis (2025)
- Quantitative relationship: Each 100-point increase in GPR Index → approximately 2.5% gold price increase (multiple studies)

**Currencies**  
Geopolitical shocks trigger predictable currency movements:

*Safe-haven currencies strengthen*:
- USD: World's reserve currency; flight-to-safety demand
- CHF (Swiss Franc): Traditional neutral safe haven
- JPY (Japanese Yen): Carry trade unwind during fear spikes

*Risk currencies weaken*:
- Commodity exporters' currencies: CAD, AUD, BRL move with commodity prices (can be positive if commodity exporter gains from price spike)
- EM currencies broadly: Capital flight from EM to DM (developed markets) during geopolitical stress

*Directly impacted currencies collapse*:
- Russian Ruble: Lost 40% vs. USD in first week after invasion; recovered partially after capital controls imposed
- Lebanese Pound: Collapsed under combined economic crisis and Israeli invasion pressure
- Ukrainian Hryvnia: Defended through NBU capital controls

**The Currency Hierarchy of Safety (Risk-Off Cascade)**:
```
Level 1 (Safest): USD, German Bunds, US Treasuries
Level 2: CHF, JPY, Gold
Level 3: GBP, EUR (less safe than before Eurozone crisis)
Level 4: AUD, CAD, NZD (commodity currencies)
Level 5: EM currencies (most vulnerable)
```

### Sanctions as Geopolitical Risk Mechanism

**Sanctions Architecture and Financial Impact**  
Modern financial sanctions are far more sophisticated and devastating than historical trade embargoes. The US Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) administers one of the world's most powerful financial weapons:

*Types*:
- *Blocking sanctions*: Target's assets within US jurisdiction frozen; US persons prohibited from transactions
- *Secondary sanctions*: Third-country entities penalized for dealing with sanctioned parties (extraterritorial reach)
- *Sectoral sanctions*: Restrict specific economic activities (financing, technology transfer) in targeted sectors
- *Correspondent banking sanctions*: Cut target country's banks from USD clearing (SWIFT exclusion)

*Russia 2022 as Case Study*:
- $300 billion in Russian central bank reserves (held in G7 jurisdictions) frozen within days
- Russia's 7 largest banks excluded from SWIFT
- Individual oligarch asset freezes: ~$50 billion in yachts, properties seized globally
- Technology export controls: No US chips, no US software, no aircraft parts

*Impact*: Russian GDP contracted 2.2% in 2022 (less than feared due to energy windfall and Chinese support); inflation 12%; long-term technological degradation from component shortages.

**The Sovereign Reserve Safety Illusion Shattered**  
The freeze of Russia's central bank reserves demonstrated that sovereign foreign currency reserves are not unconditionally safe:
- Previously, FX reserves were considered inviolable — their safety was the premise of the reserve currency system
- After February 2022, every central bank globally reassessed the safety of dollar/euro reserves
- China began accelerating diversification into gold (PBOC gold purchases: record levels 2022–2026)
- Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Gulf states quietly diversified away from US Treasuries
- India, Brazil, and other large EM countries began conducting more bilateral trade in national currencies
- Gold has emerged as the only truly "neutral" reserve asset — cannot be frozen by a foreign government

**Sanctions' Diminishing Returns**  
The effectiveness of US financial sanctions is declining structurally:
- Russia-China-India trade bypasses USD (settlements in RMB, INR)
- mBridge provides non-SWIFT settlement infrastructure
- Crypto (despite limitations) offers some sanctions evasion for small transfers
- "Reverse dollarization": Central banks reducing dollar reserve share
- IMF: Dollar share of global reserves fell from 72% (2000) to 58% (2025) — a significant structural decline

### Supply Chain Geopoliticization: A New Structural Risk

**Friend-Shoring Premium**  
The COVID pandemic exposed the risks of concentrated supply chains in potentially adversarial countries (primarily China). Post-Ukraine, post-Taiwan tensions, companies began paying "insurance premiums" for geopolitically safer supply chains:

*Reshoring/nearshoring cost estimates*:
- Manufacturing relocation from China to US: +40–60% production cost premium
- Manufacturing relocation from China to Mexico (nearshoring): +15–25% premium
- Manufacturing relocation from China to Vietnam/India: +5–15% premium
- McKinsey estimate: Companies globally spending $1.5–4 trillion on supply chain resilience investments (2023–2027)

This supply chain premiumization is a structural inflation driver built into corporate margins — part of why 2022–2026 inflation is "stickier" than post-2008.

*Semiconductor supply chains*:
- Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC): Makes ~90% of world's most advanced chips (under 5nm); located 180km from China
- Taiwan Strait risk premium is priced into semiconductor stocks and tech sector broadly
- Intel, Samsung, TSMC itself investing $100+ billion in US and European fabs to diversify
- The "CHIPS Act" ($52 billion US government) and EU Chips Act (€43 billion) are government-level geopolitical risk hedging

### Measurement and Quantification Tools

**The GPR Index (Caldara-Iacoviello)**  
Available at: geopoliticalrisk.com; Federal Reserve working paper 2022-2-1. Monthly data from 1985; quarterly from 1900.

Key empirical findings from their VAR (Vector Auto-Regression) models:
- 1 standard deviation GPR shock → -1.5% real investment (peak effect at 4 months)
- 1 standard deviation GPR shock → -0.7% employment (6 months)
- Effects larger for: Emerging markets, high-commodity-dependence countries, countries with high trade openness

**BlackRock Geopolitical Risk Dashboard**  
BlackRock's Investment Institute quantifies geopolitical risk along two dimensions:
- *Likelihood* of a geopolitical event
- *Market attention* (how much is already priced in)
- Investment implication: High-likelihood + low-attention events are most dangerous for portfolios
- Methodology: Combines GPR-type text analysis with options pricing (implied volatility pricing in specific risks)

**MSCI Political Risk Ratings**  
MSCI assigns political risk ratings (0–100) to 165 countries, incorporated into market access classifications. Countries with high political risk are excluded from MSCI Developed Market indices, reducing their access to passive capital flows. Political risk rating deterioration → index weighting reduction → forced selling by index-tracking funds → market impact.

**Sovereign CDS (Credit Default Swaps)**  
5-year sovereign CDS spreads are the real-time market price of sovereign default risk, incorporating geopolitical risk:
- Ukraine CDS: 500 bps pre-invasion → 4,000+ bps post-invasion
- Russia CDS: 200 bps → effectively infinity (no deliverable after sanctions/default)
- Iran CDS: Not actively traded (no investable market)
- Venezuela CDS: Already trading at 20,000+ bps pre-Operation Absolute Resolve; post-intervention, restructuring dynamics

### Portfolio Construction Under Geopolitical Risk

**Geopolitical Hedging Strategies**  

*Direct hedges*:
- Long gold (negative correlation with geopolitical risk stocks; positive correlation with GPR spikes)
- Long energy equities/commodities (if conflict involves oil exporters)
- Long defense sector equities (RTX, LMT, BAES, Rheinmetall — structural outperformers in high-GPR environments)
- Short EM equities/bonds (flight-to-quality flows hurt EM in risk-off)
- Long USD (reserve currency demand)
- Long options on oil (call options benefit from upward price spikes)

*Thematic positioning*:
- Defense and security technology (cybersecurity, drone manufacturers, satellite companies)
- Friend-shoring beneficiaries (Mexican manufacturing, Indian IT, Vietnamese factories)
- Energy independence investments (US LNG exporters, European renewable infrastructure)
- Critical minerals (lithium, cobalt, rare earths — supply chains geopoliticized)

**Geographic Diversification**  
MPT (Modern Portfolio Theory) suggests diversification should reduce unsystematic risk. But geopolitical correlation structures change during crises:
- "Correlation goes to 1 in a crisis" (famously said of all risk assets)
- Geopolitical shocks increase correlations: in a 2022-style European energy crisis, all European assets fell together
- True diversification requires assets with genuinely different geopolitical exposures
- Practical: US domestic equities (insulated from most geopolitical shocks), Swiss assets (traditional neutral), gold

**The Scenario Analysis Approach**  
Institutional investors increasingly use scenario analysis rather than purely statistical approaches for geopolitical risk:
1. Identify key geopolitical scenarios (Taiwan invasion, Middle East war escalation, US-China tech war)
2. Assign probability and timing to each scenario
3. Map scenario impact on each asset class (oil price, rates, credit spreads, FX)
4. Run portfolio through each scenario; calculate P&L
5. Adjust portfolio to limit downside in high-probability adverse scenarios

This approach was pioneered by sovereign wealth funds (GIC, ADIA, Norges Bank) and is now standard at major asset managers.

### 2026 Current Landscape: Elevated Geopolitical Risk Premium

The simultaneous occurrence of Russia-Ukraine war (ongoing), Israel-Gaza conflict, US-Iran 2025 war aftermath, India-Pakistan 2025 crisis, Venezuela intervention, China-Taiwan tensions, and North Korea proliferation has created a structural elevation of the geopolitical risk premium compared to 2010s baseline:

*Observable pricing effects (2025–2026)*:
- Gold at $3,200+/oz (vs. $1,800 in 2022) — structural safe-haven demand
- Defense stocks: MSCI World Aerospace & Defense index +120% since Feb 2022
- Energy sector: Structural premium in energy equities reflecting continued geopolitical tail risk on supply
- EM credit spreads: Elevated vs. pre-2022 baseline by ~100 bps
- USD: Structurally bid despite high US deficits (safe-haven demand offsets)
- Supply chain-diversification capex: A major driver of global capex cycle 2024–2027
