## Summary
The foreign exchange market is the largest and most liquid financial market in the world, with daily trading volumes exceeding $7.5 trillion. FX markets determine exchange rates between currencies and serve as the backbone of international trade, investment, and monetary policy. Understanding how currency markets work is essential for any serious student of global finance.

## Key Points
- The FX market operates 24 hours a day, five days a week, across major financial centers (Sydney, Tokyo, London, New York)
- Major currency pairs (EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD) account for the vast majority of volume
- Exchange rates are driven by interest rate differentials, inflation, trade balances, and market sentiment
- Central banks intervene to manage excessive volatility or maintain competitive exchange rates
- FX risk is a major concern for multinational corporations and international investors
- Carry trades exploit interest rate differentials between currencies

## Details

### How the FX Market Is Structured

The foreign exchange market has no central exchange — it is an over-the-counter (OTC) market where participants trade directly with each other or through electronic platforms. The major participants include:

- **Commercial banks**: The largest players, facilitating client transactions and proprietary trading
- **Central banks**: Conduct monetary policy and intervene to manage exchange rate volatility
- **Corporations**: Hedge FX exposure from international operations and repatriate profits
- **Hedge funds and institutional investors**: Speculate on currency movements or hedge portfolio exposure
- **Retail traders**: A small but growing segment via online brokers

The market is organized into three tiers: the interbank market (the core, where large banks trade with each other), the wholesale market (banks dealing with large institutional clients), and the retail market (small-scale transactions).

### Reading a Currency Quote

Currency prices are always quoted in pairs. The first currency is the **base currency** and the second is the **quote currency**. For example:

- **EUR/USD = 1.0850** means 1 Euro buys 1.0850 US Dollars
- If EUR/USD rises to 1.1000, the Euro has **appreciated** against the Dollar
- The **bid** price is what dealers will buy the base currency for; the **ask** (offer) is what they sell it for
- The **spread** (difference between bid and ask) is the dealer's compensation

**Major pairs** (most liquid, lowest spreads): EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD, USD/CHF, AUD/USD, USD/CAD, NZD/USD

**Cross pairs**: Pairs that don't include the USD, e.g., EUR/JPY, GBP/EUR

**Exotic pairs**: One major currency paired with a currency from an emerging market, e.g., USD/MXN, USD/BRL — higher spreads, lower liquidity

### What Drives Exchange Rates

**1. Interest Rate Differentials**
The most powerful driver of currency movements. When a country raises interest rates, its currency tends to appreciate because higher rates attract foreign capital seeking better returns. This is why central bank decisions (Fed, ECB, Bank of Japan) are closely watched by FX traders.

**2. Inflation**
Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) theory holds that currencies should adjust so that identical goods cost the same across countries. High inflation erodes purchasing power, causing a currency to depreciate over the long run. The Big Mac Index (The Economist) is a simple illustration of PPP.

**3. Current Account and Trade Balance**
A country running a persistent trade surplus (exporting more than it imports) tends to see its currency appreciate, since foreign buyers must purchase the domestic currency to pay for exports. Conversely, trade deficits create selling pressure on the currency.

**4. Economic Growth and Risk Sentiment**
Strong economic growth relative to peers attracts investment and supports a currency. In times of global stress, investors flee to "safe haven" currencies — the USD, CHF, and JPY traditionally strengthen during crises as capital seeks safety.

**5. Political and Geopolitical Risk**
Political instability, elections, and geopolitical shocks cause currency volatility. Markets dislike uncertainty — a currency under geopolitical pressure typically weakens as investors pull capital out.

### Key FX Concepts

**Carry Trade**
Borrowing in a low-interest-rate currency (e.g., Japanese Yen) and investing in a high-interest-rate currency (e.g., Australian Dollar) to capture the interest rate differential. Profitable in stable markets but can unwind violently during risk-off episodes, causing sharp moves in both currencies.

**Currency Pegs and Managed Floats**
Not all currencies float freely. Some countries peg their currency to the USD (e.g., Saudi Riyal, Hong Kong Dollar) or maintain a managed float within a band. Defending a peg requires large foreign exchange reserves and can be attacked by speculators if the market believes the peg is unsustainable (as in the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis or the 1992 ERM crisis when Soros "broke the Bank of England").

**Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) vs. Market Rates**
PPP exchange rates reflect what currencies "should" be worth based on price levels, while market rates reflect supply and demand. Currencies can deviate significantly from PPP for extended periods.

**FX Hedging**
Corporations with international exposure use FX instruments to protect against adverse currency moves:
- **Forward contracts**: Lock in an exchange rate for a future date
- **FX options**: The right (but not obligation) to exchange at a specific rate
- **Currency swaps**: Exchange cash flows in different currencies

### The Role of the US Dollar

The US Dollar is the world's **reserve currency** — the dominant medium for international trade, commodity pricing (oil, gold), and central bank reserves. This gives the US the "exorbitant privilege" of borrowing cheaply and running large deficits. USD strength affects emerging markets significantly: when the dollar strengthens, countries with dollar-denominated debt face higher repayment costs, often triggering crises.

The dollar's reserve status is periodically challenged (by the Euro, by Chinese renminbi ambitions, by crypto narratives) but remains dominant due to the depth of US financial markets, rule of law, and network effects.

### FX Market Dynamics: Volatility and Regime Changes

FX markets can be calm for extended periods, then experience explosive moves. Key catalysts include:
- Surprise central bank decisions (Swiss National Bank removing its Euro peg in 2015 caused the CHF to surge 30% instantly)
- Geopolitical shocks
- Economic data surprises (US non-farm payrolls, CPI prints)
- Emerging market crises (Turkey 2018, Argentina's repeated crises, Russia 2022)

**Currency crises** typically follow a pattern: unsustainable fundamentals (high inflation, large deficits, overvalued exchange rate), speculative attack, reserve depletion, sharp devaluation.

### Practical Applications for Investors

- **International equity investors** must account for FX returns, which can significantly amplify or dampen returns from foreign stocks
- **Bond investors** in foreign debt take both interest rate and currency risk
- **FX as an asset class**: Systematic strategies (momentum, carry, value, quality) can generate returns over time
- **Emerging market investing** requires deep attention to currency dynamics, as EM currencies can be highly volatile
