## Summary
Real assets are physical or tangible investments — real estate, commodities, infrastructure, timberland, farmland — as opposed to financial assets like stocks and bonds. Their defining characteristic is a fundamental link to the physical world, which historically provides **inflation protection** (since prices of physical things tend to rise with general price levels), **low correlation** to financial assets (diversification benefit), and **intrinsic value** not dependent on corporate earnings or credit quality. Real estate investment trusts (REITs) provide liquid, regulated access to real estate markets; commodity futures and ETFs provide access to raw materials; together, they form the "real assets" allocation in institutional portfolios. Endowments like Yale (which pioneered the model) allocate 20–30% to real assets.

## Key Points
- **REITs** must distribute ≥90% of taxable income as dividends (IRS requirement); this creates bond-like income streams with equity-like capital appreciation
- **Three main REIT types:** Equity REITs (own properties), Mortgage REITs (mREITs, own debt), Hybrid REITs
- **Core commodity markets:** Energy (~35% of major indices), Agriculture (~25%), Metals (~25%), Livestock (~10%)
- **Supercycle theory:** Commodities exhibit multi-decade cycles driven by supply/demand mismatches; the 2000s supercycle (China-driven) saw crude oil rise from ~$20 to $147
- **Gold's role:** 0 intrinsic yield but serves as monetary hedge, currency of last resort, and geopolitical uncertainty hedge
- **Inflation correlation:** Commodities have ~0.5–0.7 correlation with CPI inflation over 5-year periods; REITs ~0.3–0.4 (lagged)
- **Infrastructure and timberland** provide the highest inflation correlation (~0.8) but lowest liquidity

## Details

### 1. Why Real Assets? The Portfolio Rationale

The 60/40 portfolio (60% equities, 40% bonds) has been the institutional standard for decades. Its Achilles heel is **inflation**: both equities (via discount rate compression) and bonds (via real yield erosion) perform poorly during inflationary periods. Real assets fill this gap.

**Correlation matrix (approximate long-run values):**

| Asset | US Equities | US Bonds | REITs | Commodities | Inflation |
|-------|-------------|----------|-------|-------------|-----------|
| US Equities | 1.00 | −0.20 | 0.65 | 0.15 | −0.30 |
| US Bonds | −0.20 | 1.00 | −0.10 | −0.05 | −0.40 |
| REITs | 0.65 | −0.10 | 1.00 | 0.10 | 0.35 |
| Commodities | 0.15 | −0.05 | 0.10 | 1.00 | 0.55 |
| Inflation | −0.30 | −0.40 | 0.35 | 0.55 | 1.00 |

The **1970s stagflation episode** is the canonical case: the S&P 500 delivered a cumulative real return of approximately −50% from 1968–1982. Over the same period, commodities (especially oil and gold) produced spectacular real returns. Gold rose from $35/oz (1971, end of Bretton Woods) to $850/oz (January 1980) — a 24x nominal increase.

The **2021–2022 inflationary surge** provided a modern validation: while both the S&P 500 (−18% in 2022) and the Bloomberg US Aggregate Bond Index (−13%) fell, the Bloomberg Commodity Index rose +25%, and energy commodities (oil, gas, coal) gained 50–100%.

### 2. Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs)

**Definition and Structure:**
REITs were created by US Congress in 1960 as a vehicle to give ordinary investors access to large-scale real estate. To qualify as a REIT under IRS rules:
- At least **75% of total assets** must be real estate
- At least **75% of gross income** from real estate sources (rents, mortgage interest)
- Distribute **≥90% of taxable income** as dividends annually
- Have **≥100 shareholders**, with no 5 individuals owning >50% (the 5/50 rule)
- Be structured as a taxable domestic corporation

The tax benefit is enormous: REITs pay no corporate income tax on distributed income. Shareholders pay ordinary income tax on dividends (unlike qualified dividends from regular corporations). This pass-through structure means a REIT pays out its entire taxable income, avoiding the "double taxation" of corporate dividends.

**Global Scale:**
The US REIT market capitalization exceeds $1.4 trillion as of 2024 (NAREIT data). Global REIT markets add another $1+ trillion across Australia (A-REITs), Japan (J-REITs), UK, Singapore, France, and 40+ other countries. The FTSE NAREIT All Equity REIT Index provides the benchmark.

**Sector Breakdown (US, approximate):**

| Sector | Market Cap Share | Key Metric |
|--------|-----------------|------------|
| Industrial/Logistics | 15% | Vacancy rate, rent per sq ft |
| Retail (Mall, Strip) | 10% | Foot traffic, occupancy |
| Residential (Apartments) | 12% | Same-store NOI growth |
| Office | 8% | Remote work headwinds |
| Data Centers | 12% | Power density, capacity |
| Healthcare (Medical) | 8% | Lease terms, tenant quality |
| Self-Storage | 6% | Street rate, occupancy |
| Hotels/Lodging | 5% | RevPAR (Revenue per Available Room) |
| Specialty/Cell Towers | 12% | Tower lease escalators |
| Mortgage REITs (mREITs) | 8% | Net Interest Margin, leverage |

**Key REIT Metrics:**

**Funds From Operations (FFO):**
Standard earnings metric for REITs, adjusted for depreciation (real estate depreciates on the balance sheet but often appreciates in reality):
```
FFO = Net Income + Depreciation & Amortization − Gains on Property Sales
```

**Adjusted FFO (AFFO):** Further adjusts for recurring capital expenditures (maintenance capex) needed to maintain properties. AFFO is the true cash available for distribution.

**Net Asset Value (NAV):**
Estimated market value of a REIT's property portfolio minus liabilities. If REIT trades at a discount to NAV, it may be undervalued (or the market may correctly anticipate property value declines). REIT analysts spend significant effort on independent property valuation.

**Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate):**
```
Cap Rate = Net Operating Income (NOI) / Property Value
```
Conceptually similar to an earnings yield. A 5% cap rate means a property generating $5M NOI is worth $100M. Cap rates move inversely to property values (just as bond yields move inversely to bond prices). When the Fed raised rates aggressively in 2022–2023, cap rates rose, compressing REIT valuations.

**Case Study — Prologis (PLD), Industrial REIT:**
Prologis is the world's largest REIT, with ~1.2 billion sq ft of logistics/warehouse properties across 19 countries, valued at ~$170 billion. Its tenants include Amazon, UPS, DHL, and FedEx. The e-commerce boom drove extraordinary rent growth: same-store cash NOI grew ~10% annually in 2021–2022. The company's "Essentials" platform has added ancillary services (lighting, rooftop solar, EV charging) that generate additional revenue streams.

**Simon Property Group (SPG) — Retail REIT Adaptation:**
Mall REITs faced existential questions from e-commerce. SPG responded by converting anchor department stores (Sears, JCPenney) to entertainment, dining, fitness, and housing uses. It also acquired struggling retailers (J.Crew, Brooks Brothers) at distressed prices through its e-commerce subsidiary to control tenant ecosystems. As of 2024, SPG's A-grade malls maintained ~95% occupancy.

**Data Center REITs — The AI Boom:**
Equinix (EQIX) and Digital Realty (DLR) have become among the world's most valuable REITs as AI training and inference workloads create explosive demand for power-dense colocation. Power capacity (measured in megawatts) replaced square footage as the key constraint. Equinix commands premium pricing ($2,000+/kW/month for top-tier colo space) and expanded into 70+ markets globally.

**Interest Rate Sensitivity:**
REITs are often called "bond proxies" because their stable dividend income competes with fixed income. When interest rates rise, REIT prices typically fall (in the short run) for two reasons:
1. Discount rate effect: higher rates reduce the NPV of future cash flows
2. Competition effect: bond yields become more attractive relative to dividend yields

However, in **inflationary rate-rise environments**, REITs with **inflation-linked leases** (e.g., industrial REITs with annual CPI escalators) outperform, because rental income growth offsets the rate headwind.

### 3. Commodities: The Physical World's Markets

**The Commodity Complex:**

Commodities divide into:
- **Energy:** Crude oil (WTI, Brent), natural gas, heating oil, gasoline, coal
- **Metals:** Precious (gold, silver, platinum, palladium), base (copper, aluminum, zinc, nickel, lead)
- **Agriculture:** Grains (corn, wheat, soybeans), softs (coffee, cocoa, sugar, cotton), livestock (cattle, hogs)

**Major indices:**
- **S&P GSCI (Goldman Sachs Commodity Index):** Production-weighted; ~60% energy heavy. Best tracks real-world commodity exposure but creates high crude oil volatility.
- **Bloomberg Commodity Index (BCOM):** More diversified, caps any single commodity at 15% and sectors at 33%. Better for diversification purposes.
- **CRB (Commodity Research Bureau) Index:** Equally weighted, oldest commodity index (1957).

**Commodity Price Formation:**

Unlike equities, commodities cannot be valued via discounted cash flows. Their prices reflect:
1. **Supply/demand balance** (physical fundamentals)
2. **Inventories/storage** (low inventories → high spot vs. forward premiums = backwardation)
3. **Cost of production** (the long-run price floor — below which supply decreases)
4. **Speculative positioning** (CFTC Commitments of Traders report tracks this)
5. **Dollar strength** (most commodities priced in USD; a stronger dollar tends to suppress dollar-denominated commodity prices)

**Supercycles Theory:**
Commodity supercycles are multi-decade periods of above-trend prices driven by structural supply-demand misbalances, typically linked to major industrial transformation:
- **1890s–1900s:** US industrialization
- **1910s–1920s:** World War I
- **1950s–1960s:** Post-WWII reconstruction (Europe, Japan)
- **2000s–2010s:** China's urbanization (the strongest supercycle since WWII)
- **2020s–ongoing?:** Energy transition (green metals supercycle hypothesis)

The China supercycle: between 2000–2011, China's share of global copper consumption rose from 12% to 40%, its steel production from 128 Mt to 683 Mt. Copper rose from $0.60/lb to $4.50/lb; iron ore from $15/ton to $190/ton.

**The Green Metals Thesis:** The energy transition requires enormous quantities of copper (EVs use 3–4x more copper than ICE vehicles), lithium, cobalt, nickel, and rare earths for batteries, motors, and grid infrastructure. Goldman Sachs estimates the global energy transition will require 330Mt of copper through 2030 — equivalent to 30 years of current mine output. This thesis drives significant investment in mining equities and commodities.

### 4. Gold: Monetary Metal and Crisis Hedge

Gold occupies a unique position: it produces nothing, generates no income, and has limited industrial use (~10% of demand from electronics/medical). Yet it has served as a store of value for 5,000+ years and is the second-largest reserve asset in central bank portfolios after US Treasuries.

**Gold's Demand Structure (2023, World Gold Council):**
- Jewelry: ~50% (~2,000 tonnes)
- Central bank purchases: ~25% (~1,000 tonnes, hitting record highs as reserve diversification away from USD accelerates)
- Investment (ETFs, bars, coins): ~20%
- Technology: ~10%

**Gold Pricing Model:**
Gold has no cash flows, so traditional DCF doesn't apply. Instead, gold price correlates most strongly with **real interest rates** (nominal rates minus inflation expectations):

```
Gold Price ≈ f(−Real Yield, +Uncertainty, +USD weakness, +Central bank buying)
```

When the 10-year TIPS yield (real yield) fell to −1.1% in August 2020, gold hit an all-time high of $2,067/oz. When real yields rose to +2.5% in 2023, gold fell to ~$1,620/oz before recovering on central bank buying.

**GATA (Gold Anti-Trust Action Committee) claims** that central banks suppress gold prices through leasing arrangements. While controversial, central bank gold lending is real: banks lease gold to bullion dealers who sell it spot and invest proceeds, creating "paper gold" supply. The opacity of central bank gold positions (Switzerland, Germany have significant undisclosed leasing) makes the true physical market opaque.

**The Gold/Silver Ratio:** Historically averages 60:1 (60 oz silver = 1 oz gold). Extreme readings (>80:1 or <40:1) have historically predicted reversion. In March 2020, the ratio hit 125:1 — the highest in modern history — before reverting to 70:1.

### 5. Energy: Oil and Natural Gas

**Oil Market Structure:**
Crude oil is the world's largest traded commodity. Two main benchmark grades:
- **Brent Crude:** European/global benchmark, priced at ICE London. Reflects North Sea, West African, and Middle Eastern crude. Used to price ~75% of globally traded oil.
- **WTI (West Texas Intermediate):** US benchmark, priced at NYMEX. Trades at Cushing, Oklahoma. Typically $2–5 discount to Brent.

**OPEC+ cartel:** OPEC (13 members) + 10 non-OPEC allies (led by Russia) coordinate production quotas to manage prices. Combined market share: ~40% of global production and ~80% of proven reserves. The UAE's dramatic exit from OPEC (effective May 1, 2026) — allowing it to expand production from 3.4M to 5M bpd by 2027 — is the largest defection in the cartel's 65-year history and signals a structural fracture in OPEC's ability to maintain discipline.

**The Shale Revolution:** US tight oil production (fracturing horizontal wells in shale formations) rose from 0.5M bpd in 2010 to 13M bpd by 2024 — making the US the world's largest oil producer. The "shale put" theory holds that US shale producers, with break-even costs of $40–50/barrel, effectively cap oil prices. When prices fall below that floor, producers cut rigs; when they rise above it, production rushes back, capping the upside.

### 6. Building a Real Assets Allocation

**Institutional allocation (Yale Endowment model):**
The Yale Endowment under David Swensen pioneered heavy real asset allocation:
- Real assets (real estate + natural resources): ~30% of portfolio
- Target: inflation protection + low correlation to financial assets

**Retail access vehicles:**
- **Equity REITs:** Listed on exchanges; most liquid option
- **Non-traded REITs:** No exchange listing; less liquid but avoid mark-to-market volatility
- **REIT ETFs:** Vanguard Real Estate ETF (VNQ) has $35B+ AUM, 0.12% expense ratio
- **Commodity ETFs:** iShares GSCI Commodity Dynamic Roll (COMT), invesco DB Commodity Index (DBC)
- **Gold ETFs:** SPDR Gold Shares (GLD) holds physical gold; expense ratio 0.40%. iShares Silver Trust (SLV) for silver.
- **Oil ETFs:** Note contango cost — USO (oil ETF) loses ~10–30% per year in normal contango from rolling costs, making it unsuitable for long-term holding.

**The Inflation-Linking Ladder:**
For an inflation-hedging portfolio, a practical framework ranks assets by inflation sensitivity:
1. Infrastructure/pipelines (CPI-linked contracts, highest sensitivity)
2. Commodities spot (direct price level exposure)
3. Timberland/farmland (biological assets with natural yield)
4. Industrial REITs with CPI lease escalators
5. TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)
6. Gold (monetary inflation hedge, less CPI-tied)
7. General equity REITs (more rate-sensitive)
