## Summary
The yield curve plots interest rates across maturities for a single issuer (typically the US Treasury) and is one of the most powerful leading indicators in macroeconomics. Its shape encodes the market's collective view on growth, inflation, and monetary policy — and its inversions have preceded every US recession since the 1950s.

## Key Points
- A **normal** (upward-sloping) curve means long rates > short rates — healthy growth expectations
- An **inverted** curve (short rates > long rates) has predicted every US recession with a ~12–18 month lead
- The **2s10s spread** (10-year minus 2-year yield) is the most watched recession indicator
- **Bear steepening** (long rates rise faster) signals inflation fears; **bear flattening** signals Fed tightening
- The curve is shaped by three forces: monetary policy (short end), inflation expectations (long end), and term premium

## Details

### The Four Curve Shapes

**1. Normal (upward-sloping)**
Long-term yields > short-term yields. Investors demand a premium for tying up money longer — compensating for inflation risk and opportunity cost. This is the default shape in expansion phases.

**2. Flat**
Short and long yields are nearly equal. Typical in transition periods — the Fed has been hiking and the market expects growth to slow. Signals uncertainty, not yet recession.

**3. Inverted (downward-sloping)**
Short-term yields > long-term yields. Historically the most reliable recession predictor. It means:
- The Fed has hiked short rates aggressively (to fight inflation)
- The market expects rates to fall in the future (i.e., the economy weakens and the Fed cuts)
- Investors are willing to accept lower long-term yields because they expect economic deterioration

> The 2s10s spread inverted before every US recession since 1955 — with a lead time of 6–24 months. False positives: essentially zero in modern history.

**4. Humped (bell-shaped)**
Medium-term yields peak above both short and long yields. Rare and transient — signals a temporary market dislocation or positioning squeeze.

### Key Spreads to Watch

| Spread | What it measures |
|--------|-----------------|
| **2s10s** (10Y − 2Y) | Most-watched recession signal; captures Fed policy vs. long-term growth expectations |
| **3m10s** (10Y − 3M) | Fed's preferred measure; cleaner signal for near-term recession odds |
| **5s30s** (30Y − 5Y) | Long-end steepness; tracks inflation expectations at longer horizons |
| **TED spread** (3M LIBOR − T-bill) | Credit stress in the banking system |
| **OIS spread** (LIBOR − OIS) | Interbank credit risk; spiked dramatically in 2008 and 2020 |

### What Drives Each Part of the Curve

**Short end (under 2 years)**
Dominated by the Federal Funds Rate and Fed expectations. When the Fed hikes, short yields rise almost mechanically. The market prices in the expected path of Fed funds rate through forwards and futures.

**Middle (2–7 years)**
Blends Fed expectations with growth and inflation forecasts. Sensitive to changes in economic data (CPI, GDP, payrolls).

**Long end (10–30 years)**
Driven by:
- **Inflation expectations** (the biggest driver historically)
- **Term premium** — the extra yield demanded for holding long bonds (uncertainty compensation)
- **Supply/demand dynamics** — Treasury issuance, foreign central bank buying, pension fund demand
- **Real growth expectations**

The long end is less controlled by the Fed and more reflective of the market's structural view on the economy.

### Steepening vs. Flattening

These describe changes in the spread between two points on the curve:

**Steepening** (spread widens — long rates rise relative to short)
- *Bull steepening*: short rates fall faster (Fed cutting). Classic early-recession signal when the Fed pivots.
- *Bear steepening*: long rates rise faster (inflation fears, fiscal concerns, term premium repricing). Often signals bond market stress. Seen in 2023 when 10Y yields surged past 5%.

**Flattening** (spread narrows — long rates fall relative to short)
- *Bull flattening*: long rates fall faster (risk-off, flight to safety). Typical in recessions.
- *Bear flattening*: short rates rise faster (Fed hiking). The 2022 experience — the fastest flattening in decades as the Fed raised rates 525bps.

### The Inversion Mechanism — Why It Works

When short yields exceed long yields, banks face a fundamental problem: they borrow short (deposits, overnight) and lend long (mortgages, business loans). An inverted curve **compresses or eliminates their net interest margin** — making lending unprofitable. Banks tighten credit standards, loan growth slows, and the economy contracts.

This is the transmission mechanism: **inversion → credit tightening → slower growth → recession**.

The lag exists because credit tightening takes time to work through the economy — businesses take months to feel the pinch, and hiring/capex decisions lag the credit squeeze further.

### Yield Curve Control (YCC)

A policy tool where a central bank caps yields at a specific level by buying whatever bonds are needed to keep yields there. Japan has used YCC since 2016 (targeting 10Y JGB yields near 0%), making it the largest buyer of its own government bonds.

The risk: YCC can cause currency depreciation (yen weakened sharply in 2022) and distort price discovery in bond markets. The Bank of Japan began unwinding YCC gradually in 2024.

### Practical Applications for Investors

**Positioning for steepening:**
- Long 2Y, short 10Y (profits if 2Y falls more than 10Y — bull steepener)
- Long 10Y, short 2Y (profits if 10Y rises more than 2Y — bear steepener)

**Duration positioning:**
- Short duration (bills, 1–2Y bonds) when curve is flattening or rates rising
- Long duration (10Y+) when curve is steepening from inversion (recession recovery) or when flight to safety is expected

**Riding the curve:**
Buying a bond with slightly longer maturity than your holding period, then selling before maturity as it "rolls down" the (normal) curve — picking up both coupon and price appreciation.

### Real-World Example: 2022–2023 Cycle

1. **March 2022**: Fed begins hiking from 0.25%
2. **July 2022**: 2s10s inverts for the first time since 2019
3. **Oct 2022**: Inversion deepens to −80bps (most inverted since 1981)
4. **Oct 2023**: 10Y yield hits 5.0% — bear steepening driven by term premium and fiscal concerns (US debt load)
5. **Late 2023–2024**: Curve begins bull steepening as recession fears grow and market prices in Fed cuts
6. **2025–2026**: Fed begins cutting cycle; curve normalizing

This cycle was a textbook illustration of every concept above — flattening, inversion, bear steepening, and eventual re-normalization.
