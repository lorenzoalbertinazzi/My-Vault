## Summary
Cognitive biases are systematic errors in thinking that arise from the brain's shortcuts (heuristics). They evolved because fast, good-enough judgments usually beat slow, perfect ones. Understanding biases doesn't make you immune, but it gives you the vocabulary to catch them — especially in high-stakes decisions. Kahneman's System 1 / System 2 framework (fast/intuitive vs. slow/deliberate thinking) is the organizing model.

## Key Points
- Biases are features, not bugs — they're shortcuts that work most of the time
- They cluster into categories: memory, social/self-perception, probability, decision-making
- The most dangerous biases are the ones that affect how we gather information in the first place
- **Debiasing**: slow down, seek disconfirming evidence, consider base rates, use checklists
- You cannot eliminate biases; you can build systems that compensate for them

## Details

### The Organizing Framework: System 1 vs. System 2 (Kahneman)
- **System 1**: Fast, automatic, emotional, pattern-matching. Runs constantly.
- **System 2**: Slow, deliberate, logical, effortful. Engages only when triggered.

Most biases are System 1 errors applied in situations that require System 2 thinking.

### Biases That Distort Information Gathering

**Confirmation Bias** — The most pervasive. We seek out, notice, and remember information that confirms what we already believe. We unconsciously discount contradictory evidence.

> Fix: Actively seek the strongest version of the opposing case (steelman, not strawman). Ask "What would need to be true for me to be wrong?"

**Availability Heuristic** — We judge the probability of events by how easily examples come to mind. Plane crashes feel more dangerous than car crashes because they're more memorable/vivid.

> Fix: Look up base rates. Your gut probability estimate is a function of media coverage, not reality.

**Anchoring** — The first number (or piece of information) we encounter disproportionately shapes our subsequent estimates. Salary negotiators who anchor high get higher outcomes.

> Fix: Before receiving an anchor, form your own independent estimate. If anchored, deliberately consider the opposite extreme.

---

### Biases That Distort Self-Assessment

**Dunning-Kruger Effect** — Low-competence people overestimate their ability; high-competence people underestimate it. Competence teaches you what you don't know.

> Pattern: Early in learning a skill → peak confidence. After real study → confidence crashes (you see what you don't know). After mastery → calibrated, quieter confidence.

**Overconfidence Bias** — People are systematically more confident in their judgments than accuracy warrants. Asked for 90% confidence intervals, people are wrong 40%+ of the time.

> Fix: Calibration practice. Track your predictions vs. outcomes. Add "outside view" — how do similar projects/predictions historically perform?

**Fundamental Attribution Error** — We over-attribute others' behavior to their character and under-attribute it to their situation. "He's late because he's inconsiderate" vs. "He's late because there was traffic."

> Fix: Before judging someone's character, ask what situational pressures they face.

---

### Biases That Distort Decisions

**Sunk Cost Fallacy** — We continue investing in something (time, money, relationships) because of what we've already put in — even when future returns don't justify it.

> Fix: Ask "If I hadn't already invested, would I start this now with what I know?" Past costs are irrelevant to future decisions.

**Loss Aversion** — Losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman and Tversky). People will take risks to avoid losses that they wouldn't take to acquire equivalent gains.

> Implication: Frame choices in terms of losses when you need action; in terms of gains when you need calm analysis.

**Status Quo Bias** — We prefer the current state of affairs. Inertia masquerades as rational preference. Default options work because of this.

> Fix: Ask "If I were starting fresh with no prior position, what would I choose?"

**Planning Fallacy** — We underestimate how long tasks take, how much they'll cost, and how many problems will arise. Projects almost always take longer than predicted.

> Fix: Reference class forecasting — how long did similar projects actually take? Add a buffer to your inside-view estimate.

**Optimism Bias** — We believe we are less likely than average to experience negative events (accidents, illness, failure). Drives entrepreneurship but also excess risk-taking.

---

### Biases That Distort Social Judgment

**In-group Bias** — We favor members of our own group (tribe, nationality, company) and apply harsher standards to out-group members.

**Halo / Horn Effect** — One positive (or negative) trait colors our perception of all other traits. Attractive people are assumed more competent. One mistake undermines all prior good work.

**Social Desirability Bias** — In surveys and conversations, people answer in ways they think are approved of rather than their true opinions.

**Bandwagon Effect** — Beliefs and behaviors spread because others hold them, regardless of merit. The basis for trends, fads, and market bubbles.

---

### The 4 Clusters (Buster Benson's Codex)

1. **Too much information** → we filter using shortcuts → miss important things
2. **Not enough meaning** → we fill in gaps with patterns and stories → invent causation and narrative
3. **Need to act fast** → we overweight confidence and familiar options → take excessive or insufficient risks
4. **Memory is limited** → we store summaries, not records → our "memories" are reconstructions

---

### Debiasing Checklist for High-Stakes Decisions
- [ ] What would need to be true for the opposite view to be correct?
- [ ] What is the base rate for this type of outcome?
- [ ] Am I anchored to a particular number — what's my independent estimate?
- [ ] What is driving my urgency — real deadline, or manufactured scarcity?
- [ ] What would I advise a friend in this exact situation?
- [ ] Have I sought out someone who disagrees and genuinely engaged with their argument?
