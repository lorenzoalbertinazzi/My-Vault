## Summary

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) is a macro-theory of human motivation and personality developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan at the University of Rochester beginning in the early 1970s, with the full theoretical framework published in 1985. SDT holds that humans are inherently growth-oriented organisms who thrive when their three basic psychological needs are satisfied: **autonomy** (feeling that one's actions are self-chosen), **competence** (feeling effective in one's environment), and **relatedness** (feeling meaningfully connected to others). When these needs are met, intrinsic motivation — doing something for its inherent satisfaction rather than external reward — flourishes, leading to greater creativity, persistence, well-being, and psychological integration. When these needs are frustrated, intrinsic motivation atrophies, extrinsic motivation dominates, and well-being deteriorates. SDT has generated over 1,000 empirical studies and transformed understanding of education, work, healthcare, parenting, and sport.

## Key Points

- SDT distinguishes sharply between **intrinsic motivation** (doing for its own sake) and **extrinsic motivation** (doing for external reward/punishment)
- Three universal basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, relatedness
- **Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET):** Specific subtopic showing how external rewards and feedback affect intrinsic motivation
- **Organismic Integration Theory (OIT):** Explains how extrinsic motivation can be internalized across a spectrum from external regulation to integration
- **Over-justification effect:** Adding external rewards to intrinsically motivated activities paradoxically reduces motivation
- Cross-cultural evidence supports universality of the three needs across 25+ countries
- SDT has practical applications in management (Google 20% time), education, therapy, and health behavior change

## Details

### Historical Development and Founding Research

The empirical seed of SDT was planted in 1971 by Deci's now-classic experiment: college students worked on an intrinsically interesting puzzle (Soma cube). In the experimental group, subjects received $1 for each puzzle solved; in the control group, no money. Critical finding: **after payment was removed, the rewarded group showed significantly less intrinsic interest** (measured by free-time puzzle engagement) than the never-rewarded group. This was the first controlled demonstration of the **over-justification effect** — external rewards can "crowd out" internal motivation.

**Key early researchers and contributions:**
- **Edward Deci (1943–):** Pioneered CET; published landmark book with Ryan in 1985
- **Richard Ryan (1953–):** Extended theory to include need for relatedness; led cross-cultural validation
- **Johnmarshall Reeve (University of Iowa):** Applied SDT to educational contexts
- **Maarten Vansteenkiste (Ghent):** Major cross-cultural and clinical SDT researcher
- **Avi Assor:** Developed conditionality of regard framework

**Chronological milestones:**
- 1971: Deci's over-justification experiment
- 1975: Deci publishes "Intrinsic Motivation" (Plenum Press)
- 1980s: Ryan and Deci develop the needs framework
- 1985: "Intrinsic Motivation and Self-Determination in Human Behavior" — full SDT published
- 2000: Ryan & Deci, *Psychological Review* — most cited SDT paper (15,000+ citations)
- 2017: Ryan & Deci, "Self-Determination Theory: Basic Psychological Needs in Motivation, Development, and Wellness" — definitive 756-page synthesis

### The Three Basic Psychological Needs

**1. Autonomy**
Autonomy is the experience of volition — acting from one's own values, interests, and authentic self rather than feeling pressured or controlled. Autonomy does not mean independence or isolation; one can autonomously choose to follow another's guidance. Key distinction: **internal locus of causality** (I choose this because I want to) vs. **external locus of causality** (I do this because I have to).

**Operationalization:** Perceived Autonomy Scale; subjective endorsement of behavior ("I do X because it's interesting/important to me" vs. "because I get a reward/to avoid punishment")

**2. Competence**
Competence is the experience of mastery and effectiveness — feeling capable of producing desired outcomes, learning and growing. Closely related to Bandura's self-efficacy but distinct: competence in SDT is about the **felt experience of effectiveness**, not just the belief in capability.

**Research finding:** Optimal challenge (tasks slightly above current skill level) is most conducive to felt competence and intrinsic motivation — consistent with Csikszentmihalyi's flow theory.

**3. Relatedness**
Relatedness is the experience of genuine connection and belonging — caring for others and feeling cared for. Requires not just social contact but **felt significance** and warmth. Distinguished from mere affiliation: impersonal social presence doesn't satisfy relatedness.

**Cross-cultural evidence:** Need universality tested in 25+ countries (Deci et al., 2001). Results show consistent need satisfaction → well-being correlation in individualist (US, Germany) and collectivist (China, India, South Korea) cultures, refuting the argument that autonomy is a Western value.

### The Motivation Continuum: Organismic Integration Theory

SDT rejects the binary intrinsic/extrinsic motivation dichotomy and instead proposes a **continuum of motivation quality**, based on the degree of internalization and integration of extrinsic regulations:

```
Amotivation → External → Introjected → Identified → Integrated → Intrinsic
     ↑              ↑           ↑              ↑             ↑           ↑
 No intent    Do it for   Do it to    Do it because  Do it because  Do it for
              reward/     avoid guilt/ I value the  it fits my      pure
              punishment  shame        outcome       identity       enjoyment
```

**Detailed descriptions:**

| Type | Example | Perceived Locus | Regulatory Style |
|------|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Amotivation | "Why bother?" | Impersonal | None |
| External | "I exercise to get the prize" | External | External reward/punishment |
| Introjected | "I exercise so I won't feel guilty" | Somewhat external | Ego involvement; shame |
| Identified | "I exercise because health matters to me" | Internal | Personal importance |
| Integrated | "Exercising is part of who I am" | Internal | Congruent with self |
| Intrinsic | "I love how running feels" | Internal | Interest/enjoyment |

The practical implication: **internalization can be promoted**. A student who starts doing homework for external reward (grades) can progress to identified regulation (because it builds my future) and eventually integrated regulation, even if they never find pure intrinsic joy in algebra.

### Cognitive Evaluation Theory: How Rewards Affect Intrinsic Motivation

CET (Deci & Ryan, 1980) specifies conditions under which external events enhance or undermine intrinsic motivation:

**The Informational-Controlling Distinction:**
- Events with **informational** functional significance (provide competence feedback) enhance intrinsic motivation: "You did that really well — you've clearly mastered this level"
- Events with **controlling** functional significance (signal external pressure) undermine intrinsic motivation: "Good, you earned your reward"
- Verbal praise → typically informational → enhances IM
- Tangible rewards → typically controlling → undermines IM
- Deadlines, surveillance, threats, imposed goals → controlling → undermine IM
- Unexpected rewards, choice → informational/autonomy-supportive → neutral or enhancing

**Meta-analysis (Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999):** 128 studies, 11,000+ participants. Key findings:
- Tangible, expected, contingent rewards undermine intrinsic motivation for interesting tasks in 70% of studies
- Verbal rewards (praise) typically enhance intrinsic motivation
- Unexpected rewards have no effect
- Rewards for uninteresting tasks do not undermine IM (there's no IM to crowd out)

**The Neuroscience:** Reward learning activates dopaminergic circuits (striatum, VTA). When extrinsic reward becomes the dominant signal for an activity, the prefrontal-cortical circuits associated with self-generated interest are downregulated. fMRI studies (Murayama et al., 2010) show: monetary reward for an engaging cognitive task shifts activation from lateral prefrontal (exploratory, self-generated engagement) toward striatal reward circuits. Remove the reward: less prefrontal activation than baseline.

### Practical Applications

**Education:**
SDT-based autonomy-supportive teaching vs. controlling teaching:
- Autonomy-supportive: Provide rationale, acknowledge students' perspectives, minimize pressure and demands
- Controlling: Directives, contingent rewards, surveillance, competition

**Outcome data (Reeve, 2006 meta-analysis):**
- Autonomy-supportive classrooms: +23% intrinsic motivation, +18% engagement, +0.35 SD academic achievement
- Finland's education system is routinely cited as autonomy-supportive: minimal standardized testing, teacher-led curriculum, emphasis on intrinsic interest → PISA top performers

**Management and Organizations:**
**Deci, Connell & Ryan (1989):** Managers at Xerox trained in autonomy-supportive style vs. control:
- Employees in autonomy-supportive condition: Higher satisfaction, trust, job satisfaction; lower burnout
- Effect size: d = 0.45 (medium-large)

**Google's 20% time:** Engineers allocated 20% of work hours to self-chosen projects. Products arising: Gmail, Google Maps, Google News, AdSense. Classic organizational autonomy support. (Note: Policy reportedly became less central post-2012 as company scaled.)

**Healthcare and Behavior Change:**
SDT predicts: patients who autonomously regulate health behaviors will show better long-term adherence.

**Williams et al. (1998):** Diabetic patients randomized to autonomy-supportive vs. control physician communication style:
- Autonomy-support: +47% medication adherence at 6 months
- Greater perceived competence mediates the effect

**Motivational Interviewing (Miller & Rollnick):** Explicitly grounded in SDT principles — elicit the person's own reasons for change rather than imposing external pressure.

**Sport and Physical Activity:**
**Ntoumanis (2001) meta-analysis:** SDT needs satisfaction in sport correlates:
- Autonomy satisfaction ↔ intrinsic motivation: r = 0.55
- Competence satisfaction ↔ intrinsic motivation: r = 0.60
- Relatedness satisfaction ↔ intrinsic motivation: r = 0.38
- Intrinsic motivation ↔ persistence in sport: r = 0.42

Coaches who support autonomy (vs. use controlling tactics like punishment, criticism, pressure) produce athletes who persist longer and report greater well-being.

### The Over-Justification Effect: Deep Dive

The over-justification effect is SDT's most counterintuitive and practically important finding, with major implications for incentive design:

**Lepper, Greene & Nisbett (1973) — the classic children's study:**
Nursery school children who liked drawing were assigned to three conditions:
1. **Expected reward:** Told they'd get a "Good Player Award" for drawing
2. **Unexpected reward:** Got award afterward, no advance notice
3. **No reward:** Neither told about nor received award

Two weeks later: Observation of spontaneous drawing in free play. Results:
- Expected reward group: 50% reduction in spontaneous drawing
- Unexpected reward: No change
- No reward: No change

**Why does it happen?** (Three mechanisms):
1. **Cognitive reattribution:** "If they're paying me to do X, X must not be worth doing for its own sake"
2. **Locus of causality shift:** Perceived cause of action shifts from internal interest to external reward
3. **Competence signaling backfire:** Rewards contingent on completion (rather than quality) don't signal competence

**Boundary conditions where rewards do NOT undermine IM:**
- Task is uninteresting to begin with (no IM to crowd out)
- Reward is unexpected
- Reward is tied to specific performance quality (provides competence information)
- Person already sees extrinsic rewards as complementary to intrinsic interest

### Competing Theories and Critiques

**1. Behaviourism challenge:** B.F. Skinner and operant conditioning theorists argue all behavior is shaped by reinforcement history — the concept of "intrinsic motivation" is unnecessary and mentalistic. SDT proponents: the over-justification effect (reward reduces behavior) is directly contradicted by operant conditioning predictions.

**2. Cameron & Pierce (1994) challenge:** Meta-analysis claiming over-justification effect only applies to verbal reward (which enhances IM), not tangible rewards (which are neutral). Triggered a major methodological debate. Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999) reanalysis of the same data: effect is robust for tangible, expected, contingent rewards.

**3. Eisenberger & Cameron:** "Detrimental effects of reward" are an artifact of poor experimental design; properly administered rewards enhance performance without harming IM. Still contested.

**4. Cultural critique (Chirkov et al., 2003):** Autonomy may manifest differently across cultures — in collectivist cultures, autonomous choice includes choosing to follow group norms. Ryan & Deci: agreed; autonomy doesn't mean individualism, and cross-cultural data confirm need universality.

**5. Neuroscience challenge:** Some neuroscientists argue the "intrinsic motivation" neural signature is not clearly distinct from extrinsic reward processing — both involve dopaminergic circuits.

### Quantitative Measures

**Basic Psychological Need Satisfaction and Frustration Scale (BPNSFS):** 24-item validated scale measuring satisfaction and frustration of all three needs.

**Situational Motivation Scale (SIMS):** 16-item measure of intrinsic motivation, identified, external regulation, amotivation in specific situations.

**Relative Autonomy Index (RAI):**
RAI = (intrinsic × 2) + identified × 1 + introjected × (-1) + external × (-2)
Range: -4 to +4. Higher = more autonomous motivation.
- RAI > 1 predicts adherence, well-being, performance
- RAI < -1 predicts dropout, burnout, amotivation
