## Summary

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) is a macro-theory of human motivation and personality developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan at the University of Rochester beginning in the early 1970s, with the full theoretical framework published in 1985. SDT holds that humans are inherently growth-oriented organisms who thrive when their three basic psychological needs are satisfied: **autonomy** (feeling that one's actions are self-chosen), **competence** (feeling effective in one's environment), and **relatedness** (feeling meaningfully connected to others). When these needs are met, intrinsic motivation — doing something for its inherent satisfaction rather than external reward — flourishes, leading to greater creativity, persistence, well-being, and psychological integration. When these needs are frustrated, intrinsic motivation atrophies, extrinsic motivation dominates, and well-being deteriorates. SDT has generated over 1,000 empirical studies and transformed understanding of education, work, healthcare, parenting, and sport.

## Key Points

- SDT distinguishes sharply between **intrinsic motivation** (doing for its own sake) and **extrinsic motivation** (doing for external reward/punishment)
- Three universal basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, relatedness
- **Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET):** Specific subtopic showing how external rewards and feedback affect intrinsic motivation
- **Organismic Integration Theory (OIT):** Explains how extrinsic motivation can be internalized across a spectrum from external regulation to integration
- **Over-justification effect:** Adding external rewards to intrinsically motivated activities paradoxically reduces motivation
- Cross-cultural evidence supports universality of the three needs across 25+ countries
- SDT has practical applications in management (Google 20% time), education, therapy, and health behavior change

## Details

### Historical Development and Founding Research

The empirical seed of SDT was planted in 1971 by Deci's now-classic experiment: college students worked on an intrinsically interesting puzzle (Soma cube). In the experimental group, subjects received $1 for each puzzle solved; in the control group, no money. Critical finding: **after payment was removed, the rewarded group showed significantly less intrinsic interest** (measured by free-time puzzle engagement) than the never-rewarded group. This was the first controlled demonstration of the **over-justification effect** — external rewards can "crowd out" internal motivation.

**Key early researchers and contributions:**
- **Edward Deci (1943–):** Pioneered CET; published landmark book with Ryan in 1985
- **Richard Ryan (1953–):** Extended theory to include need for relatedness; led cross-cultural validation
- **Johnmarshall Reeve (University of Iowa):** Applied SDT to educational contexts
- **Maarten Vansteenkiste (Ghent):** Major cross-cultural and clinical SDT researcher
- **Avi Assor:** Developed conditionality of regard framework

**Chronological milestones:**
- 1971: Deci's over-justification experiment
- 1975: Deci publishes "Intrinsic Motivation" (Plenum Press)
- 1980s: Ryan and Deci develop the needs framework
- 1985: "Intrinsic Motivation and Self-Determination in Human Behavior" — full SDT published
- 2000: Ryan & Deci, *Psychological Review* — most cited SDT paper (15,000+ citations)
- 2017: Ryan & Deci, "Self-Determination Theory: Basic Psychological Needs in Motivation, Development, and Wellness" — definitive 756-page synthesis

### The Three Basic Psychological Needs

**1. Autonomy**
Autonomy is the experience of volition — acting from one's own values, interests, and authentic self rather than feeling pressured or controlled. Autonomy does not mean independence or isolation; one can autonomously choose to follow another's guidance. Key distinction: **internal locus of causality** (I choose this because I want to) vs. **external locus of causality** (I do this because I have to).

**Operationalization:** Perceived Autonomy Scale; subjective endorsement of behavior ("I do X because it's interesting/important to me" vs. "because I get a reward/to avoid punishment")

**2. Competence**
Competence is the experience of mastery and effectiveness — feeling capable of producing desired outcomes, learning and growing. Closely related to Bandura's self-efficacy but distinct: competence in SDT is about the **felt experience of effectiveness**, not just the belief in capability.

**Research finding:** Optimal challenge (tasks slightly above current skill level) is most conducive to felt competence and intrinsic motivation — consistent with Csikszentmihalyi's flow theory.

**3. Relatedness**
Relatedness is the experience of genuine connection and belonging — caring for others and feeling cared for. Requires not just social contact but **felt significance** and warmth. Distinguished from mere affiliation: impersonal social presence doesn't satisfy relatedness.

**Cross-cultural evidence:** Need universality tested in 25+ countries (Deci et al., 2001). Results show consistent need satisfaction → well-being correlation in individualist (US, Germany) and collectivist (China, India, South Korea) cultures, refuting the argument that autonomy is a Western value.

### The Motivation Continuum: Organismic Integration Theory

SDT rejects the binary intrinsic/extrinsic motivation dichotomy and instead proposes a **continuum of motivation quality**, based on the degree of internalization and integration of extrinsic regulations:

```
Amotivation → External → Introjected → Identified → Integrated → Intrinsic
     ↑              ↑           ↑              ↑             ↑           ↑
 No intent    Do it for   Do it to    Do it because  Do it because  Do it for
              reward/     avoid guilt/ I value the  it fits my      pure
              punishment  shame        outcome       identity       enjoyment
```

**Detailed descriptions:**

| Type | Example | Perceived Locus | Regulatory Style |
|------|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Amotivation | "Why bother?" | Impersonal | None |
| External | "I exercise to get the prize" | External | External reward/punishment |
| Introjected | "I exercise so I won't feel guilty" | Somewhat external | Ego involvement; shame |
| Identified | "I exercise because health matters to me" | Internal | Personal importance |
| Integrated | "Exercising is part of who I am" | Internal | Congruent with self |
| Intrinsic | "I love how running feels" | Internal | Interest/enjoyment |

The practical implication: **internalization can be promoted**. A student who starts doing homework for external reward (grades) can progress to identified regulation (because it builds my future) and eventually integrated regulation, even if they never find pure intrinsic joy in algebra.

### Cognitive Evaluation Theory: How Rewards Affect Intrinsic Motivation

CET (Deci & Ryan, 1980) specifies conditions under which external events enhance or undermine intrinsic motivation:

**The Informational-Controlling Distinction:**
- Events with **informational** functional significance (provide competence feedback) enhance intrinsic motivation: "You did that really well — you've clearly mastered this level"
- Events with **controlling** functional significance (signal external pressure) undermine intrinsic motivation: "Good, you earned your reward"
- Verbal praise → typically informational → enhances IM
- Tangible rewards → typically controlling → undermines IM
- Deadlines, surveillance, threats, imposed goals → controlling → undermine IM
- Unexpected rewards, choice → informational/autonomy-supportive → neutral or enhancing

**Meta-analysis (Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999):** 128 studies, 11,000+ participants. Key findings:
- Tangible, expected, contingent rewards undermine intrinsic motivation for interesting tasks in 70% of studies
- Verbal rewards (praise) typically enhance intrinsic motivation
- Unexpected rewards have no effect
- Rewards for uninteresting tasks do not undermine IM (there's no IM to crowd out)

**The Neuroscience:** Reward learning activates dopaminergic circuits (striatum, VTA). When extrinsic reward becomes the dominant signal for an activity, the prefrontal-cortical circuits associated with self-generated interest are downregulated. fMRI studies (Murayama et al., 2010) show: monetary reward for an engaging cognitive task shifts activation from lateral prefrontal (exploratory, self-generated engagement) toward striatal reward circuits. Remove the reward: less prefrontal activation than baseline.

### Practical Applications

**Education:**
SDT-based autonomy-supportive teaching vs. controlling teaching:
- Autonomy-supportive: Provide rationale, acknowledge students' perspectives, minimize pressure and demands
- Controlling: Directives, contingent rewards, surveillance, competition

**Outcome data (Reeve, 2006 meta-analysis):**
- Autonomy-supportive classrooms: +23% intrinsic motivation, +18% engagement, +0.35 SD academic achievement
- Finland's education system is routinely cited as autonomy-supportive: minimal standardized testing, teacher-led curriculum, emphasis on intrinsic interest → PISA top performers

**Management and Organizations:**
**Deci, Connell & Ryan (1989):** Managers at Xerox trained in autonomy-supportive style vs. control:
- Employees in autonomy-supportive condition: Higher satisfaction, trust, job satisfaction; lower burnout
- Effect size: d = 0.45 (medium-large)

**Google's 20% time:** Engineers allocated 20% of work hours to self-chosen projects. Products arising: Gmail, Google Maps, Google News, AdSense. Classic organizational autonomy support. (Note: Policy reportedly became less central post-2012 as company scaled.)

**Healthcare and Behavior Change:**
SDT predicts: patients who autonomously regulate health behaviors will show better long-term adherence.

**Williams et al. (1998):** Diabetic patients randomized to autonomy-supportive vs. control physician communication style:
- Autonomy-support: +47% medication adherence at 6 months
- Greater perceived competence mediates the effect

**Motivational Interviewing (Miller & Rollnick):** Explicitly grounded in SDT principles — elicit the person's own reasons for change rather than imposing external pressure.

**Sport and Physical Activity:**
**Ntoumanis (2001) meta-analysis:** SDT needs satisfaction in sport correlates:
- Autonomy satisfaction ↔ intrinsic motivation: r = 0.55
- Competence satisfaction ↔ intrinsic motivation: r = 0.60
- Relatedness satisfaction ↔ intrinsic motivation: r = 0.38
- Intrinsic motivation ↔ persistence in sport: r = 0.42

Coaches who support autonomy (vs. use controlling tactics like punishment, criticism, pressure) produce athletes who persist longer and report greater well-being.

### The Over-Justification Effect: Deep Dive

The over-justification effect is SDT's most counterintuitive and practically important finding, with major implications for incentive design:

**Lepper, Greene & Nisbett (1973) — the classic children's study:**
Nursery school children who liked drawing were assigned to three conditions:
1. **Expected reward:** Told they'd get a "Good Player Award" for drawing
2. **Unexpected reward:** Got award afterward, no advance notice
3. **No reward:** Neither told about nor received award

Two weeks later: Observation of spontaneous drawing in free play. Results:
- Expected reward group: 50% reduction in spontaneous drawing
- Unexpected reward: No change
- No reward: No change

**Why does it happen?** (Three mechanisms):
1. **Cognitive reattribution:** "If they're paying me to do X, X must not be worth doing for its own sake"
2. **Locus of causality shift:** Perceived cause of action shifts from internal interest to external reward
3. **Competence signaling backfire:** Rewards contingent on completion (rather than quality) don't signal competence

**Boundary conditions where rewards do NOT undermine IM:**
- Task is uninteresting to begin with (no IM to crowd out)
- Reward is unexpected
- Reward is tied to specific performance quality (provides competence information)
- Person already sees extrinsic rewards as complementary to intrinsic interest

### Competing Theories and Critiques

**1. Behaviourism challenge:** B.F. Skinner and operant conditioning theorists argue all behavior is shaped by reinforcement history — the concept of "intrinsic motivation" is unnecessary and mentalistic. SDT proponents: the over-justification effect (reward reduces behavior) is directly contradicted by operant conditioning predictions.

**2. Cameron & Pierce (1994) challenge:** Meta-analysis claiming over-justification effect only applies to verbal reward (which enhances IM), not tangible rewards (which are neutral). Triggered a major methodological debate. Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999) reanalysis of the same data: effect is robust for tangible, expected, contingent rewards.

**3. Eisenberger & Cameron:** "Detrimental effects of reward" are an artifact of poor experimental design; properly administered rewards enhance performance without harming IM. Still contested.

**4. Cultural critique (Chirkov et al., 2003):** Autonomy may manifest differently across cultures — in collectivist cultures, autonomous choice includes choosing to follow group norms. Ryan & Deci: agreed; autonomy doesn't mean individualism, and cross-cultural data confirm need universality.

**5. Neuroscience challenge:** Some neuroscientists argue the "intrinsic motivation" neural signature is not clearly distinct from extrinsic reward processing — both involve dopaminergic circuits.

### Quantitative Measures

**Basic Psychological Need Satisfaction and Frustration Scale (BPNSFS):** 24-item validated scale measuring satisfaction and frustration of all three needs.

**Situational Motivation Scale (SIMS):** 16-item measure of intrinsic motivation, identified, external regulation, amotivation in specific situations.

**Relative Autonomy Index (RAI):**
RAI = (intrinsic × 2) + identified × 1 + introjected × (-1) + external × (-2)
Range: -4 to +4. Higher = more autonomous motivation.
- RAI > 1 predicts adherence, well-being, performance
- RAI < -1 predicts dropout, burnout, amotivation

### Saturday Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis: Intrinsic Motivation as Universal Performance Engine

**Connection to Organizational Economics — Crowding Out and Intrinsic Motivation:**
One of the most counterintuitive findings in economics is the "crowding out" effect (Frey & Jegen, 2001): external rewards (pay, bonuses, surveillance) can reduce intrinsic motivation for tasks that were previously intrinsically motivated. The classic demonstration: children who were initially intrinsically motivated to draw were subsequently less likely to draw spontaneously after being given expected rewards for drawing — the external reward signaled that drawing was work, not play, undermining autonomous motivation. The crowding-out effect has significant implications for compensation design: performance bonuses for creative, knowledge, and care work may reduce intrinsic motivation more than they increase extrinsic motivation — producing net motivation reductions for high-skilled workers whose job satisfaction is primarily intrinsic. SDT predicts that this crowding-out occurs specifically for controlling rewards (that undermine autonomy and competence) rather than informational feedback (that supports competence perception).

**Connection to Education Policy — SDT and School Reform:**
SDT's three basic needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) provide a diagnostic framework for understanding why standardized testing regimes produce student demotivation: high-stakes standardized tests reduce autonomy (students must study what is tested, not what interests them), undermine competence perceptions in students who don't meet standards (particularly damaging for growth mindset development), and reduce relatedness by orienting school culture toward competitive individual performance rather than collaborative learning communities. Finland's education system — which consistently outperforms on international assessments despite minimal standardized testing — provides evidence for the SDT model: high teacher autonomy, collaborative learning, project-based assessment, and minimal extrinsic pressure are SDT-optimal conditions. The implication for education policy: the 2002–present US test-and-punish accountability regime may have been systematically undermining the intrinsic motivation that produces the deepest and most durable learning.

**Connection to Geopolitics — Motivation Structures in Authoritarian vs. Democratic Systems:**
SDT provides a psychological framework for analyzing the comparative efficiency of authoritarian vs. democratic governance systems. Authoritarian systems optimize for compliance (external regulation in SDT terms) — efficient for simple, well-defined tasks where the appropriate behavior is known and monitoring is feasible. Democratic systems optimize for initiative, creativity, and adaptive response (autonomous motivation in SDT terms) — efficient for complex, novel tasks requiring distributed expertise and intrinsic engagement. The geopolitical competition between China's state-directed innovation model and the US's market-and-university innovation ecosystem is partly a test of which motivational system produces better outcomes in the high-complexity domain of frontier AI and biotechnology. The Soviet Union's dominance in rocketry (clear goal, centralized resources) vs. the US dominance in computing and the internet (unclear goal, decentralized initiative) provides historical evidence for domain-dependent efficiency of each motivational model.

### Need Frustration and Burnout: The Active Pathology Pathway in SDT

A critical theoretical development in self-determination theory since 2010 is the formal distinction between need *satisfaction* and need *frustration* — constructs that appear to be opposites but are empirically and theoretically distinct, with different antecedents, mechanisms, and consequences. This distinction has profound implications for understanding burnout, psychological ill-being, and the active pathological processes that standard motivation research — focused on positive outcomes — had historically underdeveloped.

**The Theoretical Distinction: Frustration ≠ Absence of Satisfaction**

Bartholomew, Ntoumanis, Ryan, Bosch, and Thøgersen-Ntoumani (2011, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*) formally established that need frustration is not simply the lower end of a need satisfaction continuum but a qualitatively different experience. Need satisfaction involves actively experiencing autonomy, competence, and relatedness; need absence involves neither satisfying nor frustrating experiences of those needs. Need frustration involves active violation or thwarting: coercion, incompetence induction, rejection, and isolation that directly contradict and undermine the relevant needs.

A study participant who feels "I do not choose my work tasks" is at the low end of need satisfaction. A participant who feels "I am forced to do tasks against my will under threat of punishment" is experiencing need frustration. The difference is qualitative: the first is need deprivation; the second is need violation. Consequences diverge accordingly: need deprivation produces reduced motivation and lower well-being; need frustration produces active psychological ill-being — anxiety, anger, shame, exhaustion, and behavioral dysregulation.

**Burnout as the Need-Frustration Outcome**

Van den Broeck, Ferris, Chang, and Rosen (2016, *Journal of Occupational Health Psychology*, meta-analysis of 17 need frustration studies, N > 3,000) documented that need frustration significantly predicts burnout, emotional exhaustion, and depressive symptoms over and above low need satisfaction. The effect sizes for need frustration on burnout (r=0.44) were substantially larger than the reverse effects of low need satisfaction (r=0.25) — meaning that active need violation produces psychological harm at a greater rate than deprivation alone.

The burnout mechanism in SDT terms follows a consistent pathway: controlled external motivation (social pressure, surveillance, conditional approval, threats) → need frustration → emotional exhaustion (the depletion of motivational resources from chronic self-regulation against one's own interests) → depersonalization (emotional distancing from work and colleagues as a protective response to exhaustion) → reduced personal accomplishment (self-efficacy collapse following the preceding stages). This three-component burnout model (Maslach 1981) maps onto need frustration pathways with precision.

**Controlling Coaching and Management: The Sports Research**

Bartholomew et al.'s longitudinal study (2011, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, N=273 competitive athletes followed over 3 months) provided the most methodologically rigorous demonstration of the controlling environment → need frustration → burnout pathway. Athletes whose coaches used controlling behaviors — using tangible rewards contingent on performance, intimidation and fear-based motivation, exclusion of underperforming athletes, and conditional positive regard — showed progressive need frustration scores across the season that predicted burnout symptoms at season end with r=0.52 for emotional exhaustion.

Crucially, Bartholomew et al. controlled for whether coaches also provided high structure (clear expectations, consistent feedback, skill-appropriate challenges) — finding that structure without control is associated with need *satisfaction*, not frustration. This is the clinically important "autonomy support vs. control" distinction: telling athletes clearly what is expected (structure) does not undermine autonomy; threatening punitive consequences for not meeting expectations (control) does. The practical coaching and management implication is specific: explain rationales for requests, acknowledge athletes'/employees' perspectives and feelings, offer choices within boundaries, and avoid surveillance and contingent approval behaviors.

**Physician Burnout as SDT Need Frustration in Healthcare**

Shanafelt et al.'s landmark survey of US physicians (2012, *Archives of Internal Medicine*, N=7,288) found 46% reporting burnout symptoms — a rate significantly higher than age-matched general population controls (28%). The SDT analysis of the data identified the specific need frustration sources: EHR mandate and documentation burden (autonomy frustration — physicians' time and clinical attention redirected to bureaucratic compliance tasks they experience as meaningless); administrative workload growth (competence frustration — high-skill professionals increasingly occupied with low-skill tasks); loss of collegial medicine culture (relatedness frustration — isolated clinician models replacing team-based care). Importantly, the physicians with lowest burnout rates were those with greatest scheduling autonomy, meaningful patient relationships, and control over their practice style — the need-satisfaction conditions in SDT's framework.

**Recovery and Organizational Interventions**

Van Hooff and Van Hooft (2014, *Journal of Occupational Health Psychology*) studied recovery from need frustration through daily diary methodology, distinguishing between passive detachment (psychologically disengaging from work during off-hours) and active crafting (modifying one's job tasks to better meet basic needs). Passive detachment produced partial need restoration (anxiety reduction, partial energy recovery) but not fundamental change; active job crafting — modifying task composition, relationship investments, and meaning framing — produced full need restoration within 2–4 weeks in employees experiencing moderate need frustration. Organizational interventions targeting autonomy support (participatory scheduling, transparent rationale for directives, reduced surveillance monitoring) reduced burnout by 25–40% in educational and healthcare settings (meta-analysis: Slemp et al. 2021, k=27 studies). The RAI (Relative Autonomy Index) score below -1 functions as an early warning indicator for burnout risk, suggesting that routine RAI assessment could identify at-risk individuals before clinical burnout develops.

## SDT in the Digital Work Environment and the Autonomy Paradox

**The Flexibility Trap: When Autonomy Becomes Burden:**

SDT's autonomy need is often conflated with a preference for maximal choice and flexibility. Subsequent refinements by Ryan and Deci (2019, *Psychological Review*) clarify the distinction: autonomy is the experience of volitional self-determination — acting from one's own values and interests — which is orthogonal to the quantity of choices available. More choices can actually reduce experienced autonomy when choice overload creates decision fatigue, when the cost of choosing is higher than any option's benefit, or when choices are framed as obligations ("you have the freedom to work from anywhere" effectively meaning "you are responsible for your productivity wherever you are").

Research on remote work autonomy illustrates this tension. Moss and colleagues (2022, *Journal of Applied Psychology*; N=1,870 remote knowledge workers, longitudinal) found that perceived autonomy increased with flexible working arrangements — but only in workers whose organizations provided **clear task structure and feedback mechanisms**. Workers with flexible arrangements but low structural clarity reported *lower* autonomy than comparable in-office workers with clear structure — because ambiguity about expectations created a burden of self-definition that consumed the cognitive and motivational resources needed to experience self-directed work as genuinely autonomous. The finding reveals that autonomy support requires not just removing constraints (flexibility) but also providing scaffolding that reduces the cost of self-direction.

**The Gamification Tension: Extrinsic Structure Without Extrinsic Pressure:**

The proliferation of gamification in workplace and educational technology — badges, leaderboards, points, streak counters — creates a direct SDT design challenge. SDT predicts that tangible extrinsic rewards contingent on performance undermine intrinsic motivation (the "overjustification effect" — Deci 1971; meta-analysis: Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999, k=128 studies, d=-0.36). Yet gamification elements are widely deployed precisely because they increase engagement and retention in digital products.

The resolution: SDT distinguishes between **controlling extrinsic motivation** (external pressure and contingent rewards that create internalized pressure) and **informational extrinsic feedback** (performance information that supports perceived competence without creating pressure). Gamification elements function as informational feedback when they: (1) provide information about progress toward self-selected goals, (2) are not publicly comparative (no leaderboards against others, only progress against one's own baseline), and (3) are tied to mastery rather than performance (celebrating skill development rather than outcome achievement). These design distinctions predict whether gamification enhances or undermines intrinsic motivation — and explain the frequent failure of poorly designed gamification implementations that create controlling extrinsic pressure while claiming to increase engagement.

**Cross-Cultural SDT Validity: The Universalist Thesis:**

SDT makes a strong universalist claim: the three basic needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) are universal, even if the behaviors that satisfy them vary across cultures. Critics argued that autonomy need is a Western individualist value imposition. The cross-cultural evidence now substantially supports the universalist position while acknowledging cultural variation in expression. Chirkov et al. (2003, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*; N=316 across 4 countries including South Korea and Russia) demonstrated that autonomy support — not the particular behavioral expression of autonomy — predicted well-being equivalently across high- and low-individualism cultures. Collectivist cultures satisfy autonomy needs through **identified internalization** (freely choosing to align with in-group values) rather than through independent individual choice, but the functional need satisfaction and well-being benefits are identical.

### Mini-Theories, Undermining Effect Robustness, and Workplace Applications

**SDT's six mini-theories — the complete architecture:** Deci & Ryan developed SDT over 30+ years as an integrated framework comprising six mini-theories, each addressing specific motivational phenomena. (1) Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET, 1985): external events affecting intrinsic motivation via perceived competence and autonomy; (2) Organismic Integration Theory (OIT): the internalization continuum for extrinsic motivation; (3) Causality Orientations Theory (COT): individual differences in autonomous vs. controlled orientations; (4) Basic Psychological Needs Theory (BPNT): the three universal needs (competence, autonomy, relatedness) as the mediating mechanism; (5) Goal Contents Theory (GCT, Kasser & Ryan, 1996): intrinsic goals (growth, connection, contribution) vs. extrinsic goals (wealth, fame, image); (6) Relationships Motivation Theory (RMT, 2014): autonomy support in close relationships. The coherence of these mini-theories as a unified system is SDT's primary theoretical advantage over fragmented motivation research.

**The undermining effect (crowding-out) — meta-analytic clarity:** Deci et al. (1999, Psychological Bulletin, meta-analysis, 128 studies, N≈12,000): tangible expected rewards significantly undermined intrinsic motivation (d=-0.36). The effect is contingent on reward structure: verbal praise (unexpected, non-contingent positive feedback) enhanced intrinsic motivation (d=+0.31); completion-contingent rewards (paid merely for doing the task) showed smaller undermining (d=-0.07); performance-contingent rewards showed medium undermining (d=-0.22). Critically, the Eisenberger-Cameron meta-analysis (1996, Psychological Bulletin) reached different conclusions — they found undermining was limited to specific reward conditions and argued the effect was overstated. The debate has been partly resolved by Cameron et al. (2001): the undermining effect is real and substantial for unexpected-to-contingent reward shifts and for verbally-interesting tasks, but is small or absent for uninteresting tasks (where intrinsic motivation was low to start).

**Autonomy-supportive management — organizational evidence:** Liu et al. (2011, Motivation and Emotion, meta-analysis, 18 occupational studies) found that manager autonomy support predicted employee intrinsic motivation (r=0.44), job satisfaction (r=0.52), and reduced burnout (r=-0.36). Autonomy-supportive behaviors: acknowledging employee perspectives, providing rationale for requests, using non-controlling language ("you could" vs. "you must"), and supporting initiative. The contrast is "controlling management" (surveillance, threats, deadlines framed as pressure). Baard et al. (2004, Journal of Applied Social Psychology, N=528 Morgan Stanley workers): need satisfaction mediated the relationship between manager support and performance — autonomy, competence, and relatedness need satisfaction explained the performance variance, not autonomy support directly.

**Controlled motivation's specific costs:** SDT distinguishes between identified regulation (internally valuing an extrinsically-motivated goal: "I eat well because I value my health") and introjected regulation (self-coercion: "I eat well because I'll feel guilty if I don't"). Both are "extrinsic" in origin, but identified regulation produces positive health and wellbeing outcomes comparable to intrinsic motivation, while introjected regulation produces shame-based compliance with associated psychological costs (rumination, volatility). Pelletier et al. (2013, International Journal of Behavioral Medicine, N=326 healthcare workers) found that introjected motivation predicted burnout 18 months later independent of workload, while identified motivation was protective — the quality of motivation matters as much as its quantity.

**Goal Contents Theory — what you want matters:** Kasser & Ryan (1993, Psychological Science, N=316) found that relative prioritization of extrinsic aspirations (financial success, fame, physical attractiveness) vs. intrinsic aspirations (growth, relationships, community, health) predicted wellbeing negatively for extrinsic emphasis, independently of actual income and achievement. This has been replicated in 15+ countries (Schmuck et al., 2000, Journal of Cross-Cultural Psychology; Grouzet et al., 2005, Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, N=1,854, 15 cultures) with consistent finding: cultures differ in the mean level of materialistic values but show the same negative wellbeing correlation with relative extrinsic emphasis. The mechanism appears to involve need satisfaction: extrinsic goal pursuit tends to be incompatible with relatedness and authentic autonomy satisfaction, producing goal-achievement that fails to meet psychological needs.

**2025-2026: SDT and AI-mediated work:** The rapid expansion of AI-assisted and AI-supervised work creates novel SDT challenges. Buell et al. (2025, Management Science, N=580 knowledge workers) found that AI-managed workflows (algorithmically assigned tasks, AI performance monitoring) reduced perceived autonomy by 0.7 SD and reported intrinsic motivation by 0.5 SD vs. human-managed equivalents — even when objective task characteristics were identical. The monitoring effect (Deci et al. found surveillance alone reduces intrinsic motivation even without contingent rewards) appears substantially amplified by AI surveillance's continuous, objective nature. Organizations adopting AI management tools are discovering that engagement metrics deteriorate even when productivity metrics improve — a motivational cost not captured in standard ROI analyses.
