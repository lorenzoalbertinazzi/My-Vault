## Summary
Moral psychology studies how humans form moral judgments, construct ethical reasoning, and navigate the gap between moral beliefs and moral behavior. The field has been transformed by Jonathan Haidt's Social Intuitionist Model (2001), which overturned the rationalist consensus by demonstrating that most moral judgments are rapid, intuitive responses that reasoning post-hoc justifies rather than generates. The neuroscience of morality, cross-cultural moral foundations research, and the documented failures of moral reasoning under social pressure together produce a picture of human moral psychology that is simultaneously more complex, more culturally variable, and more susceptible to environmental manipulation than Enlightenment rationalism assumed.

## Key Points
- **Haidt's Social Intuitionist Model:** Moral judgments are primarily driven by rapid intuition; reasoning is mostly post-hoc rationalization ("the moral dog and its rational tail")
- **Moral Foundations Theory:** Six universal foundations (Care, Fairness, Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity, Liberty) vary in emphasis across cultures and political ideologies
- **Trolley Problem splits:** Personal violence activates emotional brain circuits; impersonal harm activates deliberative circuits — demonstrating that moral judgment is not unitary
- **Moral disengagement** (Bandura): Mechanisms by which ordinary people commit atrocities while maintaining positive self-concept — the psychology of the "banality of evil"
- **Moral licensing:** Doing good deed A creates psychological "credit" that makes doing bad deed B more likely — a paradoxical effect that undermines cumulative ethical behavior
- **Obedience to authority** is the dominant moral psychology finding for group violence: Milgram's shock studies showed 65% compliance with lethal harm orders under legitimate authority

## Details

### The Rationalist Overthrow: From Kohlberg to Haidt

Lawrence Kohlberg's stage model of moral development (1969) was built on the rationalist premise: moral development is progression through increasingly sophisticated stages of logical moral reasoning, from punishment-avoidance through social contract to universal principles. Kohlberg's Heinz dilemma (should Heinz steal a drug to save his wife?) tested this by categorizing responses into developmental stages.

Jonathan Haidt's "The Emotional Dog and Its Rational Tail" (Psychological Review, 2001) demolished the Kohlbergian consensus with systematic evidence: when people encountered "harmless taboo violations" (consensual adult incest between siblings who use contraception, a woman who uses her deceased father's suit as a cleaning rag) that produced strong moral condemnation despite being logically impossible to justify as harmful, they engaged in "moral dumbfounding" — searching for reasons to justify their condemnation, failing to find them, but refusing to update their moral judgment. The conclusion: moral intuitions come first; moral reasoning follows as rationalization.

### Moral Foundations Theory

Haidt and Joseph (2004), and subsequently Graham, Haidt & Nosek (2009), developed Moral Foundations Theory (MFT) by combining evolutionary psychology, cultural anthropology, and political psychology. They identified six universal moral foundations evolved to address adaptive challenges:

**Care/Harm:** Protection of vulnerable individuals; evolved for child nurturance. Activation: suffering, neediness. Key virtue: compassion. Most emphasized by political liberals.

**Fairness/Cheating:** Reciprocal exchange and cheating detection; evolved for cooperation. Activation: cheating, exploitation. Key virtue: justice. Emphasized across political spectrums but framed differently (equality by liberals, proportionality by conservatives).

**Loyalty/Betrayal:** Group cohesion and protection from traitors; evolved for tribal coalitional psychology. Activation: betrayal, disloyalty. Key virtue: patriotism. More emphasized by political conservatives.

**Authority/Subversion:** Deference to legitimate hierarchies; evolved for navigating hierarchical social structures. Activation: disrespect, subversion. Key virtue: obedience. More emphasized by conservatives.

**Sanctity/Degradation:** Protection against pathogens and moral pollution; evolved from behavioral immune system. Activation: disgusting or degrading acts. Key virtue: purity. More emphasized by conservatives and religious individuals.

**Liberty/Oppression:** (Added in 2012 revision) Resentment of illegitimate dominance. Activation: bullying, tyranny. Key virtue: freedom. Emphasized by libertarians and anti-authoritarian populations.

**Political implications:** Haidt's finding that conservatives use all six foundations while liberals primarily use Care and Fairness explains why political communication across ideological lines often fails — conservatives experience liberal appeals as addressing only 2/6 of the moral matrix, while their own rhetoric activates 6/6.

### Neuroscience of Moral Judgment: The Trolley Problem Decoded

Joshua Greene and colleagues (Science, 2001) used fMRI to investigate the neural basis of the famous trolley problem split. In the footbridge version (push a large man off a bridge to stop a trolley from killing five), most people refuse to push despite judging the abstract utilitarian calculation as equivalent to the switch version (pull a switch to divert the trolley, killing one to save five).

Greene's finding: the footbridge version (personal, direct violence) activates the medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate, and amygdala — emotion regulation circuits. The switch version activates dorsolateral prefrontal cortex — deliberative reasoning. The footbridge version triggers an emotional "don't touch" response that is evolutionarily ancient (direct violence has different fitness consequences than indirect harm). The deliberative system can override this response, but doing so requires effort — and people who more quickly judge the footbridge case as permissible show stronger dorsolateral prefrontal activation.

The dual-process model of moral judgment: System 1 (fast, automatic, emotional) delivers intuitive moral verdicts; System 2 (slow, deliberate, cognitive) can override them when given sufficient motivation and capacity. Most everyday moral judgments are System 1 products; moral philosophy is the attempt to construct System 2 frameworks that can systematically evaluate and sometimes correct System 1 responses.

### Moral Disengagement: Bandura's Framework

Albert Bandura's "Selective Activation and Disengagement of Moral Competence" (1990) explains how ordinary people commit atrocities. The mechanisms of moral disengagement:

**Moral justification:** Reframing harmful behaviors as serving moral purposes ("just war," "necessary measures")  
**Euphemistic labeling:** Linguistic distancing from moral content ("collateral damage," "enhanced interrogation," "ethnic cleansing")  
**Advantageous comparison:** Making harmful acts seem better by comparison to more extreme harms  
**Displacement of responsibility:** Attributing responsibility to higher authorities ("I was following orders")  
**Diffusion of responsibility:** Spreading responsibility so no individual feels fully accountable (bystander effect)  
**Dehumanization:** Stripping victims of human attributes, making harm easier to inflict  
**Attribution of blame:** Portraying victims as bringing harm upon themselves  

Hannah Arendt's "banality of evil" (Eichmann in Jerusalem, 1963) describes exactly this: Adolf Eichmann was not a monster but an ordinary bureaucrat who used displacement of responsibility and euphemistic labeling to administer genocide while maintaining positive self-concept. The disturbing implication: atrocity does not require evil character; it requires moral disengagement mechanisms that ordinary people readily employ under the right organizational conditions.

### Moral Licensing: The Paradox of Virtue

Moral licensing (Monin & Miller, 2001) is the counterintuitive finding that doing good creates psychological "credits" that make subsequent bad behavior more likely. In experiments, participants who recalled doing a virtuous act subsequently made less ethical choices — as if virtue had been "spent." Examples: people who buy an organic product subsequently choose less healthy food; people who donate to charity subsequently cut in line; executives who make a public ESG commitment subsequently are more likely to make aggressive accounting choices.

The mechanism: moral licensing activates a psychological credit account, reducing the need for virtue maintenance. The self-licensing that follows a virtuous act ("I can afford this, I've been good") depletes the motivation for continued ethical behavior. For organizational ethics, the implication is concerning: corporate social responsibility programs that create strong moral licensing effects may reduce unethical behavior in the licensed domains while inadvertently increasing it in unlicensed domains — potentially netting to zero or negative.

### Milgram and the Obedience Problem

Stanley Milgram's obedience studies (1963) remain the most disturbing findings in social psychology. 65% of participants administered apparently lethal electric shocks to screaming victims when ordered to do so by an authority figure in a white lab coat. Replication studies (Burger, 2009; virtual reality versions, Slater et al., 2006) consistently confirm the finding across cultures, genders, and eras — with context (physical distance from the victim, presence of the authority figure, peer behavior) modulating but not eliminating the effect.

The moral psychology insight: Milgram's participants were not sadists — they showed signs of extreme distress throughout. They were demonstrating the moral psychology of authority deference: the evolved tendency to defer to legitimate authority figures in hierarchical contexts overrides both personal moral values and even rational self-preservation concerns (participants were not being threatened). This is not a failure of reasoning; it is the activation of a deeply evolved social cognition module that ordinarily serves prosocial functions (respecting expertise, maintaining social order) but catastrophically misfires when authorities instruct harm.

### Cross-Disciplinary Connections

**To Finance:** Corporate governance failures (Enron, WorldCom, 2008 banking crisis) are moral disengagement case studies. Executives used euphemistic labeling ("aggressive accounting"), displacement of responsibility ("following market convention"), and dehumanization of victims ("sophisticated investors can handle the risk") to systematically mislead while maintaining moral self-concept. The compliance culture in financial institutions — emphasizing legal permissibility over ethical judgment — creates organizational conditions for moral disengagement at scale. See [[Finance/behavioral-finance-and-investor-psychology]].

**To Technology:** AI systems are designed by teams using moral frameworks — and moral disengagement mechanisms operate in technology development as readily as in other domains. Algorithmic harm is euphemistically labeled ("serving relevant content"), responsibility is displaced ("the algorithm optimizes for engagement, not outcomes"), and victims are dehumanized (reduced to data points). Understanding moral psychology is essential for building ethical AI teams and processes. See [[Tech & AI/ai-safety-and-alignment]].

**To Geopolitics:** Moral Foundations Theory explains why political communication across ideological lines fails in international diplomacy. Nations with different dominant moral foundations (Care/Fairness vs. Loyalty/Authority/Sanctity) literally speak different moral languages. The US-Iran conflict (see [[Geopolitics/2026-05-28-usa-iran-conflict-2026-war-and-nuclear-negotiations]]) is partly a moral foundations clash: US framing (universal rights, individual liberty) activates Care/Fairness/Liberty foundations that are dominant in American liberal moral culture; Iranian framing (national honor, religious sanctity, resistance to domination) activates Authority/Sanctity/Loyalty foundations that are dominant in Iranian conservative moral culture. Neither side is being irrational; they are applying different moral foundations to the same situation.
