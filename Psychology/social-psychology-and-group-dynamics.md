## Summary
Social psychology is the scientific study of how individuals' thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are influenced by the real, imagined, or implied presence of others. Its central finding — that situations powerfully shape behavior, often more than individual character — has overturned centuries of folk wisdom about virtue and vice. From Milgram's obedience experiments to Asch's conformity studies to Tajfel's social identity theory, social psychology illuminates the mechanisms behind authority compliance, group cohesion, prejudice, deindividuation, and collective decision-making failures that shape everything from organizational behavior to political movements.

## Key Points
- **Fundamental attribution error (FAE)**: humans systematically overestimate dispositional causes (character, personality) and underestimate situational causes of behavior — the core bias social psychology corrects
- **Conformity**: people conform to group norms even when the group is demonstrably wrong (Asch); informational influence (seeking truth) and normative influence (seeking acceptance) are distinct mechanisms
- **Obedience to authority**: Milgram showed that ~65% of ordinary people would administer potentially lethal shocks to strangers when instructed by an authority figure in a lab coat
- **Groupthink**: highly cohesive groups suppress internal dissent and external information, leading to systematic decision-making failures (Bay of Pigs, Challenger disaster, 2008 subprime mortgage crisis)
- **Social identity theory**: people derive self-esteem from group membership, leading to in-group favoritism and out-group discrimination even in arbitrary groups ("minimal group paradigm")

## Details

### The Situationist Revolution: Lewin, Milgram, Zimbardo
The founding insight of modern social psychology is Kurt Lewin's field theory (1935): **B = f(P, E)** — behavior (B) is a function of person (P) and environment (E). The environment often dominates.

**Stanley Milgram's Obedience Studies (1961–62)**: Participants were told they were helping with a "learning experiment" and must administer electric shocks (15V–450V in 30 steps) to a "learner" (actually a confederate) for wrong answers. An authority figure in a grey lab coat instructed participants to continue.

Results: **65% of participants delivered the maximum 450V** — marked "XXX / DANGER: Severe Shock" — despite apparent screams of pain and eventually silence. Key variables:
- When authority figure left the room: compliance fell to 20%
- When victim was in same room: compliance fell to 40%
- When a peer rebel (confederate) refused to continue: compliance fell to 10%
- When authority figure was at a distance via phone: compliance fell to ~20%

Interpretation: obedience isn't about character but about the **agentic state** — when people perceive themselves as instruments of legitimate authority, they transfer moral responsibility upward. Real-world relevance: Nazi war crime perpetrators (Hannah Arendt's "banality of evil"), corporate misconduct (WorldCom, Enron accountants), military atrocities (My Lai massacre).

**Philip Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment (1971)**: Students randomly assigned to "guard" and "prisoner" roles in a simulated prison developed authentic role-congruent behaviors within 36 hours. Guards became abusive; prisoners became passive and depressed. Zimbardo called it off after 6 days (planned: 2 weeks). Later critiques revealed methodological flaws (guards were coached to be tough), but the core finding — role assignment shapes identity and behavior — remains influential.

### Conformity: Asch's Lines
Solomon Asch (1951) had subjects identify which of three comparison lines matched a standard line — an objectively simple task. But 7 confederates in the room would unanimously give an obviously wrong answer.

**Results**: 75% of subjects conformed to the wrong answer at least once; 37% of answers overall were conforming errors. Control condition (no confederates): <1% errors.

**Mechanisms of conformity:**
1. **Informational social influence**: uncertainty about reality → use others as information source → susceptible to confident, unanimous majorities
2. **Normative social influence**: desire for acceptance/approval → suppress private judgment → susceptible when group membership matters

**Moderators**:
- **Unanimity**: one dissenter (even if wrong) reduces conformity to ~5–8% — the critical importance of legitimate minority voices
- **Group size**: conformity rises from 1 to ~4 people, then plateaus
- **Commitment**: writing down answers privately before group discussion dramatically reduces conformity
- **Culture**: collectivist cultures show somewhat higher conformity (meta-analyses: r ≈ .26 correlation); effect smaller than often assumed

**Applications**: investment committees (groupthink in asset allocation), medical diagnosis error in teaching hospitals (where junior doctors defer to seniors even when they are wrong), legal juries (Kalven & Zeisel found first-ballot majority determined final verdict 97% of the time).

### Groupthink: Irving Janis's Framework
Irving Janis (1972) coined "groupthink" after analyzing catastrophic policy failures: the Bay of Pigs invasion (1961), Pearl Harbor intelligence failures (1941), the Challenger shuttle disaster (1986), and the 2003 Iraq WMD intelligence failure.

**Eight symptoms of groupthink:**
1. Illusion of invulnerability — excessive optimism, risk-taking
2. Collective rationalization — discounting warning signals
3. Belief in group's inherent morality — ignoring ethical implications
4. Stereotyped views of out-groups — enemy is evil, weak, or stupid
5. Pressure on dissenters — direct pressure on those who question assumptions
6. Self-censorship — members withhold doubts
7. Illusion of unanimity — silence = consent
8. Self-appointed mindguards — members protect group from contrary information

**Case study — Bay of Pigs (1961)**: Kennedy's cabinet, a highly cohesive group of elite "best and brightest," failed to critically examine the CIA's invasion plan. Dissenters (Arthur Schlesinger, Dean Rusk) self-censored. The plan assumed the Cuban population would rise up — never tested. Outcome: catastrophic failure, 1,200 prisoners, $53M ransom.

**Prescriptions against groupthink:**
- Devil's advocate roles: designated challenger
- Anonymous pre-meeting surveys
- Subgroup analysis with separate deliberation
- Outside experts with explicit disagreement mandate
- Leader refrains from stating preference early in discussion

**Important caveat**: Later researchers (Baron, 2005; Esser, 1998) found mixed empirical support for Janis's full model. "Groupthink" as a precise psychological construct is better understood as a cluster of related phenomena (conformity pressure, information sharing failures, escalation of commitment) rather than a single syndrome.

### Social Identity Theory: Tajfel and Turner
Henri Tajfel's **Minimal Group Paradigm (1970)**: Subjects were divided into groups based on trivial criteria (preference for Klee vs. Kandinsky paintings, or even a coin flip). With no history, interaction, or competition, subjects still allocated more resources to in-group members, even when it cost them absolutely more to maximize in-group advantage over out-group (in-group vs. out-group).

**Social Identity Theory (SIT)**: People derive self-esteem from group memberships ("social identity"). Positive social identity requires favorable comparisons between in-group and out-group ("social comparison"). When social identity is threatened, people:
1. Leave the group (individual mobility) — if permeable
2. Redefine comparison dimensions ("we may be poor but we're happier")
3. Compete more fiercely for status distinctions

**Self-Categorization Theory (Turner, 1987)**: extends SIT — the level of self-categorization (personal identity vs. group identity vs. superordinate human identity) depersonalizes perception, making behavior more stereotype-consistent. Explains why the same person behaves differently as an individual vs. a fan in a crowd vs. a human responding to humanitarian crisis.

**Realistic Group Conflict theory (Sherif, Robbers Cave, 1954)**: Intergroup hostility arises from actual competition for scarce resources. Boys' camp study: two groups in competition became hostile; introduction of **superordinate goals** (crises requiring both groups' cooperation) reduced hostility. Implication: intergroup conflict requires structural changes (shared goals), not merely contact.

**Contact Hypothesis (Allport, 1954)**: Intergroup contact reduces prejudice under conditions of: equal status, cooperative interdependence, institutional support, and personal acquaintance potential. Meta-analysis (Pettigrew & Tropp, 2006, k=515 studies): r = −.21 between contact and prejudice. But context matters enormously; hostile or unequal contact can exacerbate prejudice.

### Deindividuation, Bystander Effect, and Diffusion of Responsibility
**Bystander Effect (Darley & Latané, 1968)**: Kitty Genovese's 1964 murder in NYC (reportedly with 38 witnesses who did nothing) inspired research. In experiments, participants were less likely to respond to an emergency as group size increased: alone = 85% helped; with 4 bystanders = 31%.

Mechanisms:
1. **Diffusion of responsibility**: "Someone else will help"
2. **Pluralistic ignorance**: observing others' inaction as signal that no emergency exists
3. **Evaluation apprehension**: fear of appearing overreacting in front of others

Countermeasures: **singularization** — making one person specifically responsible by name or direct eye contact dramatically increases helping.

**Deindividuation**: Loss of individual identity in group contexts leads to reduced self-monitoring, increased impulsive behavior, and weakened norms. Halloween study (Diener, 1976): costumed children in groups stole more candy than when alone or uncostumed. Online context: anonymity in social media deindividuates users, increasing aggression, harassment, and normative violations.

**Social loafing (Ringelmann effect, 1913)**: In group tasks, individual effort declines as group size grows. Rope-pulling study: 8-person groups pulled with only 49% of their combined individual capacity. Mechanism: reduced identifiability of individual contribution. Countermeasure: individual accountability systems.

### Persuasion and Social Influence Architecture
Robert Cialdini's six principles of influence (see [[cialdini-influence]]) operate through the social mechanisms identified in social psychology:
- **Social proof** exploits informational social influence (others' behavior as signal)
- **Authority** activates the Milgram agentic state
- **Liking** leverages in-group favoritism from social identity
- **Scarcity** operates through loss aversion and reactance theory (Brehm, 1966)

**Elaboration Likelihood Model (Petty & Cacioppo, 1986)**: Persuasion occurs via two routes:
- **Central route**: careful reasoning about argument quality → durable attitude change; requires motivation and capacity
- **Peripheral route**: heuristic cues (attractiveness, consensus, authority) → temporary attitude change; operates when motivation/capacity is low

Political advertising, marketing, and social media all preferentially target the peripheral route — message volume and emotional valence rather than content quality.

### Group Decision-Making: Polarization and Wisdom of Crowds
**Group polarization (Stoner, 1961; Moscovici & Zavalloni, 1969)**: Group discussions push members toward more extreme positions on initial attitudes — if members lean risk-seeking, discussion makes them more so; if risk-averse, more so. Mechanisms:
1. Persuasive arguments: discussion surfaces more arguments consistent with initial majority view
2. Social comparison: members compete to appear "more extreme" on salient group values

**Wisdom of Crowds (Galton, 1907; Surowiecki, 2004)**: Aggregating independent, diverse judgments reliably outperforms individual experts. Conditions: independence (no herding), diversity, decentralization, aggregation mechanism. Breakdown conditions: correlation of errors, herding, shared information environments.

**Jury decision-making**: Kalven & Zeisel's "The American Jury" (1966) found that the first-ballot majority won in 97% of cases. Deliberation rarely changes minds; it mostly allows the majority to pressure minority dissenters. Studies of jury composition show racial and gender representation affects verdict outcomes.

### Prejudice, Stereotyping, and Modern Discrimination
**Stereotype Threat (Steele & Aronson, 1995)**: Making group membership salient before performance tests causes members of stigmatized groups to underperform relative to their ability. African American students performing below white counterparts on verbal SAT-like tests — but only when the task was framed as measuring "intellectual ability." When framed as "problem solving," gap disappears. Mechanism: anxiety about confirming negative stereotype consumes working memory.

**Implicit Association Test (Greenwald, McGhee & Schwartz, 1998)**: Measures millisecond-level associations between concepts; most US white participants show implicit preference for white over black faces even when explicit attitudes are egalitarian. Debate: whether IAT predicts actual discriminatory behavior (correlation ~r=.25 in meta-analyses; effect size is real but modest).

**Aversive racism (Gaertner & Dovidio, 1986)**: Modern racial bias in liberal societies operates covertly — racial bias emerges in ambiguous situations (hiring decisions where qualifications are mixed) but not when discrimination would be obvious. Implication: bias exists despite conscious commitment to equality; structural solutions (blind auditions, standardized criteria) outperform awareness-raising alone.
