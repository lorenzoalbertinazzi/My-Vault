## Summary
Robert Cialdini's *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984) identified six universal principles through which people are reliably persuaded. These are not manipulation tricks — they are cognitive shortcuts (heuristics) the human brain uses to make decisions efficiently. Understanding them lets you recognize when they're being used on you and deploy them ethically yourself.

## Key Points
- The 6 principles: **Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity**
- Each exploits a mental shortcut that is usually adaptive but can be triggered inappropriately
- Awareness is the primary defense — recognition breaks the automatic compliance
- Cialdini later added a 7th: **Unity** (shared identity)
- These principles work below conscious awareness — that's what makes them powerful

## Details

### 1. Reciprocity
**Principle**: People feel obligated to return what they've received.

When someone gives us something — a gift, a favor, information — we feel an almost involuntary urge to give back. This drive is so strong that uninvited gifts create obligation.

**In practice**:
- Free samples at grocery stores generate disproportionate sales
- Charities that send small gifts with donation requests receive higher response rates
- In negotiation: make a concession → pressure the other party to reciprocate

**Defense**: Recognize the trigger. If a gift feels strategic, mentally reclassify it as a sales tactic and feel free to decline without guilt.

---

### 2. Commitment and Consistency
**Principle**: Once we commit to something (especially publicly), we feel compelled to behave consistently with that commitment.

We have a deep psychological need to appear consistent with our prior statements and actions. Small initial commitments expand into larger ones (the **foot-in-the-door** technique).

**In practice**:
- Car dealers get you to say "yes" to a great price, then add features after you're committed
- Political campaigns get small signatures → escalate to donations → volunteering
- Writing down goals dramatically increases follow-through (public commitment)

**Key insight**: Written commitments are stronger than verbal ones. Public commitments are stronger than private ones. Active choices bind more than passive ones.

**Defense**: Ask yourself "Would I make this same choice today if I weren't already committed?" Prior commitment is not a valid reason on its own.

---

### 3. Social Proof
**Principle**: When uncertain, we look at what others are doing and treat it as evidence of the correct behavior.

The more people we see doing something, the more correct it seems. Works especially in ambiguous situations and when the "others" are similar to us.

**In practice**:
- Laugh tracks make jokes seem funnier
- "Best seller" labels boost sales (if everyone bought it, it must be good)
- Bystander effect: the more witnesses to an emergency, the less likely any individual acts

**Pluralistic ignorance**: A crowd where everyone privately doubts but publicly acts certain — everyone assumes the others know something they don't.

**Defense**: Make your own judgment. In emergencies, choose one specific person and address them directly — breaks the diffusion of responsibility.

---

### 4. Authority
**Principle**: We defer to credible, legitimate-seeming experts.

We're trained from birth to defer to authority figures (parents, teachers, doctors, officials). This is usually adaptive — experts do know more. But the *trappings* of authority trigger the same response as genuine expertise.

**In practice**:
- Doctors' recommendations are followed even when they seem questionable
- Titles, uniforms, and expensive accessories signal authority
- "Studies show..." triggers automatic credence even without knowing the study

**Key insight**: Symbols of authority (expensive clothes, impressive titles) work just as well as real authority on quick judgments.

**Defense**: Ask two questions: Is this person genuinely an expert in *this specific area*? Do they have interests that conflict with mine?

---

### 5. Liking
**Principle**: We comply more readily with people we like.

We buy from people we like, vote for attractive candidates, and are persuaded by friends. Liking is generated by: physical attractiveness, similarity, familiarity, compliments, and association with good things.

**In practice**:
- Tupperware parties leverage friendship networks (you don't want to let a friend down)
- "Halo effect": attractive people are assumed to be more competent and trustworthy
- Advertisers pair products with attractive/likable people

**Key insight**: We extend positive feelings toward the things associated with people we like. This is why celebrity endorsements work even when celebrities know nothing about the product.

**Defense**: Consciously separate your feelings about the person from the merits of what they're proposing.

---

### 6. Scarcity
**Principle**: Opportunities seem more valuable when their availability is limited.

We want more of what we can have less of. Loss aversion makes potential losses more motivating than equivalent gains. "Limited time offer" and "only 3 left" trigger urgency.

**In practice**:
- Flash sales and countdown timers
- "Exclusive" memberships and waitlists
- Censorship makes information more desirable (the "Romeo and Juliet effect")

**Psychological Reactance**: When freedom to have something is threatened, we desire it more intensely as a way to reassert freedom.

**Defense**: Ask "Do I want this because it's genuinely good, or because I might not be able to have it?" Scarcity says nothing about quality.

---

### 7. Unity (Added in *Pre-Suasion*, 2016)
**Principle**: Shared identity creates the strongest basis for influence.

Different from liking — not "I like you" but "you are one of us." Family, ethnicity, political tribe, nationality. We automatically prefer and comply with in-group members.

**In practice**:
- "We're both [ethnicity/hometown/profession]" dramatically increases compliance
- Co-creation and collaboration ("I need your advice/input") makes people feel ownership and commitment

### Using These Principles Ethically
Cialdini distinguishes **persuasion** (creating the conditions for someone to see the real merits of your case) from **manipulation** (using triggers to get compliance that doesn't serve the person's genuine interests). The principles work in both directions — use them to communicate truth more effectively, not to deceive.

### Pre-Suasion: The Channel Before the Message

In *Pre-Suasion* (2016), Cialdini introduced a concept that extends beyond the six principles: **what you direct attention to immediately before making a request determines what mental framework people use to evaluate it**.

Before asking for a large favor, a skilled communicator arranges the environment so that the concept of generosity or collaboration is already primed. Examples:
- A website with fluffy clouds (vs. pennies) in the background led visitors to prioritize comfort (vs. price) in product choices — they weren't told to; the background primed the relevant concept
- Asking someone "Do you consider yourself adventurous?" before presenting a new product makes them more likely to try it — they've just confirmed an identity that supports the behavior
- Playing French music in a wine store increases French wine sales vs. German music; customers report the music had no effect — but their choices say otherwise

**The unity principle in practice**: "I need your advice on something..." converts an influencer into a collaborator — co-creation invokes ownership and commitment that dramatically increases follow-through versus simply asking for compliance.

### The Door-in-the-Face Technique (vs. Foot-in-the-Door)

These are opposing applications of Reciprocity and Commitment respectively:

**Foot-in-the-Door** (Commitment): Start with a small request → comply → larger request. Works because people stay consistent with their prior self-image as someone who "does this kind of thing."

**Door-in-the-Face** (Reciprocity): Start with an unreasonably large request (guaranteed rejection) → follow with a smaller, real request. Works because the concession (from big to small) creates reciprocal pressure — the prospect feels they must give something back by accepting the smaller request.

When to use which:
- FITD works best when the requests share the same category and are separated by time
- DITF works best for immediate, in-person requests where the concession is visible

### That's-Not-All Technique

A retail variant of reciprocity: quote a price → before the prospect responds, add an extra feature or discount ("And that's not all — I'll throw in X"). The brain interprets this as a concession even though the offer was never finalized. Research shows it consistently outperforms simply offering the combined package upfront, even when the total value is identical.

### Cialdini's Ethical Framework

Cialdini draws a sharp distinction that many practitioners miss:

**Legitimate persuasion**: Surface existing authentic evidence that genuinely matches your case — help people see the true value that is already there. The six principles then act as channels through which genuine worth flows.

**Manipulation**: Manufacturing evidence, creating false scarcity, faking social proof — triggering the principles with false signals.

The practical and ethical case for legitimate use: manipulated compliance generates resentment, returns, complaints, and damaged relationships when discovered. Genuine persuasion creates satisfied customers who become advocates.
